\begin{abstract}
We provide the algorithmic and computational complement to the theoretical framework of~\cite{Piel.2026} for hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces on triangular meshes. The central observation is that every basis function is represented by a column of the accumulated subdivision matrix. Selecting active degrees of freedom results in collecting their corresponding columns into the hierarchical subdivision matrix. This sparse matrix reduces all downstream operations like matrix assembly or residual evaluation to standard sparse linear algebra on the finest mesh $\T_\L$ without recomputing any integrals. We give explicit algorithms for constructing the active degrees of freedom from a subdivision partition, including a frontier-sweep procedure for the interface basis functions at level boundaries.

For the adaptive solution of PDEs, we describe a \textsc{Solve}--\textsc{Estimate}--\textsc{Mark}--\textsc{Refine}--\textsc{Pad} loop in which the non-standard \textsc{Pad} step enforces the subdivision partition condition after Dörfler marking \robert{by alternating pinch resolution and condition-I grading to a fixed point, refining the coarse regions that would otherwise break de Rham exactness}. Numerical experiments on the Maxwell eigenvalue problem confirm that exact subdivision partitions reproduce the reference spectrum without spurious modes and adaptive experiments on the mixed vector Laplace equation demonstrate \robert{our} adaptive loop.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The finite element exterior calculus (FEEC)~\cite{Arnold.FEEC} provides a systematic framework for constructing stable, structure-preserving discretisations of partial differential equations (PDEs) involving differential forms. Its central structure is the discrete de Rham complex: a sequence of finite-dimensional function spaces connected by the exterior derivative $\extd$. Exactness of this complex is the key algebraic property that prevents spurious modes in Maxwell eigenvalue problems, guarantees inf-sup stability in mixed formulations, and underpins the commuting diagram that makes error analysis tractable. Classical realisations of the FEEC use the lowest-order Whitney forms, also known as the continuous Galerkin ($\mathrm{CG}_1$), N\'ed\'elec ($\mathrm{NED}_1$), and discontinuous ($\mathrm{DG}_0$) spaces.

Subdivision algorithms, originally developed for geometric modelling, produce limit functions that are globally smooth on arbitrary triangular meshes. For example, Loop's scheme~\citep{Loop.1987} generates globally $\mathcal{C}^1$ scalar limit functions. Wang~\citep{Wang.2006,Wang.2008} introduce subdivision schemes for $1$- and $2$-forms that commute with the discrete exterior derivative from Discrete Exterior Calculus (DEC, \cite{Hirani.2003,Desbrun.2006}). These subdivision schemes give rise to subdivision $k$-form spaces in the DEC sense \citep{Goes.2015}. \cite{Piel.2026} translate their work to finite elements and introduce a framework for subdivision-based finite elements that form discrete de Rham complexes on meshes with uniform refinement.

Such uniform subdivision spaces assign the same resolution everywhere: every basis function has a support whose size is determined by the coarse mesh $\T_\lzero$. This is adequate for globally smooth solutions but wasteful when the PDE solution is rough only in a small part of the domain. In isogeometric analysis, the analogous limitation of globally refined B-spline spaces has been addressed through hierarchical B-spline spaces~\citep{Evans.2020}, which mix basis functions from different refinement levels to obtain spatially varying resolution while retaining linear independence. Extending this idea to spaces of differential forms is non-trivial: activating basis functions at different levels can break the exactness of the de Rham complex unless the selection is governed by a compatible combinatorial rule.
The analogous construction for B-spline de Rham complexes has been studied in~\cite{Buffa2011,Evans.2020,Shepherd.2024,Dijkstra.2025}, where admissibility conditions on the hierarchical mesh ensure exactness of the complex.
A central mechanism in these works is a \emph{completion step} that, after marking basis functions for refinement, enforces a compatibility condition on the active set: in~\cite{Cabanas.2026} this takes the form of \emph{$L$-chain completion}, which forces all basis functions in a connecting $L$-chain to be refined simultaneously whenever a conflicting pair is detected.
\checkit{The subdivision \textsc{Pad} step described in Section~4 serves an analogous role (restoring the subdivision partition condition after marking), but the precise correspondence between $L$-chain completion and the partition condition, and whether one implies the other, remains to be investigated.}

This paper provides the matrix-level description and algorithms for hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces that preserve the de Rham complex, serving as the practical and computational complement to~\cite{Piel.2026}. The key computational objects are the subdivision matrices $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$, which represent level-$\l$ basis functions in the Whintney basis of the fine assembly mesh $\T_\L$. A hierarchical space is formed by selecting one column of these matrices per active basis function, according to a \emph{subdivision partition} of $\T_\L$, a combinatorial condition on the patch decomposition of the domain. This condition, characterised in~\cite{Piel.2026}, simultaneously guarantees linear independence and exactness of the complex. Once the hierarchical subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ is assembled, all downstream operations (exterior derivative, mass and stiffness matrices, residual evaluation) reduce to standard sparse linear algebra on $\T_\L$ without recomputing any integrals.

\section{Subdivision $k$-form spaces: from uniform to hierarchical}

\subsection{Subdivision basis functions}

This section collects the most important properties of the uniform subdivision $k$-form spaces introduced in~\cite{Piel.2026}. We focus on the aspects that enable the construction of hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces in a similar way as~\cite{Evans.2020} did for B-splines.

Starting from an initial coarse triangular mesh $\T_\l$ at level $\l = \lzero$, the \emph{subdivision algorithm} produces a sequence of increasingly fine meshes $\T_{\lzero+1}, \ldots, \T_\L$ by repeatedly applying two steps: a \emph{topological refinement} that splits every triangle into four, and a \emph{geometric averaging} step that repositions vertices as weighted averages of neighbouring coarse vertices. We use Loop's subdivision scheme~\cite{Loop.1987} throughout. The resulting \emph{mesh hierarchy} $\{\T_\l\}_{\l=\lzero}^{\L}$ ranges from the coarsest level $\lzero$ to the finest, the \emph{assembly mesh} $\T_\L$. At each level $\l$, we denote the sets of vertices, edges, and faces by $\K^0_\l = \V_\l$, $\K^1_\l = \E_\l$, and $\K^2_\l = \F_\l$, with cardinalities $|\K^k_\l|$.

On each mesh $\T_\l$, the lowest-order Whitney $k$-form spaces are the standard FEEC spaces
\begin{equation}
    \FEECspace{0}{\l} = \mathrm{CG}_1(\T_\l), \qquad \FEECspace{1}{\l} = \mathrm{RT}_0(\T_\l), \qquad \FEECspace{2}{\l} = \mathrm{DG}_0(\T_\l),
\end{equation}
with basis functions $\freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$ indexed by the $k$-simplices $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \K^k_\l$.

The subdivision algorithm induces a linear \emph{subdivision operator} $\Sop{k}{\l}{\l+1}: \FEECspace{k}{\l} \to \FEECspace{k}{\l+1}$ between Whitney spaces on consecutive levels. Composing these one-step operators gives the \emph{accumulated subdivision operator}
\begin{equation}
    \Aop{k}{\l}{\L} \coloneqq \Sop{k}{\L-1}{\L} \circ \cdots \circ \Sop{k}{\l}{\l+1},
\end{equation}
whose coordinate representation with respect to the Whitney bases of $\FEECspace{k}{\l}$ and $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ is the \emph{subdivision matrix} $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ of size $|\K^k_\L| \times |\K^k_\l|$. Column $\coarseIdxone$ of $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ is the coordinate vector of the image of $\freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$ in the fine Whitney basis of $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$.

The \emph{subdivision $k$-form space} on level $\l$ is defined as the image of $\FEECspace{k}{\l}$ under $\Aop{k}{\l}{\L}$,
\begin{equation}
    \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}\big(\T_\l\big) \coloneqq \Aop{k}{\l}{\L}\!\big(\FEECspace{k}{\l}\big),
\end{equation}
spanned by the \emph{subdivision basis functions} $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} \coloneqq \Aop{k}{\l}{\L}\, \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$, i.e.\ the columns of $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ expressed in the fine Whitney basis. At the finest level, $\SDFspace{k}{\L}{\L} = \FEECspace{k}{\L}$ by definition. The key properties of the spaces $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ were established in~\cite{Piel.2026} and will be recalled in Section~\ref{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces}.

\subsection{Computational representations}
\label{subsec:computational_representations}

A $k$-form $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\l}{\L} \in \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \constrVec{\omega}{k}{\l}{\L} = \sum\limits_{\coarseIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}} \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}} \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \Big( \sum\limits_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\L}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\l}{\L}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \Big).
\end{equation}
We can vectorise this equation by introducing the vectors
\begin{equation}
    \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\l} \coloneqq \begin{bmatrix}
        \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{1} \\
        \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{2} \\
        \vdots \\
        \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}}
    \end{bmatrix}, \qquad 
    \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\l}{\L} \coloneqq \begin{bmatrix}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{1} \\
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{2} \\
        \vdots \\
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}}
    \end{bmatrix}, \qquad 
    \freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L} \coloneqq \begin{bmatrix}
        \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{1} \\
        \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{2} \\
        \vdots \\
        \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{\sumdimlk{\L}{k}}
    \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We can then re-write $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\l}{\L} \in \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ as
\begin{equation}
    \constrVec{\omega}{k}{\l}{\L} = \big( \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\l}{\L} \big)^T \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\l} = \big( \freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L} \big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\l}{\L} \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\l}.
\end{equation}
This implies
\begin{equation}
    \big( \freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L} \big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\l}{\L} = \big( \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\l}{\L} \big)^T = \begin{bmatrix}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{1} & \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{2} & \dots & \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
and thus shows that the columns of the subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ contain the coefficients of the subdivision basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ in the fine FEEC space $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$.

Uniform subdivision spaces $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ resolve the domain at a single global scale, which is wasteful when the PDE solution contains localised features. Hierarchical spaces break this uniformity by mixing basis functions from \emph{different} levels $\lone$: as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:hier_basis_funs}, a level-$0$ function (left) has broad support capturing large-scale variation, while a level-$2$ function (right) has a much smaller support and can resolve local detail. Selecting which levels to activate in which part of the domain yields an approximation space whose resolution adapts spatially to the solution.

In the uniform space $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$, every $k$-simplex of $\T_\l$ contributes a basis function, so $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l} = \K^k_\l$. The hierarchical space relaxes this: it selects $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l} \subset \K^k_\l$ at each level, mixing coarse basis functions (broad support, large-scale variation) and fine basis functions (small support, local detail) to adapt spatial resolution to the solution. Not every choice of $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$ yields a well-behaved space. For example, the active basis functions may fail to be linearly independent, or the spaces may fail to constitute an exact differential complex. Section~\ref{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces} develops the theory of admissible choices.

The same representation extends to the hierarchical space $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$. A form $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \in \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ is a linear combination of active basis functions drawn from all levels,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_form_sum}
    \constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}
    = \sum\limits_{\l=\lzero}^{\L} \; \sum\limits_{\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \activeDoFSet{k}{\l}}
        \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \, \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}.
\end{equation}
The active DoFs use the same simplex indices $\coarseIdxone$ as in the uniform space $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$, but restricted to $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l} \subset \K^k_\l$. To form explicit vectors we enumerate the active simplices on level $\l$ in increasing index order as $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone_1}, \ldots, \simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}|}}$ with $\coarseIdxone_1 < \cdots < \coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}|}$. The concatenated basis-function and coefficient vectors, grouped by level with blocks separated by vertical bars, are
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_dof_vector}
    \big(\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}\big)^T
    = \Bigl[\;
        \underbrace{
            \freeQ{c}{k}{0}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\; \cdots \;\;
            \freeQ{c}{k}{0}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{0}|}}
        }_{\displaystyle (\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{0})^T}
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{
            \freeQ{c}{k}{1}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\; \cdots \;\;
            \freeQ{c}{k}{1}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{1}|}}
        }_{\displaystyle (\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{1})^T}
        \;\Big|\;
        \cdots
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{
            \freeQ{c}{k}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\; \cdots \;\;
            \freeQ{c}{k}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}|}}
        }_{\displaystyle (\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\L})^T}
    \;\Bigr],
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_basis_vector}
    \big(\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}\big)^T
    = \Bigl[\;
        \underbrace{
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{0}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\cdots\;
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{0}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{0}|}}
        }_{\activeDoFSet{k}{0}}
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{1}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\cdots\;
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{1}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{1}|}}
        }_{\activeDoFSet{k}{1}}
        \;\Big|\;
        \cdots
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\L}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\cdots\;
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\L}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}|}}
        }_{\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}}
    \;\Bigr].
\end{equation}
With the active subdivision basis $\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ chosen and the fine Whitney basis $\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}$ fixed, the form $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ admits two representations that must agree. Equating them and comparing coefficients in $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ defines the hierarchical subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_form_compact}
    \constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}
    = \big(\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}\big)^T \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}
    = \big(\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}\big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}.
\end{equation}
This implies $\big(\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}\big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} = \big(\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}\big)^T$, showing that the columns of $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ are the coefficients of the active subdivision basis functions in $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$. The level grouping of $\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}$ mirrors the block column structure of $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_matrix_blocks}
    \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}
    =
    \Bigl[
        \underbrace{\bigl(\Amat{k}{0}{\L}\bigr)_{\activeDoFSet{k}{0}}}_{\mathbb{R}^{|\K^k_\L| \times |\activeDoFSet{k}{0}|}}
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{\bigl(\Amat{k}{1}{\L}\bigr)_{\activeDoFSet{k}{1}}}_{\mathbb{R}^{|\K^k_\L| \times |\activeDoFSet{k}{1}|}}
        \;\Big|\;
        \cdots
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{\bigl(\Amat{k}{\L}{\L}\bigr)_{\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}}}_{\mathbb{R}^{|\K^k_\L| \times |\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}|}}
    \Bigr],
\end{equation}
so that the block $(\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\l})^T$ multiplies precisely the $|\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}|$ columns of $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ originating from level $\l$, contributing $\sum_{\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \activeDoFSet{k}{\l}} \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \, \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ to $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$.

In practice it is the $\big(\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}\big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ picture that drives the implementation, rather than the $\big(\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}\big)^T$ picture. A bilinear form with fine-level matrix $\mathbf{M}$ on $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ restricts to the hierarchical space as $(\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^T \mathbf{M}\, \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$, so the discrete system is assembled in $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ via standard FEniCS routines and compressed by two sparse matrix multiplications. The fine Whitney basis $\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}$ is handled implicitly by FEniCS and never appears explicitly in code.

The PDE is then solved for the hierarchical DoF vector $\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}$, which collects the coefficients of the solution in the active subdivision basis. Once $\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}$ is known, the fine-level Whitney representation of the solution is recovered as $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}$. This matrix--vector product is the coordinate expression of the inclusion $\iota \colon \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L} \hookrightarrow \FEECspace{k}{\L}$, and the resulting fine-level vector can be used directly for post-processing, visualisation, and residual estimation on $\T_\L$.

The concrete task that remains is therefore constructing $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$, which reduces to choosing the active DoFs $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$. The remainder of this paper focuses on defining $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$ such that the resulting hierarchical spaces are linearly independent and preserve a discrete de Rham complex, and on assembling $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ from those choices.

\guidance{From here the matrix picture is fixed. The object $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ is always the same kind of thing --- a selection of columns from the hierarchy $\{\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}\}_\l$ --- and every downstream operation is the generic Galerkin restriction of Section~\ref{subsec:computational_representations}, \emph{independent} of which columns are chosen. The entire remaining question is therefore combinatorial: \emph{which} column selections (equivalently, which subdivision partitions) yield a linearly independent basis and a de Rham complex. The rest of the paper characterises the admissible selections (Section~\ref{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces}), assembles and adapts them (Section~4), and verifies the resulting exactness (Section~5).}

\section{Constructing hierarchical subdivision spaces}
\label{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces}

\guidance{The matrix representation of Section~\ref{subsec:computational_representations} is unchanged throughout this section; here we determine \emph{which} column selections are admissible. The two tools below --- refinement stencils and derivative stencils --- are the visual language for the two admissibility conditions (linear independence and closure of the complex), which the subdivision partition of Section~\ref{subsec:selecting_active_dofs} then enforces geometrically.}

\subsection{Refinement of basis functions}

\begin{proposition}[Prop.~NUM from~\cite{Piel.2026}]
\label{prop:uniform_spaces_nested}
Let $\lzero \leq \l \leq \L$. For a fixed initial meshes $\T_{\l}$ for $\l \in \{\lzero, \hdots, \L\}$, the
corresponding subdivision $k$-form spaces $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}\big(\T_{\l} \big)$ are nested, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:nested_spaces}
    \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L} \subset  \SDFspace{k}{\l+1}{\L} \subset \hdots \subset \SDFspace{k}{\L}{\L} = \FEECspace{k}{\L},
\end{equation}
and have the dimensions $\dimlk{\l}{k} < \dimlk{\l +1}{k} < \hdots < \dimlk{\L}{k}$.
\end{proposition}

This proposition implies the refinement relation for the subdivision basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$. The resulting properties regarding the refinability of the basis functions form the backbone of the adaptive method presented in this paper. The following discussion is focused on basis functions on intermediate levels $\lone$ with $\lzero \leq \lone \leq \L$ defined as
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:basisrel}
    \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \Aop{k}{\lone}{\L} \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\lone}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\lone}{\L}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \; \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \, ,
\end{equation}
where $\Amat{k}{\lone}{\L}$ is the subdivision matrix, cf.~\cite{Piel.2026}. Splitting the accumulated subdivision operator $\Aop{k}{\l}{\L}$ reveals the following relation between the basis functions $\constrVec{\Phi}{k}{\lone}{\L}$ and $\constrVec{\Phi}{k}{\lone + 1}{\L}$ on neighbouring levels.

\begin{lemma}[Refinement Relation, {\cite{Piel.2026}}]
    \label{lemma:hiermeshrel}
    Let $\lone$ be an arbitrary level such that $\lzero \leq \lone \leq \L$ and let $\Amat{k}{\l}{\l+1}$ be the subdivision matrix associated to $\Aop{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}$. Then, the basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ and $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ satisfy the following \emph{refinement relation}
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:refinement_relation}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum\limits_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\lone+1}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \; \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \; .
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    See~\cite{Piel.2026}.
\end{proof}

We will capture relationships between different basis functions across the mesh hierarchy through \textit{stencils}. Since there is a one-to-one correspondence between basis functions and simplices, we will formulate these stencils as maps between (sets of) simplices across the hierarchy. Thus, formally, a stencil is a map $\powerset(\K^k_{\lone}) \to \powerset\!\bigl(\bigsqcup_{\l=\lzero}^{\L}\bigsqcup_{k=0}^{2}\K^k_\l\bigr)$, where $\powerset$ denotes the powerset.

We choose to formulate stencils with respect to simplices instead of basis functions because that enables us to use the adjacency operator $\KmKnl{m}{n}{\lone}$ and refinement operators $\KKltoL{k}{\lone}{\ltwo}$ introduced in~\cite{Piel.2026}. The remainder of this section sets up refinement stencils based on the refinement relation~\ref{eq:refinement_relation}, while the next section lays the foundations for the derivative stencil by investigating the relation of basis functions of different form degrees.

The hierarchical refinement relation in Lemma~\ref{lemma:hiermeshrel} provides a way to re-express a basis function $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ as a linear combination of finer basis functions. This idea of expressing coarse basis functions through finer basis functions is a core concept in this work. The following definition introduces the corresponding formalism called refinement stencils. After that, we highlight one particular known refinement stencil and then present a construction for more general refinement stencils.

\begin{definition}[Refinement Stencil]
    \label{def:deep_ref_stencil}
    Let $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ denote the basis function of $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ that is associated to the simplex $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}$.
    A refinement stencil is a collection of simplices $\simplex{k}{\ltwo}{\fineIdxone}$ across the finer levels $\ltwo \in \{\lone\!+\!1, \, \hdots, \L\}$ whose associated basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\ltwo}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ can exactly re-express $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$. More concretely, a refinement stencil $\RSt\big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big)$ is a map
    \begin{equation}
        \label{equ:refstencilunion}
        \RSt : \K^k_{\lone} \to \powerset \Big(\bigcup_{\ltwo = \lone+1}^{\L} \K^k_{\ltwo} \Big), \quad  \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \mapsto \RSt \big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)
    \end{equation}
    such that there exist coefficients $\constrQ{c}{k}{\ltwo}{\lone}{\fineIdxone} \neq 0$ satisfying
    \begin{equation}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\simplex{k}{\ltwo}{\fineIdxone} \in \RSt \big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)} \constrQ{c}{k}{\robert{\ltwo}}{\robert{\lone}}{\fineIdxone} \; \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\ltwo}{\L}{\fineIdxone}.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{corollary}

    Let $\RSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)$ be a refinement stencil of $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ with $\lzero \leq \lone \leq \ltwo \leq \L$. It then holds that
    \begin{equation}

The coefficients $\constrQ{c}{k}{\ltwo}{\lone}{\fineIdxone}$ above are connected to the non-zero entries of the subdivision matrices. In fact, we can compute them by tracing back the contributions of certain fine basis functions to the coarse one analogously to the refinement relation~(\ref{eq:refinement_relation}). The only difference is that the refinement stencil $\RSt \big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)$ now contains simplices across different refinement levels, i.e. $\ltwo$ is not constant for $\simplex{k}{\ltwo}{\fineIdxone}$. We are only concerned with the existence of the coefficients $\constrQ{c}{k}{\ltwo}{\lone}{\fineIdxone}$ because our goal is typically not to exactly re-express the coarse basis functions but to test wether they are in the space spanned by some finer basis functions. That is why we are going to formulate indicators for such linear dependence of coarse basis functions based on the existence of refinement stencils.

In the following, we will systematically construct refinement stencils by iterating the canonical refinement stencil defined next. According to eq.~\ref{eq:refinement_relation}, all basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ with non-zero entries $\Amatij{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0$ are required to re-express the coarse basis function $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$. The following definition formalises this selection of basis functions.

\begin{definition}[\robert{Canonical} Refinement Stencil]
    Let $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ denote the basis function of $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ that is associated to the simplex $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}$. The canonical refinement $\cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big)$ of $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ is the map
    \begin{eqnarray}
        \cRSt: \K^k_{\lone} \to \powerset\big( \K^k_{\lone+1}\big), \qquad \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \mapsto \Big\{ \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone} \in \K^k_{\lone+1} \colon \; \Amatij{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0 \Big\}.
    \end{eqnarray}
\end{definition}

As we did before, we will slightly abuse the notation and overload the canonical refinement stencil to act on sets of simplices on the same level $\lone$, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \cRSt: \powerset\big(\K^k_{\lone}\big) \to \powerset\big( \K^k_{\lone+1}\big), \qquad \simplexsubset{k}{\lone} \mapsto \bigcup_{\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \simplexsubset{k}{\lone}}\cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big).
\end{equation}

We call this refinement stencil canonical because it is the only one that contains simplices solely on level $\lone\!+\!1$ and is thus uniquely determined by the refinement relation~\ref{eq:refinement_relation}. Once we allow simplices from finer levels $\ltwo > \lone\!+\!1$, uniqueness is lost. For instance, if $\cRSt(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}) = \{\simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_1}, \ldots, \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_n}\}$, then for each $m = 1, \ldots, n$,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:example_ref_stencil}
    \RSt_m = \cRSt\big(\simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_m}\big) \cup \big\{\simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_i} : i \neq m\big\}
\end{equation}
is a valid refinement stencil of $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}$ with simplices on levels $\lone\!+\!1$ and $\lone\!+\!2$, yielding $n$ distinct alternatives to $\cRSt(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone})$. To guarantee linear independence of the chosen basis functions, we need to ensure that every refinement stencil of every active DoFs contains at least one inactive DoF. We refer to \citep{Piel.2026} for this discussion and only use their results here to motivate the choice of active DoFs in Subsection~\ref{subsec:selecting_active_dofs}.

\subsection{Derivatives of basis functions}

The previous concepts set up the hierarchical nature of the subdivision $k$-form spaces. Ultimately, they will allow us to pick and choose our active degrees of freedom as we wish (with certain restrictions).

We now set up the differential structure of the spaces. First,~\cite{Piel.2026} shows that the uniform subdivision $k$-form spaces comprise the de Rham complex
\begin{equation}
\begin{tikzcd}
  \constrVec{\mathfrak{R}}{}{\lone}{\L}\colon \quad 0 \arrow[r] & \SDFspace{0}{\lone}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \SDFspace{1}{\lone}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \SDFspace{2}{\lone}{\L} \arrow[r] & 0.
\end{tikzcd}
\end{equation}
on all intermediate levels $\lone$ with $\lzero \leq \l \leq \lone \leq \L$. Throughout this work, we do not distinguish between $\extd : \FEECspace{k}{\lone} \to \FEECspace{k+1}{\lone}$ and $\auxextd : \SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L} \to \SDFspace{k+1}{\lone}{\L}$ like in~\cite{Piel.2026}. We will denote both operators by $\extd$ because they largely coincide for our purposes.

The coordinate expression of the action of the exterior derivative $\extd$ on the spaces $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ follows directly from the coarse-level oriented incidence matrix $\Dmat{k}{\lone}$~\citep{Hirani.2003,Desbrun.2006,Piel.2026} by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:derivative_relation_basis}
    \extd \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\coarseIdxtwo=1}^{\sumdimlk{\lone}{k+1}} \Dmatij{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \, \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k+1}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxtwo}.
\end{equation}

Since $\Dmat{k}{\lone}$ is the signed incidence matrix of $\T_\lone$, only the $(k+1)$-simplices of $\T_\lone$ adjacent to $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}$ appear with non-zero coefficients. In the context of splines, this is equivalent to forward-differencing, in particular for triangular splines like box splines~\citep{de_boor_box_1993}.

\begin{definition}[Derivative Stencil]
    \label{def:derivative_stencil}
    Let $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ be a basis function of $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ associated with the $k$-simplex $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \K^k_{\lone}$. The derivative stencil $\DSt$ is a map 
    \begin{equation}
        \DSt : \K^k_{\lone} \to \powerset \big( \K^{k+1}_{\lone} \big), \qquad \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \robert{\mapsto} \big\{ \simplex{k+1}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}: \; \Dmatij{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0 \big\}.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

Once again, we overload the derivative stencil such that it can also act on sets of simplices:
\begin{equation}
    \DSt : \powerset\Big(\K^k_{\lone}\Big) \to \powerset \big( \K^{k+1}_{\lone} \big), \qquad \simplexsubset{k}{\lone} \mapsto \bigcup_{\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \simplexsubset{k}{\lone}} \DSt \big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)
\end{equation}

This definition together with Eq.~\eqref{eq:derivative_relation_basis} implies the following two propositions.

\begin{proposition}
    \label{prop:d_tilde_on_phi}
    $\robert{\extd} \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum\limits_{\simplex{k+1}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \DSt(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone})} \Dmatij{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \, \robert{\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k+1}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxtwo}}$
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}
    \label{cor:derivative_support}
    $\support \big( \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} \big) = \support \big( \robert{\extd} \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} \big) = \bigcup\limits_{\simplex{k+1}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \DSt(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone})} \mathrm{supp} \big( \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k+1}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxtwo} \big).$
\end{proposition}

The derivative stencil is key for ensuring that every active $k$-form basis function has its derivative stencil present in the $(k+1)$-form space and that the resulting spaces constitute a differential complex. The following lemma makes the stencil concrete for the Wang schemes~\cite{Wang.2006,Wang.2008} used in this work.

\begin{lemma}[{\cite{Piel.2026}}]
    \label{lemma:derivative_stencil}
    Let $\freeQ{v}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \V_{\lone}$ and $\freeQ{e}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \E_{\lone}$ be a vertex and an edge on level $\lone$. For the Wang subdivision schemes~\cite{Wang.2006,Wang.2008} (whose $0$-form scheme coincides with Loop subdivision~\cite{Loop.1987}), the derivative stencils are given by
    \begin{equation}
        \DSt\big( \freeQ{v}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big) = \EVl{\lone}\big( \freeQ{v}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big) \quad \text{and} \quad \DSt\big( \freeQ{e}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \big) = \FEl{\lone}\big( \freeQ{e}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \big),
    \end{equation}
    or more generally, $\DSt\big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big) = \K^{k+1}\K^k_{\lone}\big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big)$ for any $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \K^k_{\lone}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    See~\cite{Piel.2026}.
\end{proof}

\cite{Piel.2026} go on to show that the derivative of every active basis function can be represented by active basis functions of the next space in the complex. To obtain this result, they need to show that every simplex in the derivative stencil has a refinement stencil in the active DoFs. In this work, we only use the derivative stencil to motivate the choice of active DoFs in Subsection~\ref{subsec:selecting_active_dofs}.

\subsection{Selecting active DoFs using subdivision partitions}
\label{subsec:selecting_active_dofs}

Section~\ref{subsec:computational_representations} established that constructing the hierarchical subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ reduces to choosing, at each level $\l$, an index set $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l} \subset \K^k_\l$ of active simplices. Each such choice selects the corresponding columns of $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$, assembling $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ block by block as in~\eqref{eq:hierarchical_matrix_blocks}. This section addresses the question left open there: which choices of $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$ are admissible? Collecting the active simplices across levels,
\begin{equation}
    \activeDoFSet{k}{} \coloneq \bigcup_{\l=\lzero}^{\L} \activeDoFSet{k}{\l},
\end{equation}
the corresponding hierarchical space is
\begin{equation}
    \activeSDFspaces{k}{} \coloneq \mathrm{span}\bigl( \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ is the active basis function vector from~\eqref{eq:hierarchical_basis_vector}.

Not every choice yields a well-behaved hierarchical space $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$. The selected columns of $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ must be linearly independent, must together represent forms that cover the entire domain, and the spaces for successive form degrees must close the de Rham complex. The remainder of this section characterises the admissible choices via the notion of a \emph{subdivision partition}.

\guidance{The two admissibility requirements have a purely visual reading in terms of the refinement and derivative stencils introduced above. A selection is linearly \emph{independent} when every active basis function's refinement stencil still contains an inactive DoF; the spaces \emph{close into a complex} when every active function's derivative stencil is itself active. The subdivision partition defined below is the geometric bookkeeping that enforces both conditions at once, so that admissibility can be checked by inspecting patch interfaces rather than the abstract stencil machinery. This is what the stencils were for.}

\robert{The preceding subsections establish that every level-$\l$ basis function $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ is a column of the subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$, and that the exterior derivative commutes with these matrices via~\eqref{eq:derivative_relation_basis}. Choosing active degrees of freedom therefore amounts to selecting a subset of columns across the hierarchy $\bigl\{\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}\bigr\}_\l$, subject to linear independence, full-domain coverage, and closure of the de Rham complex.

A geometrically natural unit for organising this selection is a \emph{mesh patch}: a connected submesh of $\T_\l$ whose simplices are all activated together. Tiling the domain with patches at (possibly) different levels turns the column-selection problem into a geometric tiling problem, with the constraints checkable locally at patch boundaries. Crucially, the subdivision of a patch's basis functions is already encoded in $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$: restricting to the columns indexed by $\K^k(\calP_\l)$ gives exactly the level-$\L$ representations of those basis functions, with no additional patch-level operator required.

A \emph{patch} $\calP_\l \subset \T_\l$ is a non-empty, edge-connected collection of faces of $\T_\l$, with boundary $\partial\calP_\l$ the set of edges adjacent to exactly one face of $\calP_\l$. We write $\calP_{\l \to \L}$ for the collection of level-$\L$ faces obtained by repeatedly splitting the faces of $\calP_\l$.

Two patches $\calP_{\l_i}$ and $\calP_{\l_j}$ are \emph{non-overlapping} if their images in $\T_\L$ share at most edges: $\I_{(\calP_{\l_i}, \calP_{\l_j}) \to \L} \coloneqq \calP_{\l_i \to \L} \cap \calP_{\l_j \to \L} \subset \E_\L(\T_\L)$. A \emph{covering} $\C(\T_\L) = \bigcup_{\l_i,n}\calP^n_{\l_i}$ is a set of pairwise non-overlapping patches whose images tile $\T_\L$, i.e.\ $\T_\L = \bigcup_{\l_i,n}\calP^n_{\l_i \to \L}$. We write $\C_{\l_i}$ for the level-$\l_i$ patches and $\C_{>\l_i}$, $\C_{\geq\l_i}$, $\C_{<\l_i}$, $\C_{\leq\l_i}$ for the corresponding level subsets.}

\robert{
\begin{definition}[Subdivision Partition]
    \label{def:subdiv_partition}
    A covering $\C(\T_\L)$ is a \emph{subdivision partition} if every patch satisfies a minimum-size condition: every face of every patch, after one step of refinement, must contain at least one vertex whose entire face-ring lies strictly in the interior of the refined patch (i.e.\ not on any interface with a neighbouring patch). This rules out interior linear dependence of basis functions; see~\cite{Piel.2026} for the precise statement.
\end{definition}
}

\robert{The key design choices in Definition~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs} are illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:active_dofs}. The need for interface DoFs is easiest to see in the $k=1$ column. At level $\l=0$ (top row, orange edges), only the edges interior to the level-$0$ patches are active; the boundary edges shared with the finer level-$1$ region are absent. At level $\l=1$ (middle row), those same boundary edges appear as active 1-form DoFs. Without them, the exterior derivative $\extd\omega^0$ of a level-$0$ 0-form basis function near the patch boundary would map to an edge with no associated active 1-form DoF, breaking the de Rham complex. More generally, when two patches at levels $\l_i < \l_j$ share a boundary, interface DoFs are added at \emph{every} intermediate level $\l_i < \l_t \leq \l_j$. This prevents basis functions from very different levels meeting abruptly across the interface, which would otherwise create an ill-conditioned system. We do not have rigorous estimates that quantify this stiffening effect, and it remains a subject for further investigation.

The interface DoFs are attributed exclusively to the \emph{finer} side of the patch boundary, not the coarser side. Including coarser-side DoFs would introduce linear dependence: any coarser-level basis function restricted to the interface is already expressible as a linear combination of the finer-level basis functions there (cf.\ the refinement stencil condition in Section~\ref{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces}). Restricting to the finer side therefore maintains linear independence while still closing the de Rham complex, as made precise in the theorems at the end of this section.}

\begin{definition}[Active $k$-form degrees of freedom]
    \label{def:active_k_form_dofs}
    Let $\C\big(\T_{L}\big) = \bigcup_{\l_i = \l_0}^L \; \bigcup_{n = 0}^{N_{\l_i}} \; \calP^n_{\l_i}$ be a subdivision partition of $\T_L$. The active $k$-form degrees of freedom $\D^k\big(\C\big)$ are defined as
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:active_k_form_dofs}
        \D^k \big(\C\big) \coloneqq \underbrace{\bigcup_{\calP^n_{\l_i} \in \C}}_{(i)} \Bigg[ \underbrace{\K^k \big( \calP^n_{\l_i} \big)}_{(ii)} \cup \underbrace{\bigcup_{\calP^m_{\l_j} \in \C_{> \l_i}}}_{(iii)} \; \underbrace{\bigcup_{\l_t = \l_i +1}^{\l_j}}_{(iv)} \bigg[ \underbrace{\K^k\V_{\l_t} \Big( \V_{\l_t} \big( \I_{(\calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_t}, \calP^m_{\l_j})} \big) \Big)}_{(v)} \cap \underbrace{\K^k_{\l_t} \big( \calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_t} \big)}_{(vi)} \bigg] \Bigg].
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

Unpacking this definition: for every patch (\textit{i}) $\calP^n_{\l_i}$, we first add all of its $k$-simplices (\textit{ii}). Further, for all relatively finer patches (\textit{iii}) $\calP^m_{\l_j}$ with $\l_j > \l_i$ and all intermediate levels (\textit{iv}) $\l_t$ with $\l_i < \l_t \leq \l_j$, all interface simplices (\textit{v}) are added if they lie on the coarser side (\textit{vi}) of the interface, i.e.\ can be generated by refining patch $\calP^n_{\l_i}$ to level $\l_t$.

\begin{definition}[Adaptive subdivision differential form spaces]
    \label{def:adaptive_function_spaces}
    Fix an initial mesh $\T_{\l}$ and maximum subdivision level $L$. Let the subdivision partition $\C\big(\T_L\big)$ induce the active $k$-form degrees of freedom $\D^k \big( \C \big)$. The \textbf{active basis functions} of the space $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ are given by
    \begin{equation}
        \Phi_{\D^k (\C)} \coloneqq \bigcup_{\sigma^k_{\l_i, p} \in \D^k} \Big\{ \phi^k_{\l_i \to L, p} \Big\}
    \end{equation}
    and thus
    \begin{equation}
        \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L} \coloneqq \mathrm{span}\big( \Phi_{\D^k (\C)} \big).
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\noindent Note that the basis functions are increasingly dense for higher levels $\l_i$ because the resolution of the patches $\calP_{\l_i}$ and thus $\D^k_{\l_i}$ scales with roughly $4^{\l_i - \l}$. Two essential properties must be established for the spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$: linear independence of the basis functions and the de Rham complex. The proofs of both properties are given in~\cite{Piel.2026}; the main results are restated below.

\begin{remark}
    The numerical examples in Section~5 use subdivision partitions where only adjacent levels share an interface. In that case, the active DoF formula~\eqref{eq:active_k_form_dofs} simplifies: only a single layer of interface simplices needs to be added at the finer of the two adjacent levels.
\end{remark}

Finally, we are going to re-state the two main theorems of \cite{Piel.2026} regarding linear independence and the exactness of the complex.

\begin{theorem}
    Let $\C$ be a subdivision partition of the mesh hierarchy $\T_\l$ for $\l \in \{ \lzero, \hdots, \L\}$. The basis functions of the hierarchical spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ from Definition~\ref{def:adaptive_function_spaces} are linearly independent.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    The necessary properties of a subdivision partition were chosen so that linear independence was guaranteed, see \cite{Piel.2026} for the entire proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
    Let $\C$ be an subdivision partition. The hierarchical spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ constitute the differential complex
    \begin{equation}
    \begin{tikzcd}
        \mathfrak{R}_\C: \qquad 0 \arrow[r] & \mathbb{R} \arrow[r, "\subset"] & \hierSDFspace{0}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \hierSDFspace{1}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \hierSDFspace{2}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r] & 0.
    \end{tikzcd}
    \end{equation}
    The hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces with vanishing trace form the differential complex
    \begin{equation}
    \begin{tikzcd}
        \mathring{\mathfrak{R}}_\C: \qquad 0 \arrow[r] & \hierSDFspace{0}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \hierSDFspace{1}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \hierSDFspace{2}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\int"] & \mathbb{R} \arrow[r] & 0.
    \end{tikzcd}
    \end{equation}
    If $\C$ is an \emph{exact} subdivision partition of a \emph{simply-connected} domain, the complexes $\mathfrak{R}_\C$ and $\mathring{\mathfrak{R}}_\C$ are exact.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    See \cite{Piel.2026}.
\end{proof}


\section{Implementation and adaptive loop}

\subsection{Assembling the hierarchical subdivision matrix}

\robert{The implementation of the active subdivision space proceeds in two stages.
Given a subdivision partition $P$, we first enumerate the active degrees of
freedom $\D^k[\l]$ for each form degree $k \in \{0,1,2\}$ and level $\l$
using Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildactivedofs}. We then assemble, for each form
degree $k$, the sparse matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ whose columns are
the level-$\L$ coefficient expansions of the active basis functions
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildsubdivmatrix}). Galerkin projection of all
bilinear forms to the active space then reduces to standard sparse
matrix--matrix products, without any re-assembly of integrals.

The active DoFs split into two contributions at each level $\l$.
\emph{Native} DoFs are the $k$-simplices of every active face $f \in
P[\l]$. \emph{Interface} DoFs are additional basis functions at the
boundary between a level-$\l$ active region and a refined region on level
$\l + 1$: these correspond to subdivision basis functions whose support
straddles the level boundary and are not captured by the native-DoF
construction alone. They are produced by a frontier sweep
(steps~(a)--(d) of Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildactivedofs}) that maintains the
set of interior edges separating resolved (coarse-side) faces from
unresolved (finer) regions. Each level the frontier is split into child
edges and interface vertices; the vertices emit the DoFs they introduce on
the following level. The ordering of operations within each level is fixed:
old frontier edges are consumed first, new ones are spawned, DoFs are
emitted from the previous level's vertices, and finally the frontier is
split for the next level.}

\robert{It is helpful to group the active degrees of freedom by level $\l_t$
rather than by the individual patches $\calP^n_{\l_i}$ of
Definition~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs}. The following lemma re-formulates
$\D^k(\C)$ in exactly that way, and it is this level-indexed form that
Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildactivedofs} implements.}

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lemma:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}
    The active degrees of freedom $\D^k$ induced by the subdivision partition $\C$ can equivalently be described by
    \begin{equation}
        \D^k \big( \C \big) = \bigcup_{\l_t = \l}^L \D^k_{\l_t} \big( \C \big)
    \end{equation}
    with
    \begin{equation}
        \D^k_{\l} \big( \C \big) = \K^k_{\l} \big(\C_{\l}\big)
    \end{equation}
    for $\l_t = \l$ and
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}
        \D^k_{\l_t} \big( \C \big) = \K^k_{\l_t} \big(\C_{\l_t}\big) \cup \bigcup_{\calP^n_{\l_i} \in \C_{<\l_t}} \; \bigcup_{\calP^m_{\l_j} \in \C_{\geq \l_t}} \; \bigg[ \K^k\V_{\l_t} \Big( \V_{\l_t} \big(\I_{(\calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_t}, \calP^m_{\l_j})} \big) \Big) \cap \K^k_{\l_t} \big(\calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_t}\big) \bigg].
    \end{equation}
    for $\l_t = \l \!+\!1, \hdots, L$.
\end{lemma}

\robert{The correspondence between Lemma~\ref{lemma:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}
and Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildactivedofs} is direct. The algorithm is indexed by
level $\l = 0, \ldots, \L$, mirroring $\D^k(\C) = \bigcup_{\l_t} \D^k_{\l_t}(\C)$,
and its two stages realise the two terms of~\eqref{eq:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}:
the native DoFs of Step~1 produce the term $\K^k_{\l_t}(\C_{\l_t})$, while the
frontier sweep of Step~2 produces the interface term. By contrast, the
patch-pair indexing of Definition~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs} would translate
into a doubly-nested loop over patch pairs, which is not what we implement.}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\color{orange}
\caption{$\textsc{BuildActiveDoFs}(\C)$: active degrees of freedom from a subdivision partition}
\label{alg:buildactivedofs}
\BlankLine
\tcp{1.\ Native DoFs}
$\D^2 \leftarrow \C$\;
\For{$\l = 0, \, \ldots, \, \L$}{
    $\D^0[\l] \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    $\D^1[\l] \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    \For{each $f \in \C[\l]$}{
        $\D^0[\l] \leftarrow \D^0[\l] \cup \mathrm{verts}(f)$\;
        $\D^1[\l] \leftarrow \D^1[\l] \cup \mathrm{edges}(f)$\;
    }
}
\BlankLine
\tcp{2.\ Interface DoFs (frontier sweep)}
$\mathrm{frontier} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
$\mathrm{pendingVerts} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
\For{$\l = 0, \, \ldots, \, \L$}{
    \tcp{(a) consume frontier edges whose fine side is now resolved at level $\l$}
    $\mathrm{frontier} \leftarrow \{ e \in \mathrm{frontier} : \text{fine-side faces of } e \notin P[\l] \}$\;
    \tcp{(b) spawn new frontier edges: interior edges of $\mathcal{F}_\l$ with exactly one adjacent face in $P[\l]$}
    $\mathrm{frontier} \leftarrow \mathrm{frontier} \cup \textsc{FindInterfaceEdges}(\l, \D)$\;
    \tcp{(c) emit DoFs from interface vertices spawned at level $\l - 1$}
    \For{each $v \in \mathrm{pendingVerts}$}{
        $\D^0[\l] \leftarrow \D^0[\l] \cup \{v\}$\;
        $\D^1[\l] \leftarrow \D^1[\l] \cup \mathrm{activeEdges}(v)$\;
        $\D^2[\l] \leftarrow \D^2[\l] \cup \mathrm{activeFaces}(v)$\;
    }
    $\mathrm{pendingVerts} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    \tcp{(d) split frontier edges into child edges and interface vertices for level $\l + 1$}
    \If{$\l < \L$}{
        $\mathrm{newFrontier} \leftarrow \emptyset$;\quad $\mathrm{newVerts} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
        \For{each $e \in \mathrm{frontier}$}{
            $(e_1, e_2, v_{\mathrm{mid}}) \leftarrow \textsc{SplitEdge}(e)$ at level $\l + 1$\;
            $\mathrm{newFrontier} \leftarrow \mathrm{newFrontier} \cup \{e_1, e_2\}$;\quad
            $\mathrm{newVerts} \leftarrow \mathrm{newVerts} \cup \{v_{\mathrm{mid}}\}$\;
        }
        \For{each endpoint $v$ of edges in $\mathrm{frontier}$, taken once per $v$}{
            \lIf{$v$ borders a refined region}{$\mathrm{newVerts} \leftarrow \mathrm{newVerts} \cup \{v\}$}
        }
        $\mathrm{frontier} \leftarrow \mathrm{newFrontier}$;\quad
        $\mathrm{pendingVerts} \leftarrow \mathrm{newVerts}$\;
    }
}
\Return $\D$\;
\end{algorithm}

\robert{The interface DoFs emitted in step~(c) include $2$-form (face)
contributions from the fine side of the level boundary: the native $2$-form DoFs
on the coarser side are already added in step~1, and the interface emission adds
the child faces of the coarser active fan that meet the interface, consistent with
Definition~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs}.}

\robert{Once the active DoFs $\D$ are known, the active subdivision matrix
$\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ is assembled column by column. Let
$\Smat{k}{\l}{\L}$ denote the accumulated subdivision matrix for $k$-forms
from level $\l$ to level $\L$, i.e.\ the product of per-level operators
$\Smat{k}{\l}{\l+1}, \ldots, \Smat{k}{\L-1}{\L}$. For an active simplex
$s \in \D^k[\l]$, the level-$\L$ expansion of the corresponding basis
function is column $s$ of $\Smat{k}{\l}{\L}$. The columns of
$\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ are ordered level by level (all level-$0$
active DoFs first, then level $1$, etc.); this ordering is exploited by the
preconditioner described in Section~\ref{sec:preconditioner}.}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{$\textsc{BuildSubdivMatrix}(\D, k)$: active subdivision matrix}
\label{alg:buildsubdivmatrix}
\KwIn{active DoFs $\D$, form degree $k \in \{0,1\}$}
\KwOut{matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \in \mathbb{R}^{K_\L \times K_\D}$, where $K_\L = |\mathcal{K}^k_\L|$ and $K_\D = \sum_\l |\D^k[\l]|$}
$\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \leftarrow (K_\L \times K_\D)$ sparse zero matrix;\quad $\mathrm{col} \leftarrow 0$\;
\For{$\l = 0, \ldots, \L$}{
    $\mathbf{S} \leftarrow \Smat{k}{\l}{\L}$ \tcp{accumulated subdivision matrix from level $\l$ to $\L$}
    \For{each simplex $s \in \D^k[\l]$}{
        $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}[\,:\,,\mathrm{col}] \leftarrow \mathbf{S}[\,:\,,s]$;\quad $\mathrm{col} \leftarrow \mathrm{col} + 1$\;
    }
}
\Return $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$\;
\end{algorithm}}

\robert{Given $\Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ and $\Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$, the
active-space bilinear forms are obtained by Galerkin restriction of the
embedding-level operators, assembled once before the adaptive loop:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{M}_0 = (\Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top \mathbf{M}\, \Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L},
    \quad
    \mathbf{C}_0 = (\Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top \mathbf{C}\, \Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L},
    \quad
    \mathbf{B}_0 = (\Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top \mathbf{B}\, \Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{M}$, $\mathbf{C}$, $\mathbf{B}$ are the mass, curl--curl,
and gradient-coupling matrices on $\T_\L$. The resulting block system is
\begin{equation}
    K =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\mathbf{M}_0 & \mathbf{B}_0 \\
        \mathbf{B}_0^\top & \mathbf{C}_0
    \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
which is symmetric indefinite.}

\subsection{The adaptive loop}

\guidance{The matrix representation stays fixed across the loop: every step re-selects columns of $\{\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}\}_\l$ and reassembles the generic Galerkin system of Section~\ref{subsec:computational_representations}. What the loop actually changes --- and must keep admissible --- is the subdivision partition, i.e.\ the column index set. The \textsc{Pad} step is the mechanism that repairs admissibility after marking.}

This section describes the adaptive loop used in the numerical experiments. The loop consists of the steps
\begin{equation}
\begin{tikzcd}
    \mathrm{SOLVE} \arrow[r] & \mathrm{ESTIMATE} \arrow[r] & \mathrm{MARK} \arrow[r] & \mathrm{REFINE} \arrow[r] & \mathrm{PAD} \arrow[llll, rounded corners=0pt, to path={(\tikztostart.south) -- ([yshift=-1.em]\tikztostart.south) -- ([yshift=-1.em]\tikztotarget.south) -- (\tikztotarget.south)}]
\end{tikzcd}
\end{equation}

The following sections will cover the individual steps of this loop, and focus particularly on the non-standard choices we made during the implementation. The $\mathrm{SOLVE}$ step involves solving a linear system. We used Eigen's (\robert{cite}) sparse MINRES solver with a block-diagonal approximate (mass-lumped) Schur complex preconditioner (\robert{maybe keep some information from below here and delete what's below.}). For the $\mathrm{ESTIMATE}$ step, we use a standard $\Hcurl$ estimator to compute residuals per face in the subdivision partition. These residuals are then re-distributed to the active vertices in the partition. The $\mathrm{ESTIMATE}$ step is covered in more detail in Section~\ref{subsec:estimate}. We employ greedy Doerfler marking for the vertex residuals to refine vertex stars. This typically leads to inexact subdivision partitions, and thus needs a $\mathrm{PADDING}$ step to recover its exactness, see Section~\ref{subsec:refinement_and_padding}.

\vspace{3mm}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Adaptive loop}
\label{alg:adaptive_loop}
\KwIn{initial partition $\C$, Dörfler parameter $\theta$, termination criteria}
\For{$\mathrm{step} = 0, \ldots, S - 1$}{
    $\D \leftarrow \textsc{BuildActiveDoFs}(\C)$ \tcp{Alg.~\ref{alg:buildactivedofs}}
    $(\Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L}, \Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L}) \leftarrow \textsc{BuildSubdivMatrices}(\D)$ \tcp{Alg.~\ref{alg:buildsubdivmatrix}}
    $(\mathbf{M}_0, \mathbf{C}_0, \mathbf{B}_0) \leftarrow \bigl((\Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top \mathbf{M} \Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L},\; (\Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top \mathbf{C} \Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L},\; (\Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top \mathbf{B} \Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L}\bigr)$\;
    $(\sigma_h, u_h) \leftarrow \mathrm{MINRES}$ solve of $K(\sigma, u) = (0,\; (\Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top f)$ with preconditioner $\mathbf{P}$ \tcp{Section~\ref{sec:preconditioner}}
    $\eta \leftarrow \textsc{AggregateEstimators}(\{\eta^2_T\}_{T \in \T_\L},\, P)$ \tcp{Alg.~\ref{alg:aggregate}}
    $P \leftarrow \textsc{DoerflerRefine}(P, \eta, \theta)$ \tcp{mark $+$ refine $+$ pad; Alg.~\ref{alg:doerfler}, \ref{alg:padding}}
}
\end{algorithm}}

\paragraph{SOLVE.}
\label{sec:preconditioner}
\robert{We solve the discrete system on the hierarchical space $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ with an iterative solver. For the mixed problems considered here the block system $K$ is symmetric indefinite, so we use MINRES with a block-diagonal SPD preconditioner
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{P} =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \mathbf{M}_0 & 0 \\
        0 & \mathbf{S}
    \end{pmatrix},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{S} = \mathbf{C}_0 + \mathbf{B}_0^\top\, \mathrm{diag}(\mathbf{M}_0 \mathbf{1})^{-1}\, \mathbf{B}_0,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{S}$ approximates the Schur complement of the $(1,1)$ block by replacing $\mathbf{M}_0^{-1}$ with a row-sum lumped inverse ($\mathbf{1}$ the all-ones vector of length $\dim \D^0$). Row-sum lumping bounds $\mathbf{M}_0$ from above with a mesh-independent constant for piecewise-linear triangles, giving a stable Schur approximation on the irregular meshes produced by adaptive refinement. Both blocks are factorised once per adaptive step by sparse Cholesky (LDLT) and discarded afterwards, since the active spaces are non-nested between steps; each MINRES iteration then costs one triangular back-solve per block.}

\paragraph{ESTIMATE.}
We compute error indicators $\eta_{\l,a}$ per face $\face{\l}{a} \in \C$ of the subdivision partition. The residual is evaluated on the finest level $\L$ and then aggregated to the coarse parent faces:
\begin{equation}
    \eta_{\l,a} = \int_{\Aop{}{\l}{\L}(\face{\l}{a})} \big\| \iota (f_h) - f \big\|^2,
\end{equation}
where $f_h$ is obtained by applying the discrete PDE operator to $u_h$. Note that the interface DoFs are excluded from the indicator computation.

\begin{example}[Vector Laplace estimator]
For the mixed vector Laplace problem with $(\sigma_h, u_h) \in \hierSDFspace{0}{\C}{\L} \times \hierSDFspace{1}{\C}{\L}$, the basis functions are piecewise linear approximations of the subdivision limit functions. Hence $\extd u_h$ is piecewise constant and $\boldsymbol{\delta}\extd u_h$ vanishes elementwise, while its contribution appears in the curl jumps across edges. The indicator takes the form
\begin{equation}
    \eta_{\l,a}^2 = \sum_{T \,\subset\, \Aop{}{\l}{\L}(\face{\l}{a})} \!\! \left( h_T^2 \|f - \extd \sigma_h\|_{L^2(T)}^2 + \|\sigma_h + \boldsymbol{\delta} u_h\|_{L^2(T)}^2 \right) + \sum_{e \,\subset\, \Aop{}{\l}{\L}(\face{\l}{a})} \!\! h_e \big\|[\![\mathrm{curl}(u_h)]\!]\big\|_{L^2(e)}^2.
\end{equation}
The first sum contains the elementwise residuals of both equations, and the second sum contains the curl jump across interior edges $e$ of the fine mesh $\T_\L$.
\end{example}

\robert{The element indicators $\eta^2_T$ are evaluated on every triangle $T$ of the embedding mesh $\T_\L$ and then aggregated onto the active partition by walking each fine face up the quad-tree to its ancestor in $P$. The resulting per-patch value $\eta_{\l,a}$ is the square root of the summed fine-mesh indicators over all level-$\L$ descendants of $\face{\l}{a}$.}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{$\textsc{AggregateEstimators}(\{\eta^2_T\}_{T \in \T_\L}, P)$: aggregate fine-mesh indicators onto the active partition}
\label{alg:aggregate}
\KwIn{per-element indicators $\eta^2_T$ for all $T \in \T_\L$, partition $P$}
\KwOut{per-patch estimators $\eta[\l][f]$ for $f \in P[\l]$}
$\zeta[\l][f] \leftarrow 0$ for all $\l$, $f \in P[\l]$\;
\For{each $T \in \T_\L$}{
    $g \leftarrow T$\;
    \For{$\l = \L, \L-1, \ldots, 0$}{
        \If{$g \in P[\l]$}{
            $\zeta[\l][g] \leftarrow \zeta[\l][g] + \eta^2_T$;\quad \textbf{break}\;
        }
        $g \leftarrow \lfloor g/4 \rfloor$ \tcp{parent face (SEC convention: children of $f$ are $\{4f, \ldots, 4f+3\}$)}
    }
}
\Return $\eta[\l][f] = \sqrt{\zeta[\l][f]}$ for all active $(\l, f)$\;
\end{algorithm}}

\paragraph{MARK.}
\todo{\guidance{Dissolve the worked example here (narrative point~3). When Section~\ref{subsec:worked_example} is folded in, attach Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline_marking} (residual $\to$ density $\to$ post-mark) as the running illustration of this paragraph, using the closed-form $\eta$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:worked_example_eta} as the concrete input.}}
\robert{We mark by \emph{vertex stars} rather than by individual faces, so that
every refinement acts on the combinatorial object the admissibility argument
reasons about: a complete, single-level vertex star. Marking splits into a
\emph{decision} phase (which vertices to refine) and an \emph{application} phase
(how far to refine each of them). In the decision phase we aggregate the squared
indicators $\eta_{\l,a}^2$ and the star areas onto each vertex $v$ at its coarsest
active level $\l_{\min}(v)$, rank the vertices by the error \emph{density}
$\rho(v) = \big(\textstyle\sum \eta^2\big)/\big(\sum \mathrm{area}\big)$, and greedily
select the densest vertices until the marked energy reaches the Dörfler bulk
$\theta \sum_{\l<\L} \eta_{\l,a}^2$. Faces on level $\L$ are excluded from this
total, as they cannot be refined further within the present hierarchy; the
effective fraction of refineable error selected is therefore at least $\theta$. In
the application phase we \emph{freeze}, on the input partition, a target level
$T(v) = \min\big(\text{finest active level in }v\text{'s star} + 1,\ \L\big)$ for
each selected vertex, and refine each selected star up to $T(v)$. A final
$\textsc{Pad}$ (Alg.~\ref{alg:padding}) restores admissibility.}

\robert{Three choices deserve comment. Ranking by density $\eta^2/\mathrm{area}$
cancels the $\approx\!4\times$ per-level shrinkage of the star area, so a vertex on
a fine feature competes on equal footing with coarse ones and the partition
advances by roughly one level per step. Homogenising the \emph{whole} star to
$\text{finest}+1$ (rather than only the arc active at $\l_{\min}(v)$) makes each
mark a complete uniform-level star and keeps it $1$-irregular internally; going one
level beyond the star's current finest face is what lets an already-uniform star
still advance. Freezing $T(v)$ on the input partition bounds each vertex to a
single homogenisation — reading the live partition instead would let a neighbour
chase an ever-deeper target, an unbounded cascade — and makes the application
idempotent, so the padded result is independent of processing order. The full
decision/application algorithm is given in
Appendix~\ref{app:full_pseudocode} (Alg.~\ref{alg:doerfler_full}).}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{$\textsc{DoerflerRefine}(P, \eta, \theta)$: per-vertex-star marking, refinement, and padding (condensed; full version in Alg.~\ref{alg:doerfler_full})}
\label{alg:doerfler}
\KwIn{partition $P$, per-face indicators $\eta[\l][f]$, parameter $\theta \in (0,1]$}
\KwOut{refined, admissible partition $P'$}
\tcp{DECISION: which vertices}
aggregate $\eta[\l][f]^2$ and $\mathrm{area}(f)$ onto each vertex $v$ at its coarsest active level $\l_{\min}(v)$\;
rank vertices by density $\rho(v) = \mathrm{energy}(v)/\mathrm{area}(v)$; pop highest first, recording the selected $v$, until the marked energy reaches $\theta \sum_{\l<\L} \eta[\l][f]^2$\;
\tcp{APPLICATION: refine each selected star}
\For{each selected $v$}{
    $T(v) \leftarrow \min\big(\text{finest active level in } \mathrm{star}_P(v) + 1,\ \L\big)$ \tcp{frozen on input $P$}
}
$P' \leftarrow P$\;
\For{each selected $v$, coarsest first}{
    refine every face of $v$'s star in $P'$ up to level $T(v)$, skipping faces already at level $\geq T(v)$\;
}
\Return $\textsc{Pad}(P')$ \tcp{Alg.~\ref{alg:padding}}
\end{algorithm}}

\paragraph{PAD.}
\todo{\guidance{Dissolve the worked example here (narrative point~3). Attach Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline_padding} (pre-pad $\to$ after pinch $\to$ after condition-I) as the illustration of this paragraph, and \emph{end the padding exposition with the open hand-off} placed below.}}
\robert{The padding step is non-standard and specific to our setting. After
marking, the refined covering may violate the subdivision-partition admissibility
condition of Definition~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs}, and it can do so in two
qualitatively different ways. A coarse region that is \emph{pinched} at a vertex —
its coarse faces split into two or more disconnected arcs around that vertex —
carries a spurious harmonic and hence the wrong cohomology; a coarse/fine interface
that is too tight leaves faces without a fully-covered vertex, breaking local
completeness of the basis. $\textsc{Pad}$ repairs both by running two rules
\emph{alternately to a fixed point}: pinch resolution ($\textsc{ResolveVertexPinches}$)
and condition-I padding with grading ($\textsc{PadCondI}$). Each rule only ever
\emph{refines} coarse faces, so the loop terminates (bounded by $\L$); a counter
$\mathrm{padding\_cycles}$ records the productive passes, which was $1$ in every
atomic test and every adaptive step we observed. Pinch resolution subsumes what an
earlier boundary-vertex repair did: a lone coarse boundary \emph{notch} is a single
coarse arc (\emph{fine\,--\,coarse\,--\,fine}), not a pinch, so it is not flagged and
is left to condition-I grading.}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{$\textsc{Pad}(P)$: admissibility padding (condensed; full sub-routines in Alg.~\ref{alg:pinch_full}, \ref{alg:condI_full})}
\label{alg:padding}
\KwIn{partition $P$}
\KwOut{admissible partition $P$}
\Repeat{a full pass refines nothing}{
    $P \leftarrow \textsc{ResolveVertexPinches}(P)$ \tcp{repair pinched coarse regions}
    $P \leftarrow \textsc{PadCondI}(P)$ \tcp{Good-vertex (cond.~I) $+$ grading}
}
\Return $P$\;
\end{algorithm}}

\robert{\textbf{Pinch resolution.} A vertex $v$ is \emph{pinched} when, at its
finest active level $\L_v$, its finest faces form $\geq 2$ disconnected arcs around
its one-ring; equivalently, its coarser faces form $\geq 2$ disconnected arcs. A
contiguous coarse arc counts as \emph{one} arc regardless of the levels of the
faces within it, so a pinch is detected even when its two coarse patches sit at
different levels. We detect every pinch \emph{before} mutating (so resolving one
cannot hide another), then grade each pinched vertex's coarse patches up to $\L_v$,
iterating to a fixed point.}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{$\textsc{ResolveVertexPinches}(P)$ (condensed; full version in Alg.~\ref{alg:pinch_full})}
\label{alg:pinch}
\Repeat{a pass refines nothing}{
    detect all pinched vertices: at each $v$'s finest active level $\L_v$, classify its one-ring faces as \textsc{fine} (active at $\L_v$) or \textsc{coarse} (covered by an active ancestor); flag $v$ if the \textsc{fine} faces form $\geq 2$ arcs\;
    \For{each flagged $v$}{grade $v$'s \textsc{coarse} one-ring faces up to level $\L_v$ (one level at a time along each ancestor chain)\;}
}
\end{algorithm}}

\robert{\textbf{Condition-I padding with grading.} With $\mathrm{covered}(g,\l)$
meaning that $g$ or a coarser ancestor of $g$ is active, and a vertex $w$
\emph{Good} at level $\l$ when its entire one-ring is $\mathrm{covered}(\cdot,\l)$,
condition~I (the admissibility requirement, Def.~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs})
demands that every active face have a Good vertex. We enforce it first, to a fixed
point; grading — bounding the level span of any vertex's active faces by
$\mathrm{maxSpan}$, purely for conditioning — is run last, on the span violations
that survive the now-admissible partition. This ordering avoids over-refining,
since condition~I usually refines the coarse frontier faces that would otherwise
trip grading.}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{$\textsc{PadCondI}(P)$ (condensed; full version in Alg.~\ref{alg:condI_full})}
\label{alg:condI}
\tcp{Phase 1 --- admissibility (condition I)}
\Repeat{no face is refined this pass}{
    refine every active face that has no Good vertex\;
}
\tcp{Phase 2 --- grading}
\Repeat{no vertex spans $> \mathrm{maxSpan}$ levels}{
    refine the coarsest active faces of every vertex whose active faces span $> \mathrm{maxSpan}$ levels; re-run Phase 1\;
}
\Return $P$\;
\end{algorithm}}

\subsection{Exactness and padding on atomic partitions}
\label{subsec:exactness_atomic}

\guidance{This subsection is the verification promised at the end of the \textsc{Pad} discussion: it establishes that pinch resolution and condition-I padding \emph{reliably} restore the correct cohomology across the admissibility boundary, not just on the single constructed input of the (dissolved) worked example.}

\robert{Before applying the adaptive loop to a PDE, we validate the admissibility
construction directly on a suite of atomic test cases and the two failure modes it
is designed to prevent. Each case is a small concentric-ring mesh around a single
interior vertex of chosen valence --- a topological \emph{disk}, so that
\emph{every} discrete harmonic that appears is spurious. For each partition we
compute the de Rham cohomology of the active space (from the ranks of the discrete
$\extd$) before and after $\textsc{Pad}$; Table~\ref{tab:atomic-cohomology}
collects the results under both natural and vanishing-trace (Dirichlet) boundary
conditions.}

\robert{A non-admissible partition can fail in two qualitatively different ways.
In a \emph{hard failure} the kernel/image of $\extd$ on the active space genuinely
changes, producing a spurious harmonic --- a discrete $k$-form that is closed but
not exact with no topological counterpart --- which corrupts the Hodge
decomposition and, for Maxwell, injects the spurious zero modes of
Section~\ref{subsec:worked_example}. In a \emph{soft failure} the active basis is
merely linearly \emph{dependent}: its column space is still an exact subcomplex, so
the cohomology is correct, but the naive DoF count overstates the space dimension
and the mass matrix is singular. In Table~\ref{tab:atomic-cohomology} a hard
failure shows up as a nonzero harmonic $\dim\mathcal{H}^1$ on a disk, while a soft
failure shows up as a rank deficit $n_k - \dim V^k > 0$; both distort the Euler
characteristic $\chi$ away from the disk value $\chi = 1$, and both are removed by
padding.}

\robert{The controlled examples delineate the admissibility boundary precisely
(Fig.~\ref{fig:loop_horseshoe}, \ref{fig:control_boundary}):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \robert{\textbf{A coarse loop injects a spurious $\mathcal{H}^1$.} A
    one-band-wide coarse annulus (the ``$3$-ring, inner$+$outer refined'' row) is
    \emph{both} a hard failure ($\dim\mathcal{H}^1 = 1$) and a soft failure
    ($n_1 = 360$, $\operatorname{rank} = 342$, deficit $18$): the single richest
    case. Padding refines the middle band and restores an exact, independent basis.}
    \item \robert{\textbf{Break the loop and the defect vanishes.} Replacing the
    closed annulus by an open arc (``horseshoe'') makes the coarse region a disk
    again: no spurious harmonic, confirming that the defect is topological (the
    closed loop), not a mesh-size artifact.}
    \item \robert{\textbf{It is specifically a one-band effect.} A
    \emph{two}-band-wide coarse annulus (the ``$4$-ring, middle refined'' negative
    control) is already admissible --- padding is a no-op --- so the defect is a
    one-band-strip phenomenon, not a property of any coarse annulus.}
    \item \robert{\textbf{Boundary notches are a Dirichlet-only, additive defect.}
    Refining the stars of boundary ``corner'' vertices leaves coarse notches; under
    natural BCs these are harmless slits, but under Dirichlet each is a relative
    loop, and independent notches \emph{add} (six symmetric notches give exactly six
    Dirichlet harmonics).}
\end{itemize}
\robert{The two-level \emph{pinch} and its \emph{doubling law}
($\dim\mathcal{H}^1 = 2$, $\chi = -1$ before padding, both restored after) are the
``Valence-$8$ pinch, level~2'' row of Table~\ref{tab:atomic-cohomology}, already
illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:pinch_examples}. Further atomic cases are collected in
Appendix~\ref{app:figure_gallery}.}

\subsection{Maxwell Eigenvalue problem}
\label{subsec:maxwell}

\guidance{Thesis of this subsection, stated up front so it does not read as a Maxwell-flavoured repeat of Section~\ref{subsec:exactness_atomic}: Section~\ref{subsec:exactness_atomic} established that padding restores the correct cohomology; this section shows the practical consequence for an actual eigensolver. Because a spurious harmonic is closed, its Rayleigh quotient is exactly zero, so the admissibility defect lives entirely at $\lambda = 0$ and leaves the physical band uncontaminated --- in sharp contrast to the interleaved pollution of non-compatible elements. That confinement-to-the-kernel is the new point here, not implied by the cohomology counts of Section~\ref{subsec:exactness_atomic}.}

\robert{The Maxwell cavity (curl--curl) eigenvalue problem is a classical test case for
structure-preserving discretisations. On the domain $\M = [0,\pi]^2$ its weak form
is: find $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ and $u \in \HocurlM{\M}$, $u \neq 0$, such that
\begin{equation}
  (\extd u, \extd v)_\M = \lambda (u, v)_\M \qquad \forall v \in \HocurlM{\M},
\end{equation}
with analytic spectrum $\lambda = i^2 + j^2$, $(i,j) \in \mathbb{Z}^2_{\geq 0}\setminus\{0\}$,
i.e.\ $1, 1, 2, 4, 4, 5, \ldots$. We impose homogeneous Dirichlet (vanishing-trace)
boundary conditions, so the relevant invariant is the \emph{relative} cohomology
(mod $\partial\M$). Since the square is contractible, its relative first Betti
number is zero, and \textbf{every harmonic $1$-form appearing in the
discretisation is therefore spurious}. Applying Loop subdivision alters the domain
slightly, so we do not compare the discrete spectrum directly with the analytic
$i^2+j^2$; instead we compare three subdivision partitions that use the same
approximation of $\M$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{inexact} --- a mixed-level partition whose interface violates admissibility (Fig.~\ref{fig:Maxwell_inexact_DoFmesh});
    \item \textbf{exact (padded)} --- the \emph{same} partition after $\textsc{Pad}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:Maxwell_exact_DoFmesh});
    \item \textbf{FEEC (uniform)} --- the finest FEEC space $\FEECspaceZero{1}{\L}$, refining the whole domain to level $\L$, which is the verifiably spurious-mode-free reference~\cite{Arnold.FEEC}.
\end{itemize}}

\robert{Table~\ref{tab:maxwell_spectrum} and Figure~\ref{fig:Maxwell_eigenvalues} report the
result. The physical ($\lambda > 0$) band is \emph{identical} across the three
partitions (to plotted precision) and tracks the analytic values; the only defect
of the inexact partition is $\dim\mathcal{H}^1 = 2$ spurious harmonics, which
padding removes ($\dim\mathcal{H}^1 \to 0$). The reason the pollution is confined
to $\lambda = 0$ follows from the exact-sequence structure. A non-admissible
interface makes the active space fail to be a subcomplex, and the concrete symptom
is a discrete $1$-form that is \emph{closed but not exact} --- a spurious harmonic.
Being closed, $\extd u_h = 0$, so its Rayleigh quotient
$(\extd u_h, \extd u_h)/(u_h, u_h) = 0$: it can appear \emph{only} at $\lambda = 0$
and never in the physical band. This contrasts sharply with classical spectral
pollution for non-compatible elements, where spurious eigenvalues interleave with
the physical band; here the admissibility failure is confined to the kernel of
$\extd$, so the physical spectrum stays clean.}

\robert{The defect must be read off the rank-based $\dim\mathcal{H}^1$ (from the ranks of
the discrete $\extd$), \emph{not} the raw count of near-zero eigenvalues. The
physical kernel $\extd(\hierSDFspace{0}{\C}{\L})$ of gradients is large and
mesh-dependent, and padding changes the gradient DoF count, so the raw zero-count
is not comparable between partitions: here it is $125$ (inexact) versus $90$
(exact), which taken alone would misleadingly suggest padding \emph{removed} modes,
even as the cohomologically meaningful $\dim\mathcal{H}^1$ falls from $2$ to $0$.}

\subsection{Vector Laplace problem}

The $1$-form Laplace problem is given by
\begin{equation}
  (\extd \boldsymbol{\delta} + \boldsymbol{\delta} \extd) u = f
\end{equation}
on a domain $\M$. Since the operator $\boldsymbol{\delta}$ is not well-defined for a (subdivision) $1$-form, we introduce the auxiliary variable $\boldsymbol{\delta} \sigma = u$ and use the FEEC-typical mixed weak formulation
\begin{align}
  (\sigma, \tau)_\M - (u, \extd \tau)_\M &= 0 \qquad \forall \tau \in H^1(\M), \\
  (\extd \sigma, v)_\M + (\extd u, \extd v)_\M &= (f, v)_\M \qquad \forall v \in \HocurlM{\M}.
\end{align}

This section focuses the \emph{circular layer} test case from \cite{Cabanas.2026}. Its analytical solution is given by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:circular_layer_test_case}
    \Vec{u}_{\mathrm{CL}}(x) = \tanh \big(\alpha (\vvert{\vvert{x}}^2 - R^2) \big) \; \Vec{b}(x) \quad \text{with} \quad \Vec{b}(x) = \begin{bmatrix}
        \sin(\pi x_1) \\
        \sin(\pi x_2)
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
and $\alpha = 100$ and $R = 0.3$. The vector part $\Vec{b}(x)$ provides a background field while the $\tanh$ term adds a sharp, radially symmetric transition between $+\Vec{b}$ and $-\Vec{b}$. The right-hand side $f_{\mathrm{CL}}$ corresponding to $\Vec{u}_{\mathrm{CL}}$ can be computed using the (strong) vector laplace equation. The panels of Figure~\ref{fig:circular_layer_adaptive_solution} show the evolution of the solution $\Vec{u}^n_{\mathrm{CL}}$ and the subdivision partition $\C^n_{\mathrm{CL}}$ after $n$ steps of the adaptive loop.

\section{Conclusion}

\robert{We have presented a complete matrix-level framework for hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces and their use in an adaptive loop for structure-preserving PDE discretisations. The central observation is that any active subdivision basis function is a column of an accumulated subdivision matrix $\Smat{k}{\l}{\L}$; selecting columns according to a subdivision partition produces a hierarchical space $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ whose linear independence and de Rham exactness are guaranteed by the partition condition proved in the companion paper~\cite{Piel.2026}. Once the hierarchical subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ is assembled, all bilinear forms are obtained by Galerkin restriction of the embedding-level operators without recomputing any integrals, and the active-space linear system reduces to standard sparse linear algebra on the finest mesh $\T_\L$.

The adaptive loop adds a subdivision-specific \textsc{Pad} step to the standard \textsc{Solve}--\textsc{Estimate}--\textsc{Mark}--\textsc{Refine} cycle. Padding (Algorithm~\ref{alg:padding}) alternates pinch resolution and condition-I grading to a fixed point: pinch resolution repairs a pinched coarse region that would otherwise carry a spurious harmonic (a closed but non-exact discrete $1$-form, breaking de Rham exactness), while condition-I grading enforces the interior-vertex (Good-vertex) requirement and bounds the level jump at coarse/fine interfaces. Both rules only ever refine, are local, and are inexpensive relative to the linear solve.

The numerical experiments confirm the two key structural properties of the framework. For the Maxwell eigenvalue problem, exact subdivision partitions reproduce the reference FEEC spectrum without spurious modes; inexact ones introduce them. This serves as a direct diagnostic for the correctness of the active-DoF construction and the de Rham complex structure. For the mixed vector Laplace equation, the adaptive loop concentrates refinement at the location of steep gradients and achieves convergence behaviour consistent with the hierarchical approximation theory.

Several directions remain open. The precise correspondence between the subdivision PAD step and the $L$-chain completion used in hierarchical B-spline AFEM~\cite{Cabanas.2026} has not been established; clarifying this connection would relate the two frameworks and potentially enable convergence proofs to be transferred. On the solver side, the block-diagonal LDLT preconditioner used here is effective for the problem sizes considered, but a multigrid preconditioner that respects the globally supported subdivision basis would reduce the per-step cost for larger problems; this requires a prolongation operator compatible with $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ and is left for future work. Finally, a promising direction is to adopt the overlapping patch framework of Bressan~\cite{Bressan.2024} in place of the non-overlapping subdivision partition used here. Overlapping patches can provide a richer set of admissible active-DoF configurations and may admit a more flexible PAD step, at the cost of requiring a partition-of-unity treatment to maintain linear independence; adapting the matrix-level assembly and the de Rham complex to that setting is an open problem.}

\section{Full marking and padding pseudo-code}
\label{app:full_pseudocode}

\robert{This appendix gives the full versions of the marking and padding routines
whose condensed forms appear in Section~\ref{subsec:worked_example}
(Alg.~\ref{alg:doerfler}, \ref{alg:padding}, \ref{alg:pinch}, \ref{alg:condI}).}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{$\textsc{DoerflerMarkPerVertex}(P, \eta, \theta)$: full per-vertex-star marking}
\label{alg:doerfler_full}
\KwIn{partition $P$, per-face indicator $\eta_f$ for active $f$, $\theta \in (0,1]$}
\tcp{DECISION --- which vertices}
$E \leftarrow 0$\;
\For{each level $\l$, each active face $f \in P[\l]$ with $\l < \L$}{
    $E \leftarrow E + \eta_f^2$\;
    \For{each vertex $v$ of $f$}{
        $\mathrm{energy}[\l][v] \mathrel{+}= \eta_f^2$;\quad $\mathrm{area}[\l][v] \mathrel{+}= \mathrm{area}(f)$;\quad $\l_{\min}[v] \leftarrow \min(\l_{\min}[v], \l)$\;
    }
}
\lIf{$E = 0$}{\Return $P$}
$\mathrm{PQ} \leftarrow$ max-heap\;
\For{each vertex $v$}{
    $\l \leftarrow \l_{\min}[v]$; \quad $\mathrm{PQ}.\mathrm{push}\big(\text{key} = \mathrm{energy}[\l][v]/\mathrm{area}[\l][v],\ \l,\ v\big)$\;
}
$\mathrm{selected} \leftarrow [\,]$;\quad $\mathrm{acc} \leftarrow 0$;\quad $\mathrm{marked}[\l] \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
\While{$\mathrm{acc} < \theta E$ \textbf{and} $\mathrm{PQ}$ nonempty}{
    $(\_, \l, v) \leftarrow \mathrm{PQ}.\mathrm{pop}()$;\quad $\mathrm{selected}.\mathrm{append}(\l, v)$\;
    \For{each face $f \in \mathrm{star}(v)$ with $f \in P[\l]$, $f \notin \mathrm{marked}[\l]$}{
        $\mathrm{marked}[\l] \leftarrow \mathrm{marked}[\l] \cup \{f\}$;\quad $\mathrm{acc} \mathrel{+}= \eta_f^2$\;
    }
}
\tcp{APPLICATION --- refine each selected star}
\lFor{$(\l,v) \in \mathrm{selected}$}{$T[v] \leftarrow \min(\text{finest active level of } \mathrm{star}_P(v) + 1,\ \L)$}
$P' \leftarrow$ copy of $P$\;
\For{$(\l,v) \in \mathrm{selected}$ sorted ascending by $(\l, v)$}{
    \For{each face $f$ of $v$'s one-ring at level $T[v]$}{
        \lIf{$f$ already active at level $\geq T[v]$}{\textbf{continue}}
        grade $f$'s covering active ancestor up to level $T[v]$ (one level at a time)\;
    }
}
\Return $\textsc{Pad}(P')$\;
\end{algorithm}}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{$\textsc{ResolveVertexPinches}(P)$: full pinch resolution}
\label{alg:pinch_full}
\Repeat{a pass refines nothing}{
    \tcp{Phase 1 --- detect (no mutation)}
    $\mathrm{pinches} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    \For{each vertex $v$}{
        $\L_v \leftarrow v$'s finest active level\;
        \lIf{any $\L_v$-ring face is covered only by a finer face}{skip $v$}
        classify each face of $v$'s $\L_v$ one-ring: \textsc{fine} $=$ active at $\L_v$; \textsc{coarse} $=$ covered by an active ancestor at level $< \L_v$\;
        $\mathrm{arcs} \leftarrow$ number of maximal runs of \textsc{fine} faces (cyclic for interior $v$, open for boundary $v$)\;
        \lIf{$\mathrm{arcs} \geq 2$}{$\mathrm{pinches} \leftarrow \mathrm{pinches} \cup \{(\L_v, v)\}$}
    }
    \tcp{Phase 2 --- refine}
    \For{$(\L_v, v) \in \mathrm{pinches}$}{
        \For{each \textsc{coarse} face $f$ of $v$'s $\L_v$-ring}{
            grade $f$'s covering active ancestor up to level $\L_v$ (one level at a time along the chain; deferred, erase-guarded)\;
        }
    }
}
\Return $P$\;
\end{algorithm}}

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{$\textsc{PadPartitionPerVertexGraded}(P)$: full condition-I padding with grading}
\label{alg:condI_full}
\tcp{$\mathrm{covered}(g,\l)$: $g$ or a coarser ancestor of $g$ is active}
\tcp{$\mathrm{Good}(w,\l)$: every face in $w$'s one-ring is $\mathrm{covered}(\cdot,\l)$}
\tcp{Phase 1 --- admissibility (condition I) to a fixed point}
\Repeat{no face is refined this pass}{
    $\mathrm{toRefine} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    \For{each level $\l$, each active level-$\l$ face $f$}{
        \lIf{no vertex of $f$ is $\mathrm{Good}(\cdot,\l)$}{$\mathrm{toRefine} \leftarrow \mathrm{toRefine} \cup \{f\}$}
    }
    apply $\mathrm{toRefine}$: each face $\to$ its $4$ children one level deeper\;
}
\tcp{Phase 2 --- grading: span $\leq \mathrm{maxSpan}$ levels per vertex}
\Repeat{$\mathrm{toRefine} = \emptyset$}{
    $\mathrm{toRefine} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    \For{each vertex $v$}{
        $\mathrm{lo}, \mathrm{hi} \leftarrow \min, \max$ active level among $v$'s active faces\;
        \lIf{$\mathrm{hi} - \mathrm{lo} > \mathrm{maxSpan}$}{$\mathrm{toRefine} \leftarrow \mathrm{toRefine} \cup \{v\text{'s active faces at level } \mathrm{lo}\}$}
    }
    \If{$\mathrm{toRefine} \neq \emptyset$}{
        apply $\mathrm{toRefine}$;\quad re-run Phase 1\;
    }
}
\Return $P$\;
\end{algorithm}}

\subsection{Refinement of basis functions}

\begin{proposition}[Prop.~NUM from~\cite{Piel.2026}]
\label{prop:uniform_spaces_nested}
Let $\lzero \leq \l \leq \L$. For a fixed initial meshes $\T_{\l}$ for $\l \in \{\lzero, \hdots, \L\}$, the
corresponding subdivision $k$-form spaces $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}\big(\T_{\l} \big)$ are nested, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:nested_spaces}
    \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L} \subset  \SDFspace{k}{\l+1}{\L} \subset \hdots \subset \SDFspace{k}{\L}{\L} = \FEECspace{k}{\L},
\end{equation}
and have the dimensions $\dimlk{\l}{k} < \dimlk{\l +1}{k} < \hdots < \dimlk{\L}{k}$.
\end{proposition}

This proposition implies the refinement relation for the subdivision basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$. The resulting properties regarding the refinability of the basis functions form the backbone of the adaptive method presented in this paper. The following discussion is focused on basis functions on intermediate levels $\lone$ with $\lzero \leq \lone \leq \L$ defined as
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:basisrel}
    \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \Aop{k}{\lone}{\L} \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\lone}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\lone}{\L}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \; \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \, ,
\end{equation}
where $\Amat{k}{\lone}{\L}$ is the subdivision matrix, cf.~\cite{Piel.2026}. Splitting the accumulated subdivision operator $\Aop{k}{\l}{\L}$ reveals the following relation between the basis functions $\constrVec{\Phi}{k}{\lone}{\L}$ and $\constrVec{\Phi}{k}{\lone + 1}{\L}$ on neighbouring levels.

\begin{lemma}[Refinement Relation]
    \label{lemma:hiermeshrel}
    Let $\lone$ be an arbitrary level such that $\lzero \leq \lone \leq \L$ and let $\Amat{k}{\l}{\l+1}$ be the subdivision matrix associated to $\Aop{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}$. Then, the basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ and $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ satisfy the following \emph{refinement relation}
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:refinement_relation}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum\limits_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\lone+1}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \; \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \; .
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Starting from Eq.~\eqref{equ:basisrel}, we compute
  \begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} &= \Aop{k}{\l}{\L}  \; \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} = \Aop{k}{\l+1}{\L} \Aop{k}{\l}{\l+1} \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \\
    &= \Aop{k}{\l+1}{\L} \Big( \sum_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\lone+1}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \; \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \Big) \\
    &= \sum_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\lone+1}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \Aop{k}{\l+1}{\L} \big( \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \big) \\
    &= \sum_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\lone+1}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone},
  \end{aligned}
  \end{equation}
  by splitting the subdivision operator $\Aop{k}{\l}{\L}$ and using its linearity.
\end{proof}

We will capture relationships between different basis functions across the mesh hierarchy through \textit{stencils}. Since there is a one-to-one correspondence between basis functions and simplices, we will formulate these stencils as maps between (sets of) simplices.

\begin{definition}[Stencil]
    Let $\powerset$ denote the powerset. A stencil is a map
    \begin{equation}
       \powerset \big(\K^k_{\lone}\big) \to \powerset \big( \bigsqcup\limits_{\l = \lzero}^{\L} \; \bigsqcup\limits_{k=0}^2 \; \K^k_\l \big).
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

We choose to formulate stencils with respect to simplices instead of basis functions because that enables us to use the adjacency operator $\KmKnl{m}{n}{\lone}$ and refinement operators $\KKltoL{k}{\lone}{\ltwo}$ introduced in~\cite{Piel.2026}. The remainder of this section sets up refinement stencils based on the refinement relation~\ref{eq:refinement_relation}, while the next section lays the foundations for the derivative stencil by investigating the relation of basis functions of different form degrees.

The hierarchical refinement relation in Lemma~\ref{lemma:hiermeshrel} provides a way to re-express a basis function $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ as a linear combination of finer basis functions. This idea of expressing coarse basis functions through finer basis functions is a core concept in this work. The following definition introduces the corresponding formalism called refinement stencils. After that, we highlight one particular known refinement stencil and then present a construction for more general refinement stencils.

\begin{definition}[Refinement Stencil]
    \label{def:deep_ref_stencil}
    Let $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ denote the basis function of $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ that is associated to the simplex $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}$.
    A \werner{deep}\robert{/-} refinement stencil is a collection of simplices $\simplex{k}{\ltwo}{\fineIdxone}$ across the finer levels $\ltwo \in \{\lone\!+\!1, \, \hdots, \L\}$ whose associated basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\ltwo}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ can exactly re-express $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$. More concretely, a refinement stencil $\RSt\big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big)$ is a map
    \begin{equation}
        \label{equ:refstencilunion}
        \RSt : \K^k_{\lone} \to \powerset \Big(\bigcup_{\ltwo = \lone+1}^{\L} \K^k_{\ltwo} \Big), \quad  \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \mapsto \RSt \big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)
    \end{equation}
    such that there exist coefficients $\constrQ{c}{k}{\ltwo}{\lone}{\fineIdxone} \neq 0$ satisfying
    \begin{equation}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\simplex{k}{\ltwo}{\fineIdxone} \in \RSt \big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)} \constrQ{c}{k}{\robert{\ltwo}}{\robert{\lone}}{\fineIdxone} \; \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\ltwo}{\L}{\fineIdxone}.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{corollary}
    \label{cor:refinement_support}
    Let $\RSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)$ be a refinement stencil of $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ with $\lzero \leq \lone \leq \ltwo \leq \L$. It then holds that
    \begin{equation}
        \support \big( \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} \big) = \bigcup_{ \simplex{k}{\ltwo}{\fineIdxone} \in \RSt( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} )} \support\big( \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\ltwo}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \big).
    \end{equation}
\end{corollary}

The coefficients $\constrQ{c}{k}{\ltwo}{\lone}{\fineIdxone}$ above are connected to the non-zero entries of the subdivision matrices. In fact, we can compute them by tracing back the contributions of certain fine basis functions to the coarse one analogously to the refinement relation~(\ref{eq:refinement_relation}). The only difference is that the refinement stencil $\RSt \big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)$ now contains simplices across different refinement levels, i.e. $\ltwo$ is not constant for $\simplex{k}{\ltwo}{\fineIdxone}$. We are only concerned with the existence of the coefficients $\constrQ{c}{k}{\ltwo}{\lone}{\fineIdxone}$ because our goal is typically not to exactly re-express the coarse basis functions but to test wether they are in the space spanned by some finer basis functions. That is why we are going to formulate indicators for such linear dependence of coarse basis functions based on the existence of refinement stencils.

In the following, we will systematically construct refinement stencils by iterating the canonical refinement stencil defined next. According to eq.~\ref{eq:refinement_relation}, all basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ with non-zero entries $\Amatij{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0$ are required to re-express the coarse basis function $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$. The following definition formalises this selection of basis functions.

\begin{definition}[\werner{Shallow}\robert{/Canonical} Refinement Stencil]
    Let $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ denote the basis function of $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ that is associated to the simplex $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}$. The canonical refinement $\cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big)$ of $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ is the map
    \begin{eqnarray}
        \cRSt: \K^k_{\lone} \to \powerset\big( \K^k_{\lone+1}\big), \qquad \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \mapsto \Big\{ \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone} \in \K^k_{\lone+1} \colon \; \Amatij{k}{\lone}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0 \Big\}.
    \end{eqnarray}
\end{definition}

As we did before, we will slightly abuse the notation and overload the canonical refinement stencil to act on sets of simplices on the same level $\lone$, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \cRSt: \powerset\big(\K^k_{\lone}\big) \to \powerset\big( \K^k_{\lone+1}\big), \qquad \simplexsubset{k}{\lone} \mapsto \bigcup_{\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \simplexsubset{k}{\lone}}\cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big).
\end{equation}

We call this refinement stencil canonical because it is the only that contains only simplices on level $\lone\!+\!1$ and is thus uniquely determined through the refinement relation~\ref{eq:refinement_relation}. Of course, once we allow simplices and basis functions from finer levels $\ltwo > \lone \! + \! 1$ we lose this uniqueness. For example, there exist multiple refinement stencils of $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}$ with simplices from levels $\lone\!+\!1$ and $\lone\!+\!2$. Denoting
\begin{equation}
    \cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big) = \big\{ \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_1}, \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_2}, \hdots, \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_N} \big\}
\end{equation}
with $N=\big\vert \cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big) \big\vert$, we can find many more refinement stencils of $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}$ like
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:example_ref_stencils_start}
    \RSt_1 &= \cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_1} \big) \cup \big\{\simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_2}, \hdots, \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_N} \big\}, \\[2pt]
    \RSt_2 &= \big\{ \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_1} \big\} \cup \cRSt \big(\simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_2} \big) \cup \big\{ \hdots, \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_N} \big\}, \\
    \vdots \nonumber \\
    \RSt_N &= \big\{ \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_1}, \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_2}, \hdots \big\} \cup \cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_N} \big), \\[2pt]
    \RSt_{12} &= \cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_1} \big) \cup \cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_2} \big) \cup \big\{ \hdots, \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_N} \big\}, \\[2pt]
    \label{eq:example_ref_stencils_intermediate}
    \RSt_{1N} &= \cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_1} \big) \cup \big\{ \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_2}, \hdots \big\} \cup \cRSt \big(\simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_N} \big), \\[2pt]
    \label{eq:example_ref_stencils_end}
    \RSt_{12N} &= \cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_1} \big) \cup \cRSt\big( \simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_2} \big) \cup \big\{ \hdots \big\} \cup \cRSt \big(\simplex{k}{\lone+1}{\fineIdxone_N} \big).
\end{align}

The following section leverages the refinement and derivative stencils to construct hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces that satisfy differential complexes. The cohomology of these complexes will turn out to be a delicate matter.

The rest of this section explores more properties of the basis $\constrVec{\Phi}{k}{\lone}{\L}$ regarding their derivatives. Analogously to the previous section, we introduce a derivative relation and stencil. As before, we consider basis functions on a level $\lone$ with $\lzero \leq \lone \leq \L$ to emphasize that the statements are true for basis functions on all intermediate levels.

\begin{theorem}[Derivative Relation]
    \label{theorem:derivative_relation}
    Given a set of compatible subdivision schemes, the basis functions of $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ satisfy
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:derivative_relation}
        \auxextd \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\coarseIdxtwo = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\lone}{k+1}} \freeQ{c}{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \, \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxtwo} \quad \text{with} \quad \freeQ{c}{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} = \Dmatij{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone}
    \end{equation}
    as described in Assumption~\ref{assumption:PLambda_form_complex}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    The claim follows immediately from Lemma~\ref{lemma:d_tilde_action_in_coordinates} with $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\lone}{\L} = \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Derivative Stencil]
    \label{def:derivative_stencil}
    Let $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ be a basis function of $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ associated with the $k$-simplex $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \K^k_{\lone}$. The derivative stencil $\DSt$ is a map 
    \begin{equation}
        \DSt : \K^k_{\lone} \to \powerset \big( \K^{k+1}_{\lone} \big), \qquad \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} = \big\{ \simplex{k+1}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}: \; \Dmatij{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0 \big\}.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

Once again, we overload the derivative stencil such that it can also act on sets of simplices:
\begin{equation}
    \DSt : \powerset\Big(\K^k_{\lone}\Big) \to \powerset \big( \K^{k+1}_{\lone} \big), \qquad \simplexsubset{k}{\lone} \mapsto \bigcup_{\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \simplexsubset{k}{\lone}} \DSt \big( \simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big)
\end{equation}

This definition together with Theorem~\ref{theorem:derivative_relation} implies the following two propositions.

\begin{proposition}
    \label{prop:d_tilde_on_phi}
    $\auxextd \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum\limits_{\simplex{k+1}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \DSt(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone})} \Dmatij{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \, \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxtwo}$
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}
    \label{cor:derivative_support}
    $\support \big( \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} \big) = \support \big( \auxextd \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} \big) = \bigcup\limits_{\simplex{k+1}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \DSt(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone})} \mathrm{supp} \big( \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k+1}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxtwo} \big).$
\end{proposition}

The derivative stencil provides another valuable tool for constructing our hierarchical function spaces. Every active $k$-form basis function must have its associated derivative stencil present in the $k+1$-form space. Otherwise, the de Rham complex will be broken. Before we move on to considerations of this kind, the next section makes the constructions in this section more concrete. In particular, we present subdivision schemes that are known to satisfy the commutative relation in Lemma~\ref{lemma:compatibility_condition_in_coordinates}. Further, the basis function supports and the refinement and derivative stencils will be described concretely for the schemes adopted in this work.

Recall from Proposition~\ref{prop:d_tilde_on_phi} that $\auxextd$ acts on the basis function $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ of $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:d_tilde_on_basis_function}
    \auxextd \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\simplex{k+1}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \DSt(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone})} \Dmatij{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \; \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k+1}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxtwo}.
\end{equation}
Thus, by Definition~\ref{def:derivative_stencil}, the derivative stencil is given by the following lemma.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lemma:derivative_stencil}
    Let $\freeQ{v}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \V_{\lone}$ and $\freeQ{e}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}e_m \in \E_{\lone}$ be a vertex and an edge on level $\lone$. For the Wang schemes, the derivative stencils are given by
    \begin{equation}
        \DSt\big( \freeQ{v}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big) = \EVl{\lone}\big( \freeQ{v}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \big) \quad \text{and} \quad \DSt\big( \freeQ{e}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \big) = \FEl{\lone}\big( \freeQ{e}{}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \big).
    \end{equation}
    More generally, we can write this as
    \begin{equation}
        \DSt\big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big) = \K^{k+1}\K^k_{\lone}\big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big)
    \end{equation}
    for any $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \K^k_{\lone}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    The claim follows immediately from the characterization of $\Dmat{k}{\lone}$ in Lemma~\ref{lemma:discrete_exterior_derivative}, where $\Dmatij{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0$ if $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \K^{k} \K^{k+1}_{\lone} \big( \simplex{k+1}{\lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} \big)$. Alternatively, one could compare the supports analogously to the proof of Lemma \ref{lemma:canonical_refinement_stencil}.
\end{proof}

The following last lemma for this section establishes the parallels between the compatibility of the subdivision scheme and the compatibility of the refinement and derivative stencils.
\begin{lemma}
    Let $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \K^k_{\lone}$. The derivative stencil and the canonical refinement stencil commute, i.e.
    \begin{equation}
        \cRSt \circ \DSt \big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big) = \DSt \circ \cRSt \big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big)
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    From
    \begin{equation}
        \robert{\K^{k+1}\V_{\lone} = \KmKnl{k+1}{k}{\lone}\K^{k+1}\K^k_{\l_i} \circ \K^{k}\V_{\lone}, \qquad \F\K^{k+1}_{\lone} \circ \K^{k+1}\K^k_{\l_i}\KmKnl{k+1}{k}{\lone} = \F\K^k_{\lone}}
    \end{equation}
    and the Lemmas~\ref{lemma:canonical_refinement_stencil} and~\ref{lemma:derivative_stencil} follows
    \begin{alignat}{2}
        \cRSt \circ \DSt \big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big) &= \K^{k+1}\V_{\lone +1} \circ \VFl{\lone+1} \circ \FFltoL{\lone}{\lone+1} \circ \F\K^{k+1}_{\lone} \circ \KmKnl{k+1}{k}{\lone} \big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big) && \\
        &= \KmKnl{k+1}{k}{\lone+1} \circ \K^{k}\V_{\lone +1} \circ \VFl{\lone+1} \circ \FFltoL{\lone}{\lone+1} \circ \F\K^k_{\lone} \big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big) &&= \DSt \circ \cRSt \big(\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone}\big).
    \end{alignat}
\end{proof}

In the rest of this paper, we will set up adaptive function spaces by mixing basis functions on different subdivision levels and control their linear independence and the de Rham complex by using the refinement and derivative stencils.

On a matrix-level, any subdivision basis function can be described as a column of the subdivision matrix. Ultimately, since the subdivision matrices of all levels $\lone$ map to $\L$, we can pick and choose any column of any subdivision matrix to become an active degree of freedom. This means we can assemble a vector of degree of freedom
\begin{equation}
  \activeDoFSet{k}{} \coloneq \bigcup_{\lone = \l}^{\L} \activeDoFSet{k}{\lone} \quad \text{with} \quad \activeDoFSet{k}{\lone} \subset \K^k_\lone.
\end{equation}
This means that we can obtain a set of active degrees of freedom by simply choosing sets of simplices and their associated subdivision basis functions across the hierarchy. This gives rise to the hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces $\activeSDFspaces{k}{}$ defined as
\begin{equation}
  \activeSDFspaces{k}{} \coloneq \mathrm{span}\big( \SDFbases^k_\activeDoFs \big) \quad \text{with} \quad \SDFbases^k_\activeDoFs \coloneq \bigcup_{\simplex{k}{\lone}{\coarseIdxone} \in \activeDoFSet{k}{}} \; \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Patches and interfaces}

\begin{definition}[$k$-Form patch subdivision operators]
    \label{def:patch_subdiv_operator}
    Let $\calP_{\l} \subset \T_{\l}$ be a patch. The differential $k$-form patch subdivision operators $\mathcal{A}^k_{\calP_{\l} \to L}$ is a map
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{A}^k_{\calP_{\l} \to L}: \;\; \FEECspace{k}{}\big( \closure{\calP}_{\l} \big) \;\; \mapsto \;\; \mathcal{S}_{\calP_{\l}, L} \Lambda^k \big( \T_{\l} \big) \subset \FEECspace{k}{} \big( \closure{\calP}_{\l \to L} \big)
    \end{equation}
    such that
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:k_form_patch_subdiv_operator_condition}
        \mathcal{A}^k_{\calP_{\l} \to L} \Big( \omega^k_{\l} \big\vert_{\closure{\calP}_{\l}} \Big) = \Big( \mathcal{A}^k_{\l \to L} \omega^k_{\l} \Big) \Big\vert_{\closure{\calP}_{\l \to L}} \quad \forall \omega^k_{\l} \in \FEECspace{k}{}\big( \T_{\l} \big).
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

The different domains and operators in this definition are illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:k_form_subdiv_operator}.

\begin{definition}[Degrees of Freedom map]
    Let $\calP_{\l} \subset \T_{\l}$ be a patch. The degrees of freedom maps $\Pi_{\l}$ and $\Pi_{L}$ are bijective maps
    \begin{alignat}{2}
        \Pi_{\l}&: \; \K^k_{\l}\big(\T_\l\big) &&\to \Big\{1,2,\hdots, \big\vert \K^k_{\l}\big(\closure{\calP}_{\l}\big) \big\vert\Big\} \subset \mathbb{N}, \\
        \Pi_{L}&: \; \K^k_{L}\big(\T_L\big) &&\to \Big\{1,2,\hdots, \big\vert \K^k_{L}\big(\closure{\calP}_{\l \to L}\big) \big\vert\Big\} \subset \mathbb{N}.
    \end{alignat}
\end{definition}

The purpose of these degrees of freedom maps is only a re-enumeration of the $k$-simplices of the domain and image of the $k$-form patch subdivision operators to assemble the following matrices.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lemma:subdiv_matrix_of_patch}
    The coordinate expression $\Amat{k}{\calP_\l}{\L}$ of the $k$-form patch subdivision operator $\mathcal{A}^k_{\calP_{\l \to L}}$ has the dimensions $\Big\vert \K^k_L\big(\closure{\calP}_{\l \to L}\big) \Big\vert \times \Big\vert \K^k_{\l}\big(\closure{\calP}_{\l}\big) \Big\vert$ and is given by
    \begin{equation}
        \big(\Amat{k}{\calP_\l}{\L}\big)_{\Pi_{L}(\sigma^k_{L,a}) \, \Pi_{\l}(\sigma^k_{\l, p})} = \big(\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}\big)_{ap} \qquad \text{for all} \quad \sigma^k_{L,a} \in \K^k_L\big(\closure{\calP}_{\l \to L}\big), \;\; \sigma^k_{\l,p} \in \K^k_{\l}\big(\closure{\calP}_{\l}\big).
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    The coordinate expressions of the objects in Eq.~(\ref{eq:k_form_patch_subdiv_operator_condition}) are
    \begin{alignat}{2}
        \label{eq:patch_subdiv_in_coordinates_1}
        \big( \mathcal{A}^k_{\l \to L} \omega^k_{\l} \big) \Big\vert_{\closure{\calP}_{\l \to L}} &= \sum_{\sigma^k_{L,a} \in \K^k_L(\T_L)} &&\bigg( \sum_{\sigma^k_{\l, p} \in \K^k_{\l}(\T_{\l})} \big( \Amat{k}{\l}{\L} \big)_{ap} \; c^k_{\l, p} \bigg) \psi^k_{L, a}\Big\vert_{\closure{\calP}_{\l \to L}}, \\
        \label{eq:patch_subdiv_in_coordinates_2}
        \mathcal{A}^k_{\calP_{\l} \to L} \Big( \omega^k_{\l} \big\vert_{\closure{\calP}_{\l}} \Big) &= \sum_{\sigma^k_{L,b} \in \K^k_L(\calP_{\l \to L})} &&\bigg( \sum_{\sigma^k_{\l, q} \in \K^k_{\l}(\closure{\calP}_{\l})} \big( \Amat{k}{\calP_\l}{\L} \big)_{bq} \; c^k_{\l, q} \bigg) \psi^k_{L, b}.
    \end{alignat}
    First, note that $\mathcal{A}^k_{\calP_{\l} \to L} \Big( \omega^k_{\l} \big\vert_{\closure{\calP}_{\l}} \Big) = \mathcal{A}^k_{\calP_{\l} \to L} \Big( \omega^k_{\l} \big\vert_{\closure{\calP}_{\l}} \Big) \Big\vert_{\closure{\calP}_{\l \to L}}$ by Def.~\ref{def:patch_subdiv_operator} and ultimately Lemma~\ref{lemma:support_of_patch_spaces}. Thus, the restriction in Eq.~\ref{eq:patch_subdiv_in_coordinates_1} also applies to Eq.~\ref{eq:patch_subdiv_in_coordinates_2}. Comparing coefficients then reveals that Eq.~\ref{eq:k_form_patch_subdiv_operator_condition} is satisfied if 
    \begin{equation}
        \big( \rmA^k_{\calP_\l \to L} \big)_{bq} = \big( \rmA^k_{\calP_\l \to L} \big)_{ap} \quad \text{with} \quad b = \Pi_L(\sigma^k_{L,a}) \quad \text{and} \quad q = \Pi_\l(\sigma^k_{\l,p}),
    \end{equation}
    for all $\sigma^k_{L,a} \in \K^k_L\big(\closure{\calP}^k_{\l \to L}\big)$ and $\sigma^k_{\l,p} \in \K^k_\l\big(\closure{\calP}^k_{\l}\big)$.
\end{proof}

This is the most concise coordinate expression of $\mathcal{A}^k_{\calP_{\l \to L}}$. Our implementation does not introduce a degrees of freedom map for the refined patch $\calP_{\l \to L}$ and thus we obtain the following coordinate expression:

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lemma:embedded_subdiv_matrix_of_patch}
    The coordinate expression $\rmA^{\iota, k}_{\calP_{\l \to L}}$ of $\iota_{\calP_{\l}} \circ \mathcal{A}^k_{\calP_{\l \to L}}$ has the dimensions $\Big\vert \K^k_L\big(\T_{L}\big) \Big\vert \times \Big\vert \K^k_{\l}\big(\closure{\calP}_{\l}\big) \Big\vert$ and is given by
    \begin{equation}
        \big(\rmA^{\iota,k}_{\calP_{\l \to L}}\big)_{a \, \Pi_{\l}(\sigma^k_{\l,p})} = \big(\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}\big)_{ap} \qquad \text{for all} \quad \sigma^k_{\l, p} \in \K^k_{\l}\big(\closure{\calP}_{\l}\big).
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    The proof is analogous to the proof of Lemma~\ref{lemma:subdiv_matrix_of_patch} but without the restriction of $\mathcal{A}^k_{\l \to L} \omega^k_{\l}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Subdivision partition}

\begin{definition}[Active $k$-form degrees of freedom]
    \label{def:active_k_form_dofs}
    Let $\C\big(\robert{\T_{L}}\big) = \bigcup_{\l_i = \l_0}^L \; \bigcup_{n = 0}^{N_{\l_i}} \; \calP^n_{\l_i}$ be a subdivision partition of $\T_L$. The active $k$-form degrees of freedom $\D^k\big(\C\big)$ are defined as
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:active_k_form_dofs}
        \D^k \big(\C\big) \coloneqq \underbrace{\bigcup_{\calP^n_{\l_i} \in \C}}_{(i)} \Bigg[ \underbrace{\K^k \big( \calP^n_{\l_i} \big)}_{(ii)} \cup \underbrace{\bigcup_{\calP^m_{\l_j} \in \C_{> \l_i}}}_{(iii)} \; \underbrace{\bigcup_{\l_t = \l_i +1}^{\l_j}}_{(iv)} \bigg[ \underbrace{\K^k\V_{\l_t} \Big( \V_{\l_t} \big( \I_{(\calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_t}, \calP^m_{\l_j})} \big) \Big)}_{(v)} \cap \underbrace{\K^k_{\l_t} \big( \calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_t} \big)}_{(vi)} \bigg] \Bigg].
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\robert{Unpacking this definition reveals that for every patch \textit{(i)} $\calP^n_{\l_i}$, we first add all of its $k$-simplices \textit{(ii)}. Further, for all relatively finer patches \textit{(iii)} $\calP^m_{\l_j}$ with $\l_j > \l_i$ and intermediate levels \textit{(iv)} $\l_t$ with $\L-i < \l_t \leq \l_j$, all simplices along the interface \textit{(v)} are added to the active degrees of freedom if the simplices are on the coarse side \textit{(vi)} of the interface, i.e. can be generated by refining the patch $\calP^n_{\l_i}$ to level $\l_t$.}

For future computations, it is helpful to group the active degrees of freedom by level $\l_t$ rather than by the individual patches $\calP^n_{\l_i}$. The following lemma re-formulates the active degrees of freedom in exactly that way.

\begin{lemma}
    \label{lemma:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}
    The active degrees of freedom $\D^k$ induced by the subdivision partition $\C$ can equivalently be described by
    \begin{equation}
        \D^k \big( \C \big) = \bigcup_{\l_t = \l}^L \D^k_{\l_t} \big( \C \big)
    \end{equation}
    with
    \begin{equation}
        \D^k_{\l} \big( \C \big) = \K^k_{\l} \big(\C_{\l}\big)
    \end{equation}
    for $\l_t = \l$ and
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}
        \D^k_{\l_t} \big( \C \big) = \K^k_{\l_t} \big(\C_{\l_t}\big) \cup \bigcup_{\calP^n_{\l_i} \in \C_{<\l_t}} \; \bigcup_{\calP^m_{\l_j} \in \C_{\geq \l_t}} \; \bigg[ \K^k\V_{\l_t} \Big( \V_{\l_t} \big(\I_{(\calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_t}, \calP^m_{\l_j})} \big) \Big) \cap \K^k_{\l_t} \big(\calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_t}\big) \bigg].
    \end{equation}
    for $\l_t = \l \!+\!1, \hdots, L$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{definition}[Adaptive subdivision differential form spaces]
    \label{def:adaptive_function_spaces}
    Fix an initial mesh $\T_{\l}$ and maximum subdivision level $L$. Let the subdivision partition $\C\big(\T_L\big)$ induce the active $k$-form degrees of freedom $\D^k \big( \C \big)$. The \textbf{active basis functions} of the space $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ are given by
    \begin{equation}
        \Phi_{\D^k (\C)} \coloneqq \bigcup_{\sigma^k_{\l_i, p} \in \D^k} \Big\{ \phi^k_{\l_i \to L, p} \Big\}
    \end{equation}
    and thus
    \begin{equation}
        \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L} \coloneqq \mathrm{span}\big( \Phi_{\D^k (\C)} \big).
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\noindent Note that the basis functions are increasingly dense for higher levels $\l_i$ because the resolution of the patches $\calP_{\l_i}$ and thus $\D^k_{\l_i}$ scales with roughly $4^{\l_i - \l}$. Two essential properties must be established for the spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$: linear independence of the basis functions and the de Rham complex. Both results can be proven by giving additional structure to the spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ and investigating its relation to the uniform subdivision spaces $\SDFspace{\l_i}{L}{k}\big(\T_{\l_i}\big)$. These structures will be introduced in the following sections.

\begin{remark}
    \label{remark:simplification}
    A typical case of adaptive local refinement comprises the resolution of the region around a singularity of the solution. In that case, it is natural to consider subdivision partitions that satisfy
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:simplified_setup_assumption}
        \l_j \neq \l_i \pm 1 \iff \I_{(\calP^n_{\l_i}, \calP^m_{\l_j})} = \varnothing \qquad \forall n = 1,\hdots,N_{\l_i}, \; m = 1,\hdots,N_{\l_i +1},
    \end{equation}
    i.e. the only non-trivial interfaces are between adjacent levels $\l_i$ and $\l_i +1$. Under this assumption, the active degrees of freedom in Eq.~\ref{eq:active_k_form_dofs} simplify drastically to
    \begin{equation}
        \D^k \big( \C \big) = \bigcup_{\calP^n_{\l_i} \in \C} \Bigg[ \K^k\big(\calP^n_{\l_i}\big) \cup \bigg[ \bigcup_{\calP^m_{\l_i +1} \in \C_{\l_i}} \K^k\V_{\l_i +1} \Big( \V_{\l_i +1} \big( \I_{(\calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_i +1}, \calP^m_{\l_i +1})} \big) \Big) \cap \K^k_{\l_i +1} \big( \calP^n_{\l_i \to \l_i +1} \big) \bigg] \Bigg].
    \end{equation}
    This means that we only need to add one layer of simplices of the finer level $\l_i +1$ on the side of $\calP^n_{\l_i +1}$. Note, however, that we still require $\C$ to be a subdivision partition which implies the minimal patch size condition in Eq.~\ref{eq:interior_refined_vertex_condition}. Fig.~\ref{fig:case2} in test case 4 shows an example of one such subdivision partition satisfying Eq.~\ref{eq:simplified_setup_assumption} and the induced $k$-form degrees of freedom.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Maxwell Eigenvalue problem}

The weak form of the continuous Maxwell eigenvalue problem is given by: Find $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ and $u \in \HocurlM{\M}$ such that
\begin{equation}
  (\extd u, \extd v)_\M = \lambda (u, v)_\M \qquad \forall v \in \HocurlM{\M}.  
\end{equation}
Applying Loop subdivision alters the domain of the eigenvalue problem slightly. Thus, we cannot directly compare the obtained spectrum with the analytical solution. However, siscretisations using the FEEC complex $\FEECspaceZero{k}{}$ are known to converge to the spectrum of the analytical solution on the altered domain without introducing spurious modes (cf. \cite{Arnold.FEEC}). For this reason, we compare the spectrum obtained using the hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces with the FEEC spaces $\FEECspaceZero{k}{\L}$.

Figures~\ref{fig:Maxwell_exact_DoFmesh} and \ref{fig:Maxwell_exact_eigenvalues} present a DoF mesh and the eigenvalue spectrum using the subdivision $k$-form spaces defined on the DoF mesh. We observe that the spectrum is faithfully captured. This suggests that the underlying sequence of approximation spaces is exact.

On the other hand, Figures~\ref{fig:Maxwell_inexact_DoFmesh} and \ref{fig:Maxwell_inexact_eigenvalues} present a DoF mesh and an eigenvalue spectrum corresponding to a non-exact complex of subdivision $k$-form spaces. In this case, the discrete spectrum exhibits spurious modes compared to the FEEC discretisation.

\paragraph{Bonus: Instabilities without interface DoFs.} Finally, we want to see what happens if we do not add the interface DoFs. We should probably look at two eigenmodes (with and without the DoFs) and compare them. I expect to see similar instabilities to the ones that arise from boundary truncation.

\subsection{Vector Laplace problem}

The $1$-form Laplace problem is given by
\begin{equation}
  (\extd \boldsymbol{\delta} + \boldsymbol{\delta} \extd) u = f
\end{equation}
on a domain $\M$. Since the operator $\boldsymbol{\delta}$ is not well-defined for a (subdivision) $1$-form, we introduce the auxiliary variable $\boldsymbol{\delta} \sigma = u$ and use the FEEC-typical mixed weak formulation
\begin{align}
  (\sigma, \tau)_\M - (u, \extd \tau)_\M &= 0 \qquad \forall \tau \in \FEECspace{0}{}, \\
  (\extd \sigma, v)_\M + (\extd u, \extd v) &= f \qquad \forall v \in \FEECspace{1}{}.
\end{align}

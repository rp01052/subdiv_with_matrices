\begin{abstract}
We provide the algorithmic and computational complement to the theoretical framework of~\cite{Piel.2026} for hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces on triangular meshes. The central observation is that every basis function is represented by a column of the accumulated subdivision matrix. Selecting active degrees of freedom results in collecting their corresponding columns into the hierarchical subdivision matrix. This sparse matrix reduces all downstream operations like matrix assembly or residual evaluation to standard sparse linear algebra on the finest mesh $\T_\L$ without recomputing any integrals. The substantive question is which selections of active degrees of freedom are admissible, in particular linearly independent and exactness of the complex. We answer this question via the
subdivision-partition conditions that enforce admissibility purely combinatorially. We provide explicit algorithms for constructing the active degrees of freedom from a subdivision partition, including a frontier-sweep procedure for the interface basis functions at level boundaries.

For the adaptive solution of PDEs, we describe a \textsc{Solve}--\textsc{Estimate}--\textsc{Mark}--\textsc{Refine}--\textsc{Pad} loop in which the non-standard \textsc{Pad} step enforces the subdivision partition conditions after Dörfler marking by applying pinch resolution, inadmissible-faces padding, and grading, refining the coarse regions that would otherwise break admissibility. Numerical experiments on the Maxwell eigenvalue problem confirm that exact subdivision partitions reproduce the reference spectrum without spurious modes and adaptive experiments on the mixed vector Laplace equation demonstrate the adaptive loop.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The finite element exterior calculus (FEEC)~\cite{Arnold.FEEC} provides a systematic framework for constructing stable, structure-preserving discretisations of partial differential equations (PDEs) involving differential forms. Its central structure is the discrete de Rham complex: a sequence of finite-dimensional function spaces connected by the exterior derivative $\extd$. Exactness of this complex is the key algebraic property that prevents spurious modes in Maxwell eigenvalue problems, guarantees inf-sup stability in mixed formulations, and underpins the commuting diagram that makes error analysis tractable. Classical realisations of the FEEC use the lowest-order Whitney forms, also known as the continuous Galerkin ($\mathrm{CG}_1$), N\'ed\'elec ($\mathrm{NED}_1$), and discontinuous ($\mathrm{DG}_0$) spaces.

Subdivision algorithms, originally developed for geometric modelling, produce limit functions that are globally smooth on arbitrary triangular meshes. For example, Loop's scheme~\citep{Loop.1987} generates globally $\mathcal{C}^1$ scalar limit functions. Wang~\citep{Wang.2006,Wang.2008} introduce subdivision schemes for $1$- and $2$-forms that commute with the discrete exterior derivative from Discrete Exterior Calculus (DEC, \cite{Hirani.2003,Desbrun.2006}). These subdivision schemes give rise to subdivision $k$-form spaces in the DEC sense \citep{Goes.2015}. \cite{Piel.2026} translate their work to finite elements and introduce a framework for subdivision-based finite elements that form discrete de Rham complexes on meshes with uniform refinement.

Such uniform subdivision spaces assign the same resolution everywhere: every basis function has a support whose size is determined by the coarse mesh $\T_\lzero$. This is adequate for globally smooth solutions but wasteful when the PDE solution is rough only in a small part of the domain. In isogeometric analysis, the analogous limitation of globally refined B-spline spaces has been addressed through hierarchical B-spline spaces~\citep{Evans.2020}, which mix basis functions from different refinement levels to obtain spatially varying resolution while retaining linear independence. Extending this idea to spaces of differential forms is non-trivial: activating basis functions at different levels can break the exactness of the de Rham complex unless the selection is governed by a compatible combinatorial rule.
The analogous construction for B-spline de Rham complexes has been studied in~\cite{Buffa2011,Evans.2020,Shepherd.2024,Dijkstra.2025}, where admissibility conditions on the hierarchical mesh ensure exactness of the complex.
A central mechanism in these works is a \emph{completion step} that, after marking basis functions for refinement, enforces a compatibility condition on the active set: in~\cite{Cabanas.2026} this takes the form of \emph{$L$-chain completion}, which forces all basis functions in a connecting $L$-chain to be refined simultaneously whenever a conflicting pair is detected.

This paper provides the matrix-level description and algorithms for hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces that preserve the de Rham complex, serving as the practical and computational complement to~\cite{companion_paper}. The key computational objects are the subdivision matrices $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$, which represent level-$\l$ basis functions in the Whintney basis of the fine assembly mesh $\T_\L$. A hierarchical space is formed by selecting one column of these matrices per active basis function, according to a \emph{subdivision partition} of $\T_\L$, a combinatorial condition on the patch decomposition of the domain. This condition, characterised in~\cite{companion_paper}, simultaneously guarantees linear independence and exactness of the complex. Once the hierarchical subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ is assembled, all downstream operations (exterior derivative, mass and stiffness matrices, residual evaluation) reduce to standard sparse linear algebra on $\T_\L$ without recomputing any integrals.

\paragraph*{Contributions.} This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A matrix-level construction of hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces (Section~\ref{subsec:computational_representations}),
    \item A selection procedure for the active degrees of freedom and a practical algorithm for assembling the hierarchical subdivision matrix (Sections~\ref{subsec:selecting_active_dofs} and~\ref{subsec:implementation_active_dofs}),
    \item An adaptive refinement loop \textsc{Solve}--\textsc{Estimate}--\textsc{Mark}--\textsc{Refine}--\textsc{Pad}, including a vertex-based marking and refinement scheme and a non-standard padding step that restores the admissibility conditions after refinement (Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive_loop}),
    \item Numerical verification via atomic exactness test cases and the Maxwell eigenvalue problem to confirm the exactness of the discrete complex, and adaptive simulation of the vector Laplace equation to confirm admissibility across the adaptive loop (Section~\ref{sec:numerical_experiments}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Subdivision $k$-form spaces: from uniform to hierarchical}

\subsection{Subdivision basis functions}

This section collects the most important properties of the uniform subdivision $k$-form spaces introduced in~\cite{Piel.2026}. We focus on the aspects that enable the construction of hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces in a similar way as~\cite{Evans.2020} did for B-splines.

Starting from an initial coarse triangular mesh $\T_\l$ at level $\l = \lzero$, the \emph{subdivision algorithm} produces a sequence of increasingly fine meshes $\T_{\lzero+1}, \ldots, \T_\L$ by repeatedly applying two steps: a \emph{topological refinement} that splits every triangle into four, and a \emph{geometric averaging} step that repositions vertices as weighted averages of neighbouring coarse vertices. We use Loop's subdivision scheme~\cite{Loop.1987} throughout. The resulting \emph{mesh hierarchy} $\{\T_\l\}_{\l=\lzero}^{\L}$ ranges from the coarsest level $\lzero$ to the finest mesh $\T_\L$. At each level $\l$, we denote the sets of vertices, edges, and faces by $\K^0_\l = \V_\l$, $\K^1_\l = \E_\l$, and $\K^2_\l = \F_\l$, with cardinalities $|\K^k_\l|$.

On each mesh $\T_\l$, the lowest-order Whitney $k$-form spaces are the standard FEEC spaces
\begin{equation}
    \FEECspace{0}{\l} = \mathrm{CG}_1(\T_\l), \qquad \FEECspace{1}{\l} = \mathrm{RT}_0(\T_\l), \qquad \FEECspace{2}{\l} = \mathrm{DG}_0(\T_\l),
\end{equation}
with basis functions $\freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$ indexed by the $k$-simplices $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \K^k_\l$.

The subdivision algorithm induces a linear \emph{subdivision operator} $\Sop{k}{\l}{\l+1}: \FEECspace{k}{\l} \to \FEECspace{k}{\l+1}$ between Whitney spaces on consecutive levels. Composing these one-step operators gives the \emph{accumulated subdivision operator}
\begin{equation}
    \Aop{k}{\l}{\L} \coloneqq \Sop{k}{\L-1}{\L} \circ \cdots \circ \Sop{k}{\l}{\l+1},
\end{equation}
whose coordinate representation with respect to the Whitney bases of $\FEECspace{k}{\l}$ and $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ is the \emph{subdivision matrix} $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ of size $|\K^k_\L| \times |\K^k_\l|$. Column $\coarseIdxone$ of $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ is the coordinate vector of the image of $\freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$ in the fine Whitney basis of $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$.

The \emph{subdivision $k$-form space} on level $\l$ is defined as the image of $\FEECspace{k}{\l}$ under $\Aop{k}{\l}{\L}$,
\begin{equation}
    \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}\big(\T_\l\big) \coloneqq \Aop{k}{\l}{\L}\!\big(\FEECspace{k}{\l}\big),
\end{equation}
spanned by the \emph{subdivision basis functions} $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} \coloneqq \Aop{k}{\l}{\L}\, \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$, i.e.\ the columns of $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ expressed in the fine Whitney basis. At the finest level, $\SDFspace{k}{\L}{\L} = \FEECspace{k}{\L}$ by definition. The key properties of the spaces $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ were established in~\cite{Piel.2026} and will be recalled in Section~\ref{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces}.

\subsection{Computational representations}
\label{subsec:computational_representations}

A $k$-form $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\l}{\L} \in \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \constrVec{\omega}{k}{\l}{\L} = \sum\limits_{\coarseIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}} \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}} \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \Big( \sum\limits_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\L}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\l}{\L}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \Big).
\end{equation}
We can vectorise this equation by introducing the vectors
\begin{equation}
    \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\l} \coloneqq \begin{bmatrix}
        \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{1} \\
        \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{2} \\
        \vdots \\
        \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}}
    \end{bmatrix}, \qquad 
    \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\l}{\L} \coloneqq \begin{bmatrix}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{1} \\
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{2} \\
        \vdots \\
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}}
    \end{bmatrix}, \qquad 
    \freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L} \coloneqq \begin{bmatrix}
        \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{1} \\
        \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{2} \\
        \vdots \\
        \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{\sumdimlk{\L}{k}}
    \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We can then re-write $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\l}{\L} \in \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ as
\begin{equation}
    \constrVec{\omega}{k}{\l}{\L} = \big( \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\l}{\L} \big)^T \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\l} = \big( \freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L} \big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\l}{\L} \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\l}.
\end{equation}
This implies
\begin{equation}
    \big( \freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L} \big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\l}{\L} = \big( \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\l}{\L} \big)^T = \begin{bmatrix}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{1} & \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{2} & \dots & \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
and thus shows that the columns of the subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ contain the coefficients of the subdivision basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ in the fine FEEC space $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$.

Uniform subdivision spaces $\SDFspace{k}{\lone}{\L}$ resolve the domain at a single global scale, which is wasteful when the PDE solution contains localised features. Hierarchical spaces break this uniformity by mixing basis functions from \emph{different} levels $\lone$: A level-$0$ function has broad support capturing large-scale variation, while a level-$2$ function has a much smaller support and can resolve local detail. Selecting which levels to activate in which part of the domain yields an approximation space whose resolution adapts spatially to the solution.

In the uniform space $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$, every $k$-simplex of $\T_\l$ contributes a basis function, so $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l} = \K^k_\l$. The hierarchical space relaxes this: it selects $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l} \subset \K^k_\l$ at each level, mixing coarse basis functions (broad support, large-scale variation) and fine basis functions (small support, local detail) to adapt spatial resolution using the active DoFs $\activeDoFs^k \coloneq \bigcup_{\l = \lzero}^\L \activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$. Not every choice of $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$ yields a well-behaved space. For example, the active basis functions may fail to be linearly independent, or the spaces may fail to constitute an exact differential complex. Section~\ref{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces} develops the theory of admissible choices.

The same representation extends to the hierarchical space $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$. A form $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \in \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ is a linear combination of active basis functions drawn from all levels,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_form_sum}
    \constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}
    = \sum\limits_{\l=\lzero}^{\L} \; \sum\limits_{\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \activeDoFSet{k}{\l}}
        \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \, \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}.
\end{equation}
The active DoFs use the same simplex indices $\coarseIdxone$ as in the uniform space $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$, but restricted to $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l} \subset \K^k_\l$. To form explicit vectors we enumerate the active simplices on level $\l$ in increasing index order as $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone_1}, \ldots, \simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}|}}$ with $\coarseIdxone_1 < \cdots < \coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}|}$. The concatenated basis-function and coefficient vectors, grouped by level with blocks separated by vertical bars, are
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_dof_vector}
    \big(\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}\big)^T
    = \Bigl[\;
        \underbrace{
            \freeQ{c}{k}{0}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\; \cdots \;\;
            \freeQ{c}{k}{0}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{0}|}}
        }_{\displaystyle (\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{0})^T}
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{
            \freeQ{c}{k}{1}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\; \cdots \;\;
            \freeQ{c}{k}{1}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{1}|}}
        }_{\displaystyle (\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{1})^T}
        \;\Big|\;
        \cdots
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{
            \freeQ{c}{k}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\; \cdots \;\;
            \freeQ{c}{k}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}|}}
        }_{\displaystyle (\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\L})^T}
    \;\Bigr],
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_basis_vector}
    \big(\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}\big)^T
    = \Bigl[\;
        \underbrace{
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{0}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\cdots\;
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{0}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{0}|}}
        }_{\activeDoFSet{k}{0}}
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{1}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\cdots\;
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{1}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{1}|}}
        }_{\activeDoFSet{k}{1}}
        \;\Big|\;
        \cdots
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\L}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_1}
            \;\cdots\;
            \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\L}{\L}{\coarseIdxone_{|\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}|}}
        }_{\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}}
    \;\Bigr].
\end{equation}
With the active subdivision basis $\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ chosen and the fine Whitney basis $\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}$ fixed, the form $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ admits two representations that must agree. Equating them and comparing coefficients in $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ defines the hierarchical subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_form_compact}
    \constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}
    = \big(\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}\big)^T \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}
    = \big(\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}\big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}.
\end{equation}
This implies $\big(\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}\big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} = \big(\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}\big)^T$, showing that the columns of $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ are the coefficients of the active subdivision basis functions in $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$. The level grouping of $\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}$ mirrors the block column structure of $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:hierarchical_matrix_blocks}
    \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}
    =
    \Bigl[
        \underbrace{\bigl(\Amat{k}{0}{\L}\bigr)_{\activeDoFSet{k}{0}}}_{\mathbb{R}^{|\K^k_\L| \times |\activeDoFSet{k}{0}|}}
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{\bigl(\Amat{k}{1}{\L}\bigr)_{\activeDoFSet{k}{1}}}_{\mathbb{R}^{|\K^k_\L| \times |\activeDoFSet{k}{1}|}}
        \;\Big|\;
        \cdots
        \;\Big|\;
        \underbrace{\bigl(\Amat{k}{\L}{\L}\bigr)_{\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}}}_{\mathbb{R}^{|\K^k_\L| \times |\activeDoFSet{k}{\L}|}}
    \Bigr],
\end{equation}
so that the block $(\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\l})^T$ multiplies precisely the $|\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}|$ columns of $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ originating from level $\l$, contributing $\sum_{\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \activeDoFSet{k}{\l}} \freeQ{c}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \, \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ to $\constrVec{\omega}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$.

In practice it is the $\big(\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}\big)^T \cdot \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ picture that drives the implementation, rather than the $\big(\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}\big)^T$ picture. A bilinear form with fine-level matrix $\mathbf{M}$ on $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ restricts to the hierarchical space as $(\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^T \mathbf{M}\, \Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$, so the discrete system is assembled in $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ via standard FEniCS routines and compressed by two sparse matrix multiplications. The fine Whitney basis $\freeVec{\FEECbases}{k}{\L}$ is handled implicitly by FEniCS and never appears explicitly in code.

The PDE is then solved for the hierarchical DoF vector $\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}$, which collects the coefficients of the solution in the active subdivision basis. Once $\freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}$ is known, the fine-level Whitney representation of the solution is recovered as $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \cdot \freeVec{\bar{c}}{k}{\activeDoFs}$. This matrix--vector product is the coordinate expression of the inclusion $\iota \colon \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L} \hookrightarrow \FEECspace{k}{\L}$, and the resulting fine-level vector can be used directly for post-processing, visualisation, and residual estimation on $\T_\L$.

The concrete task that remains is therefore constructing $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$, which reduces to choosing the active DoFs $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$. The remainder of this paper focuses on defining $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$ such that the resulting hierarchical spaces are linearly independent and preserve a discrete de Rham complex, and on assembling $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ from those choices.

From here the matrix picture is fixed. The object $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ is always a selection of columns from the hierarchy $\{\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}\}_\l$. The entire remaining question is therefore combinatorial: \emph{which} column selections are admissible, meaning they yield linearly independent $k$-form bases that span an exact complex. The rest of the paper characterises the admissible selections (Section~\ref{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces}), presents an adaptive mesh refinement and padding strategy that preserves admissibility (Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive_loop}), and verifies the resulting properties computationally (Section~\ref{sec:numerical_experiments}).

\section{Constructing hierarchical subdivision spaces}
\label{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces}

The general matrix representation of Section~\ref{subsec:computational_representations} is unchanged in the following. This section presents the selection of admissible active DoFs. We introduce refinement and derivative stencils as visual tools to understand linear independence and the inclusion of differentials of $k$-forms in the $k\!+\!1$-form space. The section ends by introducing subdivision partitions, an object that parameterises the DoF selection and enforces admissibility through geometric constraints regarding the decomposition of the domain mesh into patches of different subdivision levels.

\subsection{Refinement of basis functions}
\label{subsec:refinement_of_basis_functions}

\begin{proposition}[Proposition~3.13 from~\cite{Piel.2026}]
\label{prop:uniform_spaces_nested}
Let $\lzero \leq \l \leq \L$. For a fixed initial meshes $\T_{\l}$ for $\l \in \{\lzero, \hdots, \L\}$, the
corresponding subdivision $k$-form spaces $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}\big(\T_{\l} \big)$ are nested, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:nested_spaces}
    \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L} \subset  \SDFspace{k}{\l+1}{\L} \subset \hdots \subset \SDFspace{k}{\L}{\L} = \FEECspace{k}{\L},
\end{equation}
and have the dimensions $\dimlk{\l}{k} < \dimlk{\l +1}{k} < \hdots < \dimlk{\L}{k}$.
\end{proposition}

This proposition implies the refinement relation for the subdivision basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$. The resulting properties regarding the refinability of the basis functions form the backbone of the adaptive method presented in this paper. The following discussion is focused on basis functions on intermediate levels $\l$ with $\lzero \leq \l \leq \L$ defined as
\begin{equation}
    \label{equ:basisrel}
    \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \Aop{k}{\l}{\L} \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\l}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\l}{\L}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \; \freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \, ,
\end{equation}
where $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ is the subdivision matrix, cf.~\cite{Piel.2026}. Splitting the accumulated subdivision operator $\Aop{k}{\l}{\L}$ reveals the following relation between the basis functions $\constrVec{\Phi}{k}{\l}{\L}$ and $\constrVec{\Phi}{k}{\l + 1}{\L}$ on neighbouring levels.

\begin{lemma}[Refinement Relation, {\cite{Piel.2026}}]
    \label{lemma:hiermeshrel}
    Let $\l$ be an arbitrary level such that $\lzero \leq \l \leq \L$ and let $\Amat{k}{\l}{\l+1}$ be the subdivision matrix associated to $\Aop{k}{\l}{\l+1}$. Then, the basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ and $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ satisfy the following \emph{refinement relation}
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:refinement_relation}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum\limits_{\fineIdxone = 1}^{\sumdimlk{\l+1}{k}} \Amatij{k}{\l}{\l+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \; \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone} \; .
    \end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    See~\cite{Piel.2026}.
\end{proof}

We will capture relationships between different basis functions across the mesh hierarchy through \textit{stencils}. Since there is a one-to-one correspondence between basis functions and simplices, we will formulate these stencils as maps between (sets of) simplices across the hierarchy. Thus, formally, a stencil is a map $\powerset(\K^k_{\l}) \to \powerset\!\bigl(\bigsqcup_{\l=\lzero}^{\L}\bigsqcup_{k=0}^{2}\K^k_\l\bigr)$, where $\powerset$ denotes the powerset.

We choose to formulate stencils with respect to simplices instead of basis functions because that enables us to use the adjacency operator $\KmKnl{m}{n}{\l}$ and refinement operators $\KKltoL{k}{\l}{\lone}$ introduced in~\cite{Piel.2026}. The remainder of this section sets up refinement stencils based on the refinement relation~\eqref{eq:refinement_relation}, while the next section lays the foundations for the derivative stencil by investigating the relation of basis functions of different form degrees.

The hierarchical refinement relation in Lemma~\ref{lemma:hiermeshrel} provides a way to re-express a basis function $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ as a linear combination of finer basis functions. This idea of expressing coarse basis functions through finer basis functions is a core concept in this work. The following definition introduces the corresponding formalism called refinement stencils. After that, we highlight one particular known refinement stencil and then present a construction for more general refinement stencils.

\begin{definition}[Refinement Stencil]
    \label{def:deep_ref_stencil}
    Let $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ denote the basis function of $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ that is associated to the simplex $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$.
    A refinement stencil is a collection of simplices $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\fineIdxone}$ across the finer levels $\lone \in \{\l\!+\!1, \, \hdots, \L\}$ whose associated basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ can exactly re-express $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$. More concretely, a refinement stencil $\RSt\big(\simplex{k}{l}{\coarseIdxone}\big)$ is a map
    \begin{equation}
        \label{equ:refstencilunion}
        \RSt : \K^k_{\l} \to \powerset \Big(\bigcup_{\lone = \l+1}^{\L} \K^k_{\lone} \Big), \quad  \simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \mapsto \RSt \big( \simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \big)
    \end{equation}
    such that there exist coefficients $\constrQ{c}{k}{\lone}{\l}{\fineIdxone} \neq 0$ satisfying
    \begin{equation}
        \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\simplex{k}{\lone}{\fineIdxone} \in \RSt \big( \simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \big)} \constrQ{c}{k}{\robert{\lone}}{\robert{\l}}{\fineIdxone} \; \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\lone}{\L}{\fineIdxone}.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

The coefficients $\constrQ{c}{k}{\lone}{\l}{\fineIdxone}$ above are connected to the non-zero entries of the subdivision matrices. In fact, we can compute them by tracing back the contributions of certain fine basis functions to the coarse one analogously to the refinement relation~(\eqref{eq:refinement_relation}). The only difference is that the refinement stencil $\RSt \big( \simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \big)$ now contains simplices across different refinement levels, i.e. $\lone$ is not constant for $\simplex{k}{\lone}{\fineIdxone}$. We are only concerned with the existence of the coefficients $\constrQ{c}{k}{\lone}{\l}{\fineIdxone}$ because our goal is typically not to exactly re-express the coarse basis functions but to test wether they are in the space spanned by some finer basis functions. That is why we are going to formulate indicators for such linear dependence of coarse basis functions based on the existence of refinement stencils.

In the following, we will systematically construct refinement stencils by iterating the canonical refinement stencil defined next. According to Equation~\eqref{eq:refinement_relation}, all basis functions $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l+1}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ with non-zero entries $\Amatij{k}{\l}{\l+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0$ are required to re-express the coarse basis function $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$. The following definition formalises this selection of basis functions.

\begin{definition}[Canonical Refinement Stencil]
    Let $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ denote the basis function of $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ that is associated to the simplex $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$.     \begin{eqnarray}
        \cRSt: \K^k_{\l} \to \powerset\big( \K^k_{\l+1}\big), \qquad \simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \mapsto \Big\{ \simplex{k}{\l+1}{\fineIdxone} \in \K^k_{\l+1} \colon \; \Amatij{k}{\l}{\l+1}{\fineIdxone}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0 \Big\}.
    \end{eqnarray}
\end{definition}

We call this refinement stencil canonical because it is the only one that contains simplices solely on level $\l\!+\!1$ and is thus uniquely determined by the refinement relation~\eqref{eq:refinement_relation}. Figure~\ref{fig:refinement_stencils} visualises the canonical refinement stencils of $k$-form DoFs and shows that we can obtain the canonical refinement stencil by going to the adjacent faces of $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$, refining them, taking their finer vertices and finally all fine $k$-simplices adjacent to these vertices. \cite{companion_paper} shows that we can construct all meaningful refinement stencils $\RSt$ across the entire mesh hierarchy by recursively applying the canonical refinement stencil $\cRSt$.

To guarantee linear independence of the selected active basis functions, we need to ensure that every refinement stencil of every active DoF contains at least one inactive DoF. In this paper, the refinement stencil serves as visual indicator of linear independence and is a key motivation to define the subdivision partition in Section~\ref{subsec:selecting_active_dofs}.

\subsection{Derivatives of basis functions}
\label{subsec:derivatives_of_basis_functions}

We now set up the differential structure of the subdivision $k$-form spaces. First,~\cite{Piel.2026} shows that the uniform subdivision $k$-form spaces comprise the de Rham complex
\begin{equation}
\begin{tikzcd}
  \constrVec{\mathfrak{R}}{}{\l}{\L}\colon \quad 0 \arrow[r] & \SDFspace{0}{\l}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \SDFspace{1}{\l}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \SDFspace{2}{\l}{\L} \arrow[r] & 0.
\end{tikzcd}
\end{equation}
on all intermediate levels $\l$ with $\lzero \leq \l \leq \l \leq \L$. Throughout this work, we do not distinguish between $\extd : \FEECspace{k}{\l} \to \FEECspace{k+1}{\l}$ and $\auxextd : \SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L} \to \SDFspace{k+1}{\l}{\L}$ like in~\cite{Piel.2026}. We will denote both operators by $\extd$ because they largely coincide for our purposes.

The coordinate expression of the action of the exterior derivative $\extd$ on the spaces $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ follows directly from the coarse-level oriented incidence matrix $\Dmat{k}{\l}$~\citep{Hirani.2003,Desbrun.2006} by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:derivative_relation_basis}
    \extd \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} = \sum_{\coarseIdxtwo=1}^{\sumdimlk{\l}{k+1}} \Dmatij{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \, \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k+1}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxtwo}.
\end{equation}

Since $\Dmat{k}{\l}$ is the signed incidence matrix of $\T_\l$, only the $(k\!+\!1)$-simplices of $\T_\l$ adjacent to $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$ appear with non-zero coefficients. In the context of splines, this is analogous to forward-differencing, in particular for triangular splines like box splines~\citep{de_boor_box_1993}.

\begin{definition}[Derivative Stencil]
    \label{def:derivative_stencil}
    Let $\constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ be a basis function of $\SDFspace{k}{\l}{\L}$ associated with the $k$-simplex $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \K^k_{\l}$. The derivative stencil $\DSt$ is a map 
    \begin{equation}
        \DSt : \K^k_{\l} \to \powerset \big( \K^{k+1}_{\l} \big), \qquad \simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \; \mapsto \; \big\{ \simplex{k+1}{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo}: \; \Dmatij{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \neq 0 \big\}.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

The derivative stencil is the key concept to ensure that the derivative of every active $k$-form basis function can be expressed exactly in the $(k\!+\!1)$-form space and that the resulting spaces constitute a differential complex. For the Wang schemes~\citep{Wang.2006,Wang.2008}, Definition~\ref{def:derivative_stencil} implies that the derivative stencil of a $k$-form simplex $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$ is given by $\DSt\big(\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}\big) = \K^{k+1}\K^k_{\l}\big(\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone}\big)$, see~\cite{Piel.2026}. Figure~\ref{fig:derivative_stencils} visualises these refinement stencils.

To obtain a differential complex, we need to ensure that the derivative stencil of every active $k$-form basis function can be represented by active $k\!+\!1$-form basis functions of the next space in the complex, either because it is contained direcly or because it has an active refinement stencil. This property is a key constraint for the choice of active DoFs in Subsection~\ref{subsec:selecting_active_dofs}.

\subsection{Selection of active degrees of freedom}
\label{subsec:selecting_active_dofs}

Section~\ref{subsec:computational_representations} established that constructing the hierarchical subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ reduces to choosing, at each level $\l$, an index set $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l} \subset \K^k_\l$ of active simplices. Each such choice selects the corresponding columns of $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$, assembling $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ block by block as in~\eqref{eq:hierarchical_matrix_blocks}. This section addresses the question left open there: which choices of $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$ are admissible? Recall that collecting the active simplices across levels yields the active DoFs
\begin{equation}
    \label{def:activeDoFs}
    \activeDoFSet{k}{} \coloneq \bigcup_{\l=\lzero}^{\L} \;\, \activeDoFSet{k}{\l}.
\end{equation}

We now provide a generic definition of the adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces induced by an arbitrary set of active DoFs $\activeDoFSet{k}{}$.

\begin{definition}[Adaptive subdivision differential form spaces]
    \label{def:generic_adaptive_function_spaces}
    Fix an initial mesh $\T_{\lzero}$ with finest level $\L$ and select the active DoFs $\activeDoFSet{k}{}$. The \emph{active basis functions} of the \emph{adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces} $\activeSDFspaces{k}{}$ are given by
    \begin{equation}
        \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \coloneqq \bigcup_{\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \activeDoFSet{k}{}} \big\{ \constrQ{\SDFbasis}{k}{\l}{\L}{\coarseIdxone} \big\} \qquad \text{and thus} \qquad \activeSDFspaces{k}{} \coloneqq \mathrm{span}\big( \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \big).
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

Not every choice of active DoFs $\activeDoFSet{k}{}$ yields a well-behaved hierarchical spaces $\activeSDFspaces{k}{}$. The active DoFs $\activeDoFSet{k}{}$ must together represent forms that cover the entire domain, must be linearly independent, and should constitute a exact complex. The remainder of this section introduces the tools needed to parameterise the active DoFs $\activeDoFSet{k}{}$ and to reason about these properties of the adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces $\activeSDFspaces{k}{}$.

The requirements of linear indepence and expressing exterior differentials have a visual interpretation in terms of the refinement and derivative stencils introduced in the previous Sections~\ref{subsec:refinement_of_basis_functions} and~\ref{subsec:derivatives_of_basis_functions}. A selection of active basis functions is linearly independent when the active DoFs do not contain a refinement stencil for any individual active DoFs. Similarly, the spaces constitute a differential complex if the simplices in derivative stencil of any active basis function are themselves present in the active DoFs, either directly or through a refinement stencil.

\smallskip
A geometrically natural unit for organising the selection of active DoFs is a \emph{mesh patch} to gathers simplices that are all activated together. We consider such a patch $\calP_\l \subset \T_\l$ to be a non-empty collection of edge-connected faces of $\T_\l$. We denote the set of $k$-simplices of the patch $\calP_\l$ as $\K^k(\calP_\l)$. The boundary $\partial\calP_\l$ of the patch is the set of edges adjacent to exactly one face of $\calP_\l$. We write $\calP_{\l \to \lone}$ for the patch faces obtained by repeatedly splitting the faces of $\calP_\l$ from level $\l$ to $\lone$. Two patches $\calP_{\l}$ and $\calP_{\lone}$ are \emph{non-overlapping} if their refinements to level $\L$ share at most edges, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \I_{(\calP_{\l}, \calP_{\lone}) \to \L} \coloneqq \calP_{\l \to \L} \, \cap \, \calP_{\lone \to \L} = \partial\calP_{\l \to \L} \, \cup \, \partial\calP_{\lone \to \L} \;\; \subset \E_\L(\T_\L).
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[covering]
    \label{def:covering}
    A \emph{covering} $\C(\T_\L) = \bigcup_{\l = \lzero}^\L \bigcup_{n = 1}^{\mathrm{N_{\l}}} \calP^n_{\l}$ is a set of pairwise non-overlapping patches $\calP^n_{\l}$ that tile $\T_\L$ such that $\T_\L = \bigcup_{\l = \lzero}^\L \bigcup_{n = 1}^{\mathrm{N_{\l}}} \calP^n_{\l \to \L}$, where $\mathrm{N_{\l}}$ is the number of patches on level $\l$. We further denote certain level subsets of a covering by
    \begin{equation}
        \C_{\l} = \bigcup\limits_{n = 1}^{\mathrm{N}_\l} \calP^n_{\l \to \L}, \quad \C_{>\lone} = \bigcup\limits_{\l = \lone + 1}^{\L} \C_\l, \quad \C_{\geq\lone} = \bigcup\limits_{\l = \lone }^{\L} \C_\l, \quad \C_{<\lone} = \bigcup\limits_{\l = \lzero }^{\lone - 1} \C_\l, \quad \C_{\leq\lone} = \bigcup\limits_{\l = \lzero }^{\lone} \C_\l.
    \end{equation}
    The \emph{boundary} $\partial \C$ of a covering $\C$ is given by $\partial \C \coloneq \bigcup_{\l = \lzero}^{\L} \partial \C_\l$, where $\partial \C_\l \coloneq \C_\l \cap \partial \T_\l$.
\end{definition}

We refer to the set $\K^k(\C) \coloneq \bigcup_{\l=\lzero}^{\L} \K^k(\C_\l)$ as the \emph{patch DoFs}.
Thus, spanning an adaptive space using the patch DoFs of covering $\C$ ensures that the entire domain is covered with active basis functions. It does not yet guarantee either linear independece or a differential complex. In the following, we will develop an intuition for the selection of additional active degrees of freedom and constraints regarding the covering.

\smallskip

Consider the covering $\C(\T_\L) = \calP^1_0 \cup \calP^1_1 \cup \calP^1_2$ with $\L \geq 2$ shown at the top of Figure~\ref{fig:active_dofs}. The patch DoFs are going to be active for any form degree $k$ and level $\l$. Note that the derivative stencils of interface vertices on level $2$ take us outside of the patch edges, meaning that their derivatives cannot be represented in the current space that contains only patch DoFs. Since we already are on the finest level of the covering, we cannot represent the missing edge DoFs through their refinement stencils. Thus, the only option is to activate these edge DoFs incident to the interface vertices on level $2$. The derivative stencil of these additional active edge DoFs now takes us outside of the patch faces on level $2$. Again, the only option is to add the interface faces on level $2$. Row $\l=2$ of Figure~\ref{fig:active_dofs} shows exactly the active DoFs resulting from this extention around the interfaces of the patch $\calP^1_2$.

The same logic becomes a little more nuanced for the patch $\calP^1_1$ on level $\l=1$. We need to extend the patch DoFs again to be able to represent all derivates. However, if the additional active edge and face DoFs overlap the fine patch $\calP^1_2$, the interface edges and faces of level $\l=2$ make the ones of level $\l=1$ interface linearly dependent. The interface DoFs that overlap the coarser patch $\calP^1_0$ are not linearly dependent. Row $\l=1$ in Figure~\ref{fig:active_dofs} shows that we only add interface DoFs on level $\l=1$ if they overlap the coarser patch. Row $\l=0$ in Figure~\ref{fig:active_dofs} also reveals that we do not need to add any interface DoFs for the coarsest patch $\calP^1_0$ because they are all linearly dependent after adding the interface DoFs of all finer patches. The extra DoFs on level $\l=1$ that are at the interface between the patches at level $\l=0$ and $\l=2$ are also a result of the need to express derivatives of $k$-forms in the $k\!+\!1$-form spaces.

\smallskip

This selection mechanism of alternating refinement and derivative stencils generalises to the following definition of the active DoFs of a covering $\C$ and lets us define the adaptive subdivision differential form spaces.

\begin{definition}[Active $k$-form degrees of freedom]
    \label{def:active_k_form_dofs}
    Let $\C (\T_{\L} ) = \bigcup_{\l = \lzero}^\L \; \bigcup_{n = 0}^{\mathrm{N}_{\l}} \; \calP^n_{\l}$ be a covering of $\T_L$. The active $k$-form degrees of freedom $\activeDoFSet{k}{} (\C)$ are defined as
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:active_k_form_dofs}
        \activeDoFSet{k}{} \big(\C\big) \coloneqq \underbrace{\bigcup\limits_{\calP^n_{\l} \in \C}}_{(i)} \bigg[ \underbrace{\K^k \big( \calP^n_{\l} \big)}_{(ii)} \;\; \cup \;\; \underbrace{\bigcup\limits_{\calP^m_{\ltwo} \in \C_{> \l}}}_{(iii)} \;\; \underbrace{\bigcup\limits_{\lone = \l+1}^{\ltwo}}_{(iv)} \; \Big[ \underbrace{\K^k\V_{\lone} \Big( \V_{\lone} \big( \I_{(\calP^n_{\l \to \lone}, \calP^m_{\ltwo})} \big) \Big)}_{(v)} \cap \underbrace{\K^k_{\lone} \big( \calP^n_{\l \to \lone} \big)}_{(vi)} \Big] \; \bigg].
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

Unpacking this definition shows: for every patch (\textit{i}) $\calP^n_{\l}$, we first add all of its patch DoFs (\textit{ii}). Further, for all relatively finer patches (\textit{iii}) $\calP^m_{\ltwo}$ with $\ltwo > \l$ and all intermediate levels (\textit{iv}) $\lone$ with $\l < \lone \leq \ltwo$, all interface simplices (\textit{v}) are added if they lie on the coarser side (\textit{vi}) of the interface, i.e. can be generated by refining patch $\calP^n_{\l}$ to level $\lone$.

\begin{definition}[Adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces induced by a covering]
    \label{def:adaptive_function_spaces}
    The adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ and $\hierSDFspaceZero{k}{\C}{\L}$ (with vanishing trace) induced by the covering $\C(\T_\L)$ are given by
    \begin{equation}
        \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L} \coloneqq \mathrm{span}\big( \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs(\C)}{\L} \big) \qquad \text{and} \qquad \hierSDFspaceZero{k}{\C}{\L} \coloneqq \mathrm{span}\big( \constrVec{\mathring{\SDFbases}}{k}{\activeDoFs(\C)}{\L} \big),
    \end{equation}
    where the active basis functions $\constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{\activeDoFs(\C)}{\L}$ and $\constrVec{\mathring{\SDFbases}}{k}{\activeDoFs(\C)}{\L} \coloneq \constrVec{\SDFbases}{k}{(\activeDoFs(\C) \setminus \K^k(\partial \C))}{\L}$ are constructed as in Definition~\ref{def:generic_adaptive_function_spaces}.
\end{definition}

The selection of the active DoFs in Definition~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs} guarantees that the adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ form a differential complex~\citep{companion_paper}, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:differential_complex}
    \extd: \; \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L} \to \hierSDFspace{k+1}{\C}{\L} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \extd \circ \extd: \; \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L} = 0.
\end{equation}

This is also true for the spaces $\hierSDFspaceZero{k}{\C}{\L}$ with vanishing traces.

The other essential properties of the adaptive $k$-form subdivision spaces, namely linear independence and correct cohomology of the discrete complex, are tied to the geometry of the patches that constitute the covering, see~\cite{companion_paper}. This will be the subject of the following Section~\ref{subsec:subdiv_partition}.

\subsection{Operations on coverings}

This section develops the necessary concepts to describe the two particular configurations that compromise linear independence and the cohomology of the complex. These foundations enable us to introduce the geometric predicates of pinch vertices and inadmissible faces in Section~\ref{subsec:subdiv_partition} which allow us to constrain the geometry of coverings and indicate the need for padding in the adaptive loop in Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive_loop}.

\smallskip

The first concept we are going to formalise is the persistence of vertices across the mesh hierarchy $\T_\l$ for $\l = \lzero, \, \dots, \, \L$. The important observation is that, once a vertex first appears on level $\l$, the vertex remains part of the hierarchy throughout all iterations of the subdivision algorithm, only its position can shift slightly. We capture this equivalence of vertices by defining the equivalence classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ as follows.

\begin{definition}[Vertex equivalence class]
    \label{def:vertex_classed}
    Fix a mesh hierarchy $\T_\l$ for $\l = \lzero, \, \dots, \, \L$. The \emph{vertex equivalence classes} $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ are defined by
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:def_vertex_class}
        \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \coloneq \big\{ \vertex{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo}, \; \vertex{\l \to \l+1}{\fineIdxone}, \; \dots, \; \vertex{\l \to \L}{\coarseIdxtwo} \big\},
\end{equation}
    where $\l = \min \big\{ \, \lone \in \{\lzero, \, \dots, \, \L\} \; \colon \; \coarseIdxtwo \leq \vert \V(\T_\lone) \vert \, \big\}$ and $\vertex{\l \to \lone}{\coarseIdxtwo} = \Aop{}{\l}{\lone} \vertex{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo}$ denotes refined vertices.
\end{definition}

We chose the $\widehat{\cdot}$ notation because we think of the equivalence relation as lifting the vertex $\vertex{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo}$ across all finer levels. The number of vertex equivalence classes of a whole mesh hierarchy ending with $\T_\L$ is $\vert \T_\L \vert$. Most of these equivalence classes will not appear as vertices of a covering $\C(\T_\L)$. This implies that the set
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:def_vertex_classes_of_covering}
    \widehat{\V}_\C \coloneq \big\{ \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \; \colon \; \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \cap \V(\C) \neq \varnothing \big\}
\end{equation}
of vertex equivalence classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ of the covering $\C(\T_\L)$ is more practically relevant. We find that $\vert \widehat{\V}_\C \vert \leq \vert \V(\C) \vert$ since every equivalence class $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \widehat{\V}_\C$ contains at least one vertex of $\C$.

Finally, we define the \emph{source level} $\sourcelevel_\coarseIdxtwo$ and \emph{target level} $\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo$ of a vertex equivalence class $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \widehat{\V}_\C$ by
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:def_sourcelevel}
    \sourcelevel_\coarseIdxtwo &\coloneq \min \, \big\{ \l \in \{\lzero, \, \dots, \, \L\} \; \colon \; \vertex{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \cap \V(\C) \big\}, \\
    \label{eq:def_targetlevel}
    \targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo &\coloneq \max \big\{ \l \in \{\lzero, \, \dots, \, \L\} \; \colon \; \vertex{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \cap \V(\C) \big\}.
\end{align}
The reason we call those levels source and target level is due to the effect of the quadrisection operator introduced later in Definition~\ref{def:vertex_quadrisection_operator}.

For example, consider the covering in Figure~\ref{fig:active_dofs} again. The vertex equivalence class $\vertclass{c} = \{ \vertex{0}{c}, \, \vertex{1}{c}, \, \vertex{2}{c}\}$ with $\sourcelevel_c = 0$ and $\targetlevel_c = 2$ corresponds to the center vertex of the covering at the intersection of all interfaces. The other interface vertices are in vertex classes with two elements, while the remaining vertiex equivalence classes are singletons.

\smallskip

The next concept we need to introduce is a notion of an inverse face quadrisection. Applying this $\emph{ancestor}$ operator to a fine face returns the coarse face of the covering $\C$ that covers the fine face.
\begin{definition}[Ancestor operator]
    \label{def:ancestor_operator}
    Let $\C$ be a covering. The \emph{ancestor} operator $\ancestor{\cdot}$ is a map $\ancestor{\cdot}: \F(\T_\lone) \to \F(\C)$ defined by the relation
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:def_ancestor}
        \face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} = \ancestor{\face{\lone}{\fineIdxone}} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \face{\lone}{\fineIdxone} \in \Aop{}{\l}{\lone} \big( \face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \big),
    \end{equation}
    Note that the ancestor operator is only well-defined if $\l \leq \lone \leq \L$.
\end{definition}

\smallskip

Combining the vertex equivalence classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ and the ancestor operator $\ancestor{\cdot}$ allows us to define the \emph{covering star} $\coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo})$ of a $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$. This operator returns all faces of a covering $\C$ that ``surround'' the vertex class $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$, see Figure~\ref{fig:covering_star}.
\begin{definition}[Covering star]
    \label{def:covering_star}
    Let $\C$ be a covering with vertex equivalence classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \widehat{\V}_\C$. The \emph{covering star} $\coveringStar\; \colon \; \widehat{\V}_\C \to \powerset\big( \F(\C) \big)$ is defined by
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:def_partition_star}
        \coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}) \coloneq \Big\{ \, \ancestor{\face{\L}{\fineIdxone}} \; : \; \face{\L}{\fineIdxone} \in \FVl{\L} \big( \vertex{\L}{\coarseIdxtwo} \big) \, \Big\}.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

The covering star operator $\coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo})$ collects \emph{every} active face of the covering $\C$ incident to the vertex. This includes not only the faces of which the vertex is an corner, but also the coarse face lying across an interface edge through the vertex. We denote the set of active faces of the covering star that are contained in the covering on level $\l$ by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:def_partition_star_slice}
    \coveringStarl{\l}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}) \coloneq \coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}) \cap \F(\C_\l).
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Covering-interior vertex at level $\l$]
    \label{def:interior_vertex}
    We call a vertex $\vertex{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \V(\C)$ \emph{interior at level $\l$} if the covering star $\coveringStar{\C}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo})$ of its equivalence class $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \widehat{\V}_\C$ does not contain any face on a level finer than $\l$, i.e.,
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:interior_vertex}
        \coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}) \cap \F(\C_{>\l}) = \varnothing.
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

This definition implies that a vertex class is covering-interior at level $\lone > \l$ if it is covering-interior at level $\l$.

Note that the set of covering-interior vertices of $\C$ does not only contain the interior vertices of the patches of $\C$ but can also contain interface vertices and vertices on the boundary $\partial \C$. For example, Figure~\ref{fig:covering_interior_vertices} displays a covering with nested refinement up to level $3$ towards its center. The vertices are colored by the level at which they become covering-interior. The outermost layer is covering-interior from level $0$ because its covering star contains only faces on level $0$. The next layer is covering-interior from level $1$ because the covering stars of its vertex classes contains only faces on level $0$ and $1$. This logic applies to all layers in the figure until we reach the finest level $3$, at which all the remaining vertex classes become covering-interior.

\smallskip

The remainder of this section introduces operators that enable the kinds of refinement that we will later need for the adaptive loop in Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive_loop}. The \emph{face quadrisection operator} $\quadriF{\C}(\simplexsubset{2}{\l})$ refines a \emph{single-level} set of faces $\simplexsubset{2}{\l} \subseteq \F(\C_\l)$ to level $\l+1$ by replacing each face with its four children, that is, it removes the faces $\simplexsubset{2}{\l}$ from level $\l$ and adds their children on level $\l+1$, leaving all other levels untouched.

\begin{definition}[Face quadrisection operator]
    \label{def:face_quadrisection_operator}
    Let $\C$ be a covering and choose a set $\simplexsubset{2}{\l} \subseteq \F(\C_\l)$. The \emph{face quadrisection operator} 
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:def_face_quadrisection_operator}
        \quadriF{\C}(\simplexsubset{2}{\l}) \coloneq \C_{<\l} \; \cup \; \big\{ \C_{\l} \setminus \simplexsubset{2}{\l} \big\} \; \cup \; \big\{ \C_{\l + 1} \cup \Aop{}{\l}{\l + 1} \big(\simplexsubset{2}{\l}\big) \big\} \; \cup \; \C_{>\l + 1};
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

In contrast to the that, the \emph{vertex quadrisection operator} $\quadriV{\C}{\targetlevel}(\coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}))$ refines a whole covering star by nested face quadrisections from the source level $\sourcelevel_\coarseIdxtwo$ to the target level $\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo$.

\begin{definition}[Vertex quadrisection operator]
    \label{def:vertex_quadrisection_operator}
    Let $\C$ be a covering and $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \mathrm{V}_\C$ be a vertex class with source level $\sourcelevel_\coarseIdxtwo$. Choose a target level $\targetlevel$ such that $\sourcelevel_\coarseIdxtwo \leq \targetlevel \leq \L$. The \emph{vertex quadrisection operator} $\quadriV{\C}{\targetlevel}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo})$ is constructed iteratively by:
    \begin{enumerate}[topsep=3pt, itemsep=-2pt]%[nolistsep]
        \item initialize $\C^{\sourcelevel\coarseIdxtwo} = \C$,
        \item for $\l = \sourcelevel\coarseIdxtwo, \, \dots, \, \targetlevel - 1$, carry out flat quadrisection of the coarsest faces in the covering star $\coveringStarCl{\C^\l}{\l}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo})$ of $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ in the covering $\C^\l$, i.e.
        \begin{equation}
            \label{eq:vertex_quadrisection}
            \C^{\l+1} = \quadriF{\C^\l}\big( \coveringStarCl{\C^\l}{\l}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}) \big),
        \end{equation}
        \vspace{-7mm}
        \item set $\quadriV{\C}{\targetlevel}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}) \coloneq \C^{\targetlevel}$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The quadrisection operators enable consistent refinement of the adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ in the sense that
\begin{equation}
    \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L} \subseteq \hierSDFspace{k}{\quadriF{\C}(\simplexsubset{2}{\l})}{\L} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L} \subseteq \hierSDFspace{k}{\quadriV{\C}{\targetlevel}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo})}{\L}.
\end{equation}
The quadrisection operators defined in this section are the basis upon which we will later built the algorithms in the adaptive loop in Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive_loop}.

\subsection{Subdivision partitions and properties of the adaptive spaces}
\label{subsec:subdiv_partition}

We are now ready to define the first geometric predicate of face admissibility.

\begin{definition}[admissible face]
    \label{def:admissible_face}
    We call a face $\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \F(\C)$ \emph{admissible} if at least one of its vertices $\vertex{\l}{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \V\F\big( \face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \big)$ is covering-interior at level $\l$. Otherwise, the face is called \emph{inadmissible}.
\end{definition}

Figure~\ref{fig:inadmissible_faces} shows a covering whose faces on level $1$ are all admissible while the faces on level $0$ (purple) are inadmissible. As we will see in Section~\ref{subsec:exactness_atomic}, the adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces on this covering have some interesting properties: There are $3$ linearly dependent active vertex DoFs, $18$ linearly dependent active edge DoFs and $15$ linearly dependent active face DoFs. At the same time, the spaces on this covering have the correct cohomology of $\mathrm{dim}(\mathcal{H}^0) = 1$ and $\mathrm{dim}(\mathcal{H}^1) = \mathrm{dim}(\mathcal{H}^2) = 0$.

\smallskip

The second predicate is that of a pinch vertex.

\begin{definition}[pinch vertex]
    \label{def:pinch_vertex}
    Let $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \widehat{\V}_\C$ be a vertex equivalence class of $\C$ with target level $\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo$ and compute the number
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm{N}_\coarseIdxtwo \coloneq \Big\vert \big\{ \edge{\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone} \in \E\V(\vertex{\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxtwo}) \; : \; \F\E(\edge{\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone}) \cap \C_{\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo} \neq \varnothing \;\;\; \text{and} \;\;\; \F\E(\edge{\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone}) \setminus \C_{\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo} \neq \varnothing \big\} \Big\vert
    \end{equation}
    of edges whose adjacent faces split into an active and an inactive face on level $\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo$, see Figure~\ref{fig:pinches}. If $\edge{\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo}{\coarseIdxone}$ is a boundary edge in $\E(\partial\T_{\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo})$, we always count it as having at least one inactive adjacent face DoF to represent the exterior of $\T_{\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo}$. The number $\mathrm{N}_\coarseIdxtwo$ will then always be even. \emph{number of disconnected arcs} $\gamma_\coarseIdxtwo$ of the vertex class $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ is given by
    \begin{equation}
        \gamma_\coarseIdxtwo = \begin{cases}
            1, \quad & \text{if} \;\; \mathrm{N}_\coarseIdxtwo = 0, \\
            \tfrac{1}{2} \mathrm{N}_\coarseIdxtwo, \quad & \text{otherwise}.
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
    If $\gamma_\coarseIdxtwo \geq 2$, the vertex class $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ is a called \emph{pinch} vertex.
\end{definition}

The two predicates allow us to define \emph{subdivision partitions} as a subset of coverings.

\begin{definition}[Subdivision Partition]
    \label{def:subdiv_partition}
    A covering $\C(\T_\L)$ is called a \emph{subdivision partition} if all of its faces $\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \F(\C)$ are admissible and none of its vertex classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \widehat{\V}_\C$ are pinch vertices.
\end{definition}

Finally, we are going to re-state the two main theorems of \cite{companion_paper} regarding linear independence and the exactness of the complex.

\begin{theorem}
    Let $\C$ be a subdivision partition of the mesh $\T_\L$. The active basis functions selected by Def.~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs} of the adaptive spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ are linearly independent.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    Linear dependencies only occur at inadmissible faces but a subdivision partition does not contain any inadmissible faces. See \cite{companion_paper} for the entire proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}
    Let $\C$ be a covering of the mesh $\T_\L$. The adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ constitute the differential complex
    \begin{equation}
    \begin{tikzcd}
        \mathfrak{R}_\C: \qquad 0 \arrow[r] & \mathbb{R} \arrow[r, "\subset"] & \hierSDFspace{0}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \hierSDFspace{1}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \hierSDFspace{2}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r] & 0.
    \end{tikzcd}
    \end{equation}
    The adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces with vanishing traces form the differential complex
    \begin{equation}
    \begin{tikzcd}
        \mathring{\mathfrak{R}}_\C: \qquad 0 \arrow[r] & \hierSDFspace{0}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \hierSDFspace{1}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\extd"] & \hierSDFspace{2}{\C}{\L} \arrow[r, "\int"] & \mathbb{R} \arrow[r] & 0.
    \end{tikzcd}
    \end{equation}
    If $\C$ is a \robert{subdivision partition of a \emph{simply-connected} domain}, the complexes $\mathfrak{R}_\C$ and $\mathring{\mathfrak{R}}_\C$ are exact.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    See \cite{companion_paper}.
\end{proof}

\section{Implementation and adaptive loop}

This section turns the constructions of Section~\ref{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces} into a working adaptive algorithm. Section~\ref{subsec:implementation_active_dofs} shows how the active DoF selection and the adaptive subdivision matrices $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ are assembled in practice. Section~\ref{subsec:adaptive_loop} then embeds this machinery in a standard \textsc{Solve}--\textsc{Estimate}--\textsc{Mark}--\textsc{Refine}--\textsc{Pad} adaptive loop, where the additional \textsc{Pad} restores the covering output by \textsc{Refine} to a subdivision partition (Definition~\ref{def:subdiv_partition}) after each refinement via pinch resolution, admissibility padding, and grading.

\subsection{Assembling the hierarchical subdivision matrix}
\label{subsec:implementation_active_dofs}

The implementation of the active subdivision spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ proceeds in two stages. 
Given a subdivision partition $\C$, we first identify the active degrees of
freedom $\D^k$ for each form degree $k \in \{0,1,2\}$ 
using Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildactivedofs} in the appendix. Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildsubdivmatrix} then assembles, for each form
degree $k$, the sparse matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ whose columns are
the level-$\L$ coefficient expansions of the active basis functions $\Phi_{\D^k (\C)}$, see Equations~\eqref{eq:hierarchical_form_compact} and~\eqref{eq:hierarchical_matrix_blocks}. Galerkin projection of all
bilinear forms to the active space then reduces to standard sparse
matrix-matrix products, without any re-assembly of integrals, see for example Equation~\eqref{eq:FE_matrix_assembly}.

The active DoFs split can be split into two contributions at each level $\l$.
\emph{Patch} DoFs are the $k$-simplices of every active face $\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \C$. \emph{Interface} DoFs are additional basis functions at the
boundary between patches on different levels. This grouping of $\D^k(\C)$ by levels is achieved by the following lemma.

\begin{lemma}[Splitting active DoFs by level, cf.~\cite{companion_paper} for proof.]
    \label{lemma:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}
    The active degrees of freedom $\D^k(\C)$ from Definition~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs} can equivalently be described by
    \begin{equation}
        \D^k \big( \C \big) = \bigcup_{\l = \lzero}^\L \; \D^k_{\l} \big( \C \big)
    \end{equation}
    with
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}
        \D^k_{\l} \big( \C \big) = \K^k_{\l} \big(\C_{\l}\big) \cup \bigcup\limits_{\calP^n_{\lone} \in \C_{<\l}} \; \bigcup\limits_{\calP^m_{\ltwo} \in \C_{\geq \l}} \; \Big[ \K^k\V_{\l} \Big( \V_{\l} \big(\I_{(\calP^n_{\lone \to \l}, \, \calP^m_{\ltwo})} \big) \Big) \cap \K^k_{\l} \big(\calP^n_{\lone \to \l}\big) \Big]
    \end{equation}
    for $\l = \lzero, \, \dots, \, \L$.
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lemma:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}
is the basis of the active-DoF assembly, given in full as
Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildactivedofs} in Appendix~\ref{app:active_dof_pseudocode}.
The algorithm is indexed by
level $\l = 0, \, \dots, \, \L$, mirroring $\D^k(\C) = \bigcup_{\l_t} \D^k_{\l_t}(\C)$,
and its two stages realise the two terms of Equation~\eqref{eq:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}. By contrast, the
patch-pair indexing of Definition~\ref{def:active_k_form_dofs} would translate
into a doubly-nested loop over patch pairs, which is not what we implement.
The native DoFs are read off directly from the active faces at each level,
while the interface DoFs are produced by a frontier sweep that maintains the
set of interior edges separating resolved (coarse-side) faces from
unresolved (finer) regions.

Once the active DoFs $\D^k$ have been identified, the adaptive subdivision matrix
$\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ is assembled column by column. According to Equation~\eqref{eq:hierarchical_matrix_blocks}, Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildsubdivmatrix} extracts the columns of $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$ corresponding to the active DoFs $\activeDoFSet{k}{\l}$ on level $\l$.

\robert{\begin{algorithm}[tb]
\caption{$\textsc{BuildSubdivMatrix}(\D, k)$: adaptive subdivision matrix}
\label{alg:buildsubdivmatrix}
\KwIn{active DoFs $\D$, form degree $k$, uniform subdivision matrices $\Amat{k}{\l}{\L}$}
\KwOut{adaptive subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$}
\BlankLine
$\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L} \leftarrow (\vert \K^k_\L \vert \times \vert \D^k \vert)$ sparse zero matrix\;
$\mathrm{col} \leftarrow 0$\;
\For{$\l = 0, \, \dots, \, \L$}{
    \For{each simplex $\simplex{k}{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \D^k_\l$}{
        $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}[\,:\,,\mathrm{col}] \leftarrow \Amat{k}{\l}{\L}[\,:\,, \coarseIdxone]$\;
        $\mathrm{col} \leftarrow \mathrm{col} + 1$\;
    }
}
\Return $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$\;
\end{algorithm}}

The adaptive subdivision matrices enter the overall adaptive loop during the \emph{unrefinement} in the \textsc{Solve} step in Section~\ref{subsec:solve}. The unrefinement gathers the contributions of the FEEC basis functions $\freeQ{\FEECbasis}{k}{\L}{\fineIdxone}$ of the spaces $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ to $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ using the adaptive subdivision matrices $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$, see Example~\ref{ex:matrix_assembly} and~\cite{Piel.2026} for more details.

\subsection{The adaptive loop}
\label{subsec:adaptive_loop}

This section describes the adaptive loop used in the numerical experiments. The loop consists of the steps
\begin{equation}
\begin{tikzcd}
    \textsc{Solve} \arrow[r] & \textsc{Estimate} \arrow[r] & \textsc{Mark} \arrow[r] & \textsc{Refine} \arrow[r] & \textsc{Pad} \arrow[llll, rounded corners=0pt, to path={(\tikztostart.south) -- ([yshift=-1.em]\tikztostart.south) -- ([yshift=-1.em]\tikztotarget.south) -- (\tikztotarget.south)}]
\end{tikzcd}
\end{equation}

and is initialized with a subdivision partition $\C^0$. Throughout the adaptive loop, the general picture of the computational objects in Section~\ref{subsec:computational_representations} stays the same, the loop only refines $\C$ iteratively, and with that the active DoFs $\activeDoFSet{k}{}(\C)$ and the adaptive subdivision $k$-form spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$. The \textsc{Pad} step is necessary because the \textsc{Refine} step produces only a covering, not a subdivision partition, see Definition~\ref{def:subdiv_partition}. This means that the \textsc{Pad} step effectively restores linear independence and exactness of the complex.

\subsubsection{Solve}
\label{subsec:solve}

The details of the \textsc{Solve} step are typically problem-specific. The common thread for methods using subdivision differential forms is that the bilinear forms that constitute the weak formulation are first assembled using the spaces $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$ on $\T_\L$ and then unrefined to the spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ using the adaptive subdivision matrices $\Amat{k}{\C}{\L}$, see~\cite{Piel.2026}. The resulting linear system then needs to be solved by an appropriate solver. The following example spells out the single steps for the vector Laplace test case from Section~\ref{subsec:vector_laplace}.

\begin{example}
    \label{ex:matrix_assembly}
    This example describes the $\textsc{Solve}$ step for the vector Laplace equation from Section~\ref{subsec:vector_laplace}. We use a standard finite elements library like FEniCS \citep{Logg.2012} to assemble the $0$-form mass matrix $\mathbf{M}_\L$ and the gradient-coupling matrix $\mathbf{B}_\L$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:mixed_laplacian1} and the curl--curl matrix $\mathbf{C}_\L$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:mixed_laplacian2} over the FEEC spaces $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$.

    Unrefining these matrices using the adaptive subdivision $k$-form matrices $\Amat{k}{\C}{\L}$ yields the matrices
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:FE_matrix_assembly}
        \mathbf{M}_\C = (\Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top \; \cdot \; \mathbf{M}_\L \; \cdot \; \Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L},
        \quad
        \mathbf{C}_\C = (\Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top \; \cdot \; \mathbf{C}_\L \; \cdot \; \Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L},
        \quad
        \mathbf{B}_\C = (\Amat{0}{\activeDoFs}{\L})^\top \; \cdot \; \mathbf{B}_\L \; \cdot \; \Amat{1}{\activeDoFs}{\L}
    \end{equation}
    over the spaces $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ or $\hierSDFspaceZero{k}{\C}{\L}$. The right-hand side of Equation~\eqref{eq:mixed_laplacian2} is $f_\C = (\Amat{1}{\C}{\L})^\top \cdot f_\L$.

The mixed vector Laplace problem can then be assembled into the block matrix system
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mixed_block_system}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        -\mathbf{M}_\C & \mathbf{B}_\C \\
        \mathbf{B}_\C^\top & \mathbf{C}_\C
    \end{bmatrix} \; \cdot \; \begin{bmatrix}
        \sigma_\C \\
        u_\C
    \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
        0 \\
        f_\C
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
This system is symmetric indefinite, so we use \emph{MINRES} with a block-diagonal SPD preconditioner. Section~\ref{subsec:vector_laplace} confirms that the number of iterations stays flat over the iterations over the adaptive loop.
\end{example}

\subsubsection{Estimation}
\label{subsec:estimate}

We compute error indicators $\eta_{\l,\coarseIdxone}$ per face $\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \C$ of the covering $\C$. The residual is evaluated on the finest level $\L$ and then aggregated to the coarse parent faces:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:project_estimator_on_L_to_partition}
    \eta^2_{\l,\coarseIdxone} = \sum\limits_{\face{\L}{\fineIdxone} \; \in \; \Aop{}{\l}{\L}(\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone})} \eta^2_{\L,\fineIdxone},
\end{equation}
where $\eta^2_{\L,\fineIdxone}$ is a standard FEEC residual we can evaluate in $\FEECspace{k}{\L}$. Note the residuals are only computed for faces in the covering and the interface faces are excluded to avoid overlap and double-counting. In the following, we will refer to $\eta^2$ and $\rho^2$ as the \emph{total} and \emph{refinable} residuals, respectively, defined as
\begin{equation}
    \label{def:total_and_refinable_residuals}
    \eta^2 = \sum\limits_{\l=\lzero}^{\L} \; \eta^2_\l \qquad \text{and} \qquad \rho^2 = \sum\limits_{\l=\lzero}^{\L-1} \; \eta^2_\l = \eta^2 - \eta^2_{\L} \qquad \text{with} \qquad \eta^2_{\l} = \sum\limits_{\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \; \in \; \F(\C_\l)} \eta^2_{\l,\coarseIdxone}.
\end{equation}
The refinable residual discards the contributions of faces $\face{\L}{\coarseIdxone}$ on level $\L$ of the subdivision partition $\C$. We measure this refinable residual because faces cannot be refined beyond level $\L$ without global mesh refinement to extend the mesh hierarchy. For this reason, the residual accumulated on level $\L$ can no longer be decreased. In fact, we find that
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:saturation_rate}
    \rho^2 / \eta^2 = 1 - \eta^2_\L / \eta^2 \eqqcolon \xi
\end{equation}
This \emph{saturation rate} $\xi \in [0,1]$ provides a good analytic tool and a concrete stopping criterion for the adaptive loop.

\begin{example}[Vector Laplace estimator]
For the mixed vector Laplace problem with $(\sigma_h, u_h) \in \hierSDFspace{0}{\C}{\L} \times \hierSDFspace{1}{\C}{\L}$ from Section~\ref{subsec:vector_laplace}, the basis functions are piecewise linear approximations of the subdivision limit functions. Hence $\extd (\iota u_h)$ is piecewise constant in $\FEECspace{2}{\L}$ and $\boldsymbol{\delta}\extd (\iota u_h)$ vanishes elementwise, while its contribution appears in the curl jumps across edges. In $\FEECspace{2}{\L}$, the indicator takes the form
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \eta_{\L,\fineIdxone}^2 = \quad &\sum\limits_{\face{\L}{\coarseIdxone} \,\in\, \F_\L} \!\! \robert{h_{\face{\L}{\fineIdxone}}^2} \big( \|f - \extd \sigma_h\|_{L^2(\face{\L}{\fineIdxone})}^2 + \|\sigma_h + \boldsymbol{\delta} u_h\|_{L^2(\face{\L}{\fineIdxone})}^2 \big) \\
    + &\sum\limits_{\edge{\L}{\fineIdxtwo} \, \in \, \E_\L} \robert{h_e} \big\|[\![\mathrm{curl}(u_h)]\!]\big\|_{L^2(\edge{\L}{\fineIdxtwo})}^2.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The first sum contains the elementwise residuals of both equations, and the second sum contains the curl jump across interior edges $e$ of the fine mesh $\T_\L$. The per-face indicator $\eta_{\L,\fineIdxone}^2$ on level $\L$ can be translated back to the subdivision partition $\C$ using Equation~\eqref{eq:project_estimator_on_L_to_partition}.
\end{example}

\noindent We now have estimators $\eta_{\l,\coarseIdxone}$ per face of the subdivision partition $\C$. However, the atomic units of refinement in our scheme are vertex stars because they give rise to clean analysis. To enable vertex-based refinement for the adaptive algorithm, we need to sum up the face estimators $\eta_{\l,\coarseIdxone}$ to vertex estimators. Recall from Section~\ref{subsec:selecting_active_dofs} the vertex equivalence classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ from Equation~\eqref{eq:def_vertex_class} and their covering stars $\coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo})$ from Equation~\eqref{eq:def_partition_star}, the active faces of $\C$ that touch the vertex. The vertex-based residual $\nu_\coarseIdxtwo$ our code uses is defined by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:vertex_residual}
    \nu^2_\coarseIdxtwo = \tfrac{1}{a_\coarseIdxtwo} \; \sum\limits_{\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \; \in \; \coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}) \setminus \F_\L} \eta^2_{\l,\coarseIdxone}, \qquad \mathrm{where} \qquad a_\coarseIdxtwo = \sum\limits_{\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \; \in \; \coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}) \setminus \F_\L} \mathrm{area}(\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone}).
\end{equation}
Dividing by the cumulative area of the faces rescales the integral face estimator to a density per vertex. This rescaling makes the marking roughly level- and valence-agnostic. Otherwise, the contribution of coarse faces would be weighted higher and their adjacent vertices would be over-represented during the Dörfler marking step. Note also that we do not take faces on level $\L$ into account since they cannot be refined further, so their error is partically fixed.

Because the covering star includes the coarse face across any interface edge through the vertex, $\nu_\coarseIdxtwo$ accounts for exactly the faces that refining the vertex's star would homogenise during the \textsc{Refine} step, see Section~\ref{subsec:refine}. For this reason, $\nu_\coarseIdxtwo$ measures exactly the error that selecting the vertex would actually resolve in the sense of Dörfler marking, see Section~\ref{subsec:mark}.

\subsubsection{Marking}
\label{subsec:mark}

Our code marks vertices rather than faces. This enables us to use the quadrisection operator $\quadriV{\C}{\targetlevel}$ to refine single vertex stars, the central combinatorial object of the admissibility arguments in~\cite{companion_paper}. We rank the vertices by the vertex estimator $\nu_\coarseIdxtwo$, see Equation~\eqref{eq:vertex_residual} from Section~\ref{subsec:estimate}. For this purpose, we enumerate the $\vert \mathrm{V}_\C \vert$ vertex equivalence classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ of $\C$ in order of decreasing residual, so that $\nu_{\coarseIdxtwo_1} \geq \nu_{\coarseIdxtwo_2} \geq \ldots \geq \nu_{\coarseIdxtwo_{\mathrm{V}-1}} \geq \nu_{\coarseIdxtwo_{\vert \mathrm{V}_\C \vert}}$.

The Dörfler marking algorithm builds the set $\mathrm{V}_\C^\mathrm{mark} \coloneq \{ \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_1}, \, \dots, \, \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_q} \}$ by greedily selecting the first $q$ vertex equivalence classes with the largest estimators such that the \emph{marked refinable residual}
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:marked_refinable_residual}
    \rho^2_{\R_\theta} \; \coloneqq \sum\limits_{\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \; \in \; \R_\theta} \eta^2_{\l, \coarseIdxone}, \qquad \text{where} \qquad \R_\theta = \bigcup\limits_{p=1}^q \coveringStar(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_p}) \setminus \F(\C_\L),
\end{equation}
captures at least a fraction $\theta$ of the refinable residual $\rho$ of Equation~\eqref{def:total_and_refinable_residuals}, i.e.
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:doerfler_selection}
    \rho_{\R_\theta} \; \geq \; \theta \, \rho
\end{equation}
where $\theta \in (0, 1]$ is the Dörfler parameter. The set $\R_\theta \subset \F(\C_{<\L})$ collects all the faces that are refined during the \textsc{Refine} step, see Section~\ref{subsec:refine}; faces on level $\L$ are excluded, so $\rho_{\R_\theta}$ is a restriction of the refinable residual $\rho$ rather than of the total residual $\eta$.

\subsubsection{Refinement}
\label{subsec:refine}

The \textsc{Refine} step refines the marked faces $\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \R_\theta$ of the covering stars of the vertex equivalence classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \mathrm{V}_\C^{\mathrm{mark}}$ from Equation~\eqref{eq:marked_refinable_residual}. Recall that the source level $\sourcelevel_\coarseIdxtwo$ and the target level $\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo$ measure the coarsest and finest levels at which a vertex of the vertex class $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ occurs in the covering $\C$, see Definition~\ref{def:vertex_classed}. Since we want to refine the faces around the vertex class by one level (unless $\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo = \L$), we compute the target level $\tilde{\targetlevel}_\coarseIdxtwo = \min(\targetlevel_{\coarseIdxtwo_p} \! + \! 1, \L)$ that is expected after refining around the vertex class $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$. The expected target levels $\tilde{\targetlevel}_\coarseIdxtwo$ must be computed on the fixed input subdivision partition $\C$ before any refinement takes place to avoid a cascade of ever increasing target levels. 

The \textsc{Refine} step homogenises the partition star of each marked vertex class $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \mathrm{V}_\C^{\mathrm{mark}}$ to one level beyond its target level $\targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo$. Formally, we can express that using the graded quadrisection $\quadriV{\C}{\tilde{\targetlevel}_\coarseIdxtwo}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo})$ from Equation~\eqref{eq:vertex_quadrisection}.

The result of the \textsc{Refine} step is the subdivision partition $\C^{\mathrm{refine}}$ given by
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=3pt, itemsep=-2pt]
    \item initialise the subdivision partition $\C^0 = \C$,
    \item refine covering stars iteratively for all $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_p} \in \mathrm{V}_\C^{\mathrm{mark}}$ with $p = 1, \, \dots, \, \vert \mathrm{V}_\C^{\textrm{mark}} \vert$ to obtain
    \begin{equation}
        \C^{p} = \quadriV{\C^{p-1}}{\tilde{\targetlevel}_{\coarseIdxtwo}}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}),
    \end{equation}
    \vspace{-7mm}
    \item set $\C^{\mathrm{refine}} \coloneq \C^{\vert \mathrm{V}_\C^{\textrm{mark}} \vert}$.
\end{enumerate}
Because the expected target levels $\tilde{\targetlevel}_{\coarseIdxtwo}$ are computed on the fixed input covering $\C$ and each quadrisection only ever homogenises a star upward to $\tilde{\targetlevel}_{\coarseIdxtwo}$, the order in which the $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \mathrm{V}_\C^{\mathrm{mark}}$ are processed does not affect the resulting partition $\C^p$. We may therefore apply them in any order, our code processes the coarsest vertex classed first for instance.

The \textsc{Refine} step in the previous section produces the covering $\C^{\mathrm{refine}}$. Crucially, $\C^{\mathrm{refine}}$ is not a subdivision partition because $\C^{\mathrm{refine}}$ might now contain pinch vertices or inadmissible faces. The \textsc{Pad} step repairs these defects and turns $\C^{\mathrm{refine}}$ in the subdivision partition $\C^{\mathrm{pad}}$. The goal is to keep the padded faces added during this process to a minimum to avoid inflating the number of DoFs in non-marked regions. The only exception is some grading that we apply around vertices whose covering star covers too many levels. This grading does not change the restored admissibility properties but improves the conditioning of the resulting matrix-vector system. This section covers all three kinds of padding one by one.

The \textsc{Pad} needs no machinery beyond the covering star $\coveringStar$ and the two quadrisection operators $\quadriF{\C}$ and $\quadriV{\C}{\targetlevel}$ of Section~\ref{subsec:subdiv_partition} since every padding substep is a quadrisection of either a covering star of a vertex class or of inadmissible faces.

\paragraph{Resolving vertex pinches.}

We iterate over all vertex classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ of the refined subdivision partition to build the set of pinch vertices
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{V}_{\C^{\mathrm{refine}}}^{\mathrm{pinch}} = \{ \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \mathrm{V}_{\C^{\mathrm{refine}}} \; \colon \; \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \text{\; is a pinch vertex of } \C^{\mathrm{refine}}\}
\end{equation}
according to Definition~\ref{def:pinch_vertex}. The subdivision partition $\C^{\mathrm{pinch}} \coloneq \C^{\vert \mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{pinch}} \vert}$ with all pinches resolved is obtained from the iteration
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:pinch_resolved_subdiv_partition}
    \C^p = \quadriV{\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_p}}{\targetlevel_{\coarseIdxtwo_p}}(\C^{p-1}) \quad \text{for} \quad \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_p} \in \mathrm{V}_{\C^{\mathrm{refine}}}^{\mathrm{pinch}} \quad \text{with} \quad \; p \in \{1, \, \dots, \, \vert \mathrm{V}_{\C^{\mathrm{refine}}}^{\mathrm{pinch}} \vert \}, 
\end{equation}
and $\C^0 = \C^\mathrm{refine}$ the subdivision partition after the \textsc{Refine} step and $\targetlevel_{\coarseIdxtwo_p}$ the target level of $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_p}$ in $\C^p$. In words, for each pinched vertex $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \mathrm{V}_\C$, we homogenise its covering star to its target level, lifting the coarse arcs to $\targetlevel_{\coarseIdxtwo_p}$ and merging them into a single disc. 

Every pinch vertex carries a cohomology defect. The severeness of the defect depends on the concrete configuration of the pinch, see Definition~\ref{def:pinch_vertex} and Figure~\ref{fig:pinches}. Section~\ref{subsec:exactness_atomic} later confirms that resolving the coarse region around a pinch vertex successfully repairs these defects.

\paragraph{Resolving inadmissible faces.}

Similarly to resolving pinches, we iterate over all face $\face{\l}{\coarseIdxone}$ of the subdivision partition $\C^{\mathrm{pinch}}$ to build the set of inadmissible faces
\begin{equation}
    \F_{\mathrm{inadmissible}} = \big\{ \face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \in \F(\C^{\mathrm{pinch}}) \; \colon \; \face{\l}{\coarseIdxone} \text{\; is not admissible} \big\}
\end{equation}
according to Definition~\ref{def:admissible_face}. The subdivision partition $\C^{\mathrm{admissible}}$ where all faces are admissible is given by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:inadmissible_faces_resolved_subdiv_partition}
    \C^{\mathrm{admissible}} = \quadriF{\F_{\mathrm{inadmissible}}} (\C^{\mathrm{pinch}}).
\end{equation}
In words, we refine every inadmissible face by one level because the refined faces are guaranteed to have an admissible vertex on level $\l+1$.

\paragraph{Grading covering stars.}

The grading is supposed to smooth out transitions across vertex classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \mathrm{V}_{\C^{\mathrm{admissible}}}$ with large level differences. Unlike the first two rules, the grading step does not restore exactness or linear independence but improves the conditioning of the matrix-vector system and helps to avoid numerical artifacts in these regions. The predicate to identify the vertex classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo}$ to grade is the \emph{level gap} $\mu_\coarseIdxtwo$ defined by
\begin{equation}
    \label{def:level_gap}
    \mu_\coarseIdxtwo \coloneq \targetlevel_\coarseIdxtwo - \sourcelevel_\coarseIdxtwo.
\end{equation}
The grading code iterates over the vertex classes $\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \in \mathrm{V}_{\C^{\mathrm{admissible}}}$ and assembles the set
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{V}_{\C^{\mathrm{admissible}}}^{\mathrm{grading}} \coloneq \big\{ \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo} \; \colon \; \mu_\coarseIdxtwo > 2 \big\}
\end{equation}
The cut-off value of $2$ works well in our simulations of the vector Laplace equation in Section~\ref{subsec:vector_laplace} but can generally be chosen arbitrarily.

The vertex classes marked for grading are refined using the quadrisection operator to obtain the graded subdivision partition $\C^{\mathrm{grading}} \coloneq \C^{\vert \mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{admissible}} \vert}$ as the result of the iterative construction
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:grading_subdiv_partition}
    \C^p = \quadriV{\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_q}}{\targetlevel_{\coarseIdxtwo_p} - 2}(\C^{p-1}) \quad \text{for} \quad \vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_p} \in \mathrm{V}_{\C^{\mathrm{admissible}}}^{\mathrm{grading}} \quad \text{with} \quad \; p \in \{1, \, \dots, \, \vert \mathrm{V}_{\C^{\mathrm{admissible}}}^{\mathrm{grading}} \vert \}
\end{equation}
and $\C^0 = \C^{\mathrm{admissible}}$. Note the additional $-2$ in the quadrisection operator $\quadriV{\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_p}}{\targetlevel_{\coarseIdxtwo_p} - 2}$. It ensures that the covering star $\coveringStarC{\C^p}(\vertclass{\coarseIdxtwo_p})$ on the graded partition $\C^p$ has a level gap of $2$ or less.

\paragraph{Summary.}

Every stage of the \textsc{Pad} step uses either the vertex-class based quadrisection operator $\quadriV{\C}{\targetlevel}$ or the face-based $\quadriF{\C}$.

The pinch padding resolves pinch vertices and thus repairs cohomological artifacts and spurious harmonics, while the admissibility padding mostly restores linear independece of the active basis funtions $\activeDoFSet{k}{\C}$. The grading only refines around vertex classes with large level jumps to improve the conditioning of the resulting linear system.

Every rule only ever refines and adds a few faces that locally repair its violation, and no face is refined beyond level $\L$. The order of the padding stages matters. In particular, resolving a pinch vertex can lead to an admissible face becoming inadmissible, cf. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline_padding}.

We do not \robert{(yet?)} have a proof that $\textsc{Pad}$ terminates. For our implementation, we wrapped the pinch resolution, face admissibility padding and the grading in a loop that terminates once a pass no longer refines any face, i.e., none of the predicates fires. A counter records the productive passes, which has not exceeded the minimum of $1$ in any test case we ran.

\subsection{Exactness and padding on atomic partitions}
\label{subsec:exactness_atomic}

\guidance{This subsection is the verification promised at the end of the \textsc{Pad} discussion: it establishes that pinch resolution and inadmissible-faces padding \emph{reliably} restore the correct cohomology across the admissibility boundary, not just on the single constructed input of the (dissolved) worked example.}

\robert{Before applying the adaptive loop to a PDE, we validate the admissibility
construction directly on a suite of atomic test cases and the two failure modes it
is designed to prevent. Each case is a small concentric-ring mesh around a single
interior vertex of chosen valence --- a topological \emph{disk}, so that
\emph{every} discrete harmonic that appears is spurious. For each partition we
compute the de Rham cohomology of the active space (from the ranks of the discrete
$\extd$) before and after $\textsc{Pad}$; Table~\ref{tab:atomic-cohomology}
collects the results under both natural and vanishing-trace (Dirichlet) boundary
conditions.}

\robert{A non-admissible partition can fail in two qualitatively different ways.
In a \emph{hard failure} the kernel/image of $\extd$ on the active space genuinely
changes, producing a spurious harmonic --- a discrete $k$-form that is closed but
not exact with no topological counterpart --- which corrupts the Hodge
decomposition and, for Maxwell, injects the spurious zero modes of
Section~\ref{subsec:worked_example}. In a \emph{soft failure} the active basis is
merely linearly \emph{dependent}: its column space is still an exact subcomplex, so
the cohomology is correct, but the naive DoF count overstates the space dimension
and the mass matrix is singular. In Table~\ref{tab:atomic-cohomology} a hard
failure shows up as a nonzero harmonic $\dim\mathcal{H}^1$ on a disk, while a soft
failure shows up as a rank deficit $n_k - \dim V^k > 0$; both distort the Euler
characteristic $\chi$ away from the disk value $\chi = 1$, and both are removed by
padding.}

\robert{The controlled examples delineate the admissibility boundary precisely:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \robert{\textbf{A coarse loop injects a spurious $\mathcal{H}^1$.} A
    one-band-wide coarse annulus (the ``$3$-ring, inner$+$outer refined'' row) is
    \emph{both} a hard failure ($\dim\mathcal{H}^1 = 1$) and a soft failure
    ($n_1 = 360$, $\operatorname{rank} = 342$, deficit $18$): the single richest
    case. Padding refines the middle band and restores an exact, independent basis.}
    \item \robert{\textbf{Break the loop and the defect vanishes.} Replacing the
    closed annulus by an open arc (``horseshoe'') makes the coarse region a disk
    again: no spurious harmonic, confirming that the defect is topological (the
    closed loop), not a mesh-size artifact.}
    \item \robert{\textbf{It is specifically a one-band effect.} A
    \emph{two}-band-wide coarse annulus (the ``$4$-ring, middle refined'' negative
    control) is already admissible --- padding is a no-op --- so the defect is a
    one-band-strip phenomenon, not a property of any coarse annulus.}
    \item \robert{\textbf{Boundary notches are a Dirichlet-only, additive defect.}
    Refining the stars of boundary ``corner'' vertices leaves coarse notches; under
    natural BCs these are harmless slits, but under Dirichlet each is a relative
    loop, and independent notches \emph{add} (six symmetric notches give exactly six
    Dirichlet harmonics).}
\end{itemize}
\robert{The two-level \emph{pinch} and its \emph{doubling law}
($\dim\mathcal{H}^1 = 2$, $\chi = -1$ before padding, both restored after) are the
``Valence-$8$ pinch, level~2'' row of Table~\ref{tab:atomic-cohomology}, already
illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:pinch_examples}. Further atomic cases are collected in
Appendix~\ref{app:figure_gallery}.}

\subsection{Maxwell Eigenvalue problem}
\label{subsec:maxwell}

\guidance{Thesis of this subsection, stated up front so it does not read as a Maxwell-flavoured repeat of Section~\ref{subsec:exactness_atomic}: Section~\ref{subsec:exactness_atomic} established that padding restores the correct cohomology; this section shows the practical consequence for an actual eigensolver. Because a spurious harmonic is closed, its Rayleigh quotient is exactly zero, so the admissibility defect lives entirely at $\lambda = 0$ and leaves the physical band uncontaminated --- in sharp contrast to the interleaved pollution of non-compatible elements. That confinement-to-the-kernel is the new point here, not implied by the cohomology counts of Section~\ref{subsec:exactness_atomic}.}

\robert{The Maxwell cavity (curl--curl) eigenvalue problem is a classical test case for
structure-preserving discretisations. On the domain $\M = [0,\pi]^2$ its weak form
is: find $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ and $u \in \HocurlM{\M}$, $u \neq 0$, such that
\begin{equation}
  (\extd u, \extd v)_\M = \lambda (u, v)_\M \qquad \forall v \in \HocurlM{\M},
\end{equation}
with analytic spectrum $\lambda = i^2 + j^2$, $(i,j) \in \mathbb{Z}^2_{\geq 0}\setminus\{0\}$,
i.e.\ $1, 1, 2, 4, 4, 5, \ldots$. We impose homogeneous Dirichlet (vanishing-trace)
boundary conditions, so the relevant invariant is the \emph{relative} cohomology
(mod $\partial\M$). Since the square is contractible, its relative first Betti
number is zero, and \textbf{every harmonic $1$-form appearing in the
discretisation is therefore spurious}. Applying Loop subdivision alters the domain
slightly, so we do not compare the discrete spectrum directly with the analytic
$i^2+j^2$; instead we compare three subdivision partitions that use the same
approximation of $\M$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{inexact} --- a mixed-level partition whose interface violates admissibility (Fig.~\ref{fig:Maxwell_inexact_DoFmesh});
    \item \textbf{exact (padded)} --- the \emph{same} partition after $\textsc{Pad}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:Maxwell_exact_DoFmesh});
    \item \textbf{FEEC (uniform)} --- the finest FEEC space $\FEECspaceZero{1}{\L}$, refining the whole domain to level $\L$, which is the verifiably spurious-mode-free reference~\cite{Arnold.FEEC}.
\end{itemize}}

\robert{Table~\ref{tab:maxwell_spectrum} and Figure~\ref{fig:Maxwell_eigenvalues} report the
result. The physical ($\lambda > 0$) band is \emph{identical} across the three
partitions (to plotted precision) and tracks the analytic values; the only defect
of the inexact partition is $\dim\mathcal{H}^1 = 2$ spurious harmonics, which
padding removes ($\dim\mathcal{H}^1 \to 0$). The reason the pollution is confined
to $\lambda = 0$ follows from the exact-sequence structure. A non-admissible
interface makes the active space fail to be a subcomplex, and the concrete symptom
is a discrete $1$-form that is \emph{closed but not exact} --- a spurious harmonic.
Being closed, $\extd u_h = 0$, so its Rayleigh quotient
$(\extd u_h, \extd u_h)/(u_h, u_h) = 0$: it can appear \emph{only} at $\lambda = 0$
and never in the physical band. This contrasts sharply with classical spectral
pollution for non-compatible elements, where spurious eigenvalues interleave with
the physical band; here the admissibility failure is confined to the kernel of
$\extd$, so the physical spectrum stays clean.}

\robert{The defect must be read off the rank-based $\dim\mathcal{H}^1$ (from the ranks of
the discrete $\extd$), \emph{not} the raw count of near-zero eigenvalues. The
physical kernel $\extd(\hierSDFspace{0}{\C}{\L})$ of gradients is large and
mesh-dependent, and padding changes the gradient DoF count, so the raw zero-count
is not comparable between partitions: here it is $125$ (inexact) versus $90$
(exact), which taken alone would misleadingly suggest padding \emph{removed} modes,
even as the cohomologically meaningful $\dim\mathcal{H}^1$ falls from $2$ to $0$.}

\subsection{Vector Laplace problem}
\label{subsec:vector_laplace}

The $1$-form Laplace problem is given by
\begin{equation}
  (\extd \boldsymbol{\delta} + \boldsymbol{\delta} \extd) u = f
\end{equation}
on a domain $\M$. Since the operator $\boldsymbol{\delta}$ is not well-defined for a (subdivision) $1$-form, we introduce the auxiliary variable $\boldsymbol{\delta} \sigma = u$ and use the FEEC-typical mixed weak formulation
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:mixed_laplacian1}
    (\sigma, \tau)_\M - (u, \extd \tau)_\M &= 0 \qquad \forall \tau \in H^1(\M), \\
    \label{eq:mixed_laplacian2}
    (\extd \sigma, v)_\M + (\extd u, \extd v)_\M &= (f, v)_\M \qquad \forall v \in \HocurlM{\M}.
\end{align}

This section studies the \emph{circular layer} test case from \cite{Cabanas.2026} as an $L$-sweep that exercises the full \textsc{Solve}--\textsc{Estimate}--\textsc{Mark}--\textsc{Refine}--\textsc{Pad} loop. Its analytical solution is given by
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:circular_layer_test_case}
    \Vec{u}_{\mathrm{CL}}(x) = \tanh \big(\alpha (\vvert{\vvert{x}}^2 - R^2) \big) \; \Vec{b}(x) \quad \text{with} \quad \Vec{b}(x) = \begin{bmatrix}
        \sin(\pi x_1) \\
        \sin(\pi x_2)
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
and $\alpha = 100$ and $R = 0.3$. The vector part $\Vec{b}(x)$ provides a background field while the $\tanh$ term adds a sharp, radially symmetric transition between $+\Vec{b}$ and $-\Vec{b}$ localised on the ring $\vvert{\vvert{x}} = R$. The right-hand side $f_{\mathrm{CL}}$ corresponding to $\Vec{u}_{\mathrm{CL}}$ can be computed using the (strong) vector Laplace equation. The exact solution is thus smooth almost everywhere and steep only on a thin annulus --- the canonical setting for adaptivity. We impose \emph{natural} boundary conditions (no trace on $u$), in contrast to the Dirichlet Maxwell case of Section~\ref{subsec:maxwell}.

\robert{Because $\Vec{u}_{\mathrm{CL}}$ is known in closed form, every solve reports two quantities: the computable residual estimator $\eta$ of Section~\ref{subsec:estimate}, which \emph{drives marking}; and the true error $\Ltwoerror \coloneqq \vvert{u_h - \Vec{u}_{\mathrm{CL}}}_{L^2(\M)}$, computed against $\Vec{u}_{\mathrm{CL}}$ interpolated into a degree-$3$ vector-CG space on the finest mesh, which \emph{measures accuracy} and is never used for marking. Marking by an estimator and measuring by the truth is what makes the error-versus-DoF comparison below meaningful.}

\paragraph{Experimental design.} \robert{For each maximum refinement depth $L \in \{3, 4, 5\}$ we run the adaptive loop (per-vertex Dörfler marking with $\theta = 0.9$, followed by \textsc{Pad}) to estimator saturation, and, as a reference, a single \emph{uniform} solve at level $L$. The saturation criterion is discussed below; it stops the L=3 run at step $4$, L=4 at step $6$, and L=5 at step $8$. Table~\ref{tab:cl_headline} collects the headline numbers at the final, saturated adaptive step. The adaptive scheme reaches the accuracy of the uniform level-$L$ solve at a small fraction of its $1$-form DoFs $n_1$, and the saving \emph{grows with depth} --- $3.3\times$, $4.6\times$, $5.9\times$ at $L = 3, 4, 5$ --- because the layer needs fine resolution only locally, so the deeper the refinement allowed, the more uniform refinement wastes on the smooth bulk. Figure~\ref{fig:cl_solution} shows the mechanism directly: the partition concentrates refinement on the circular layer while leaving the interior and exterior coarse.}

\paragraph{The DoF saving grows with $L$.} \robert{Figure~\ref{fig:cl_convergence} (left) plots the true $L^2$ error against $n_1$ on log--log axes. The three adaptive runs sit on a single convergence envelope, while each uniform reference (hollow square, with a dotted floor at its error) lies far to the right at the same accuracy: the horizontal gap \emph{is} the DoF saving, $3.3\times \to 4.6\times \to 5.9\times$, growing with $L$. We compare in the true $L^2$ error rather than in $\eta$ because $\eta$ is not comparable across $L$ (it carries the finest cell size $h_\L = h_0/2^L$ in its scaling, see below), whereas the $L^2$ error is a genuine norm with no finest-mesh normalisation --- it is identical at step $0$ for every $L$, as it must be.}

\robert{The adaptive $L^2$ error floors slightly \emph{above} the uniform value at the same $L$, and this ``$L^2$ gap'' widens with $L$ (Table~\ref{tab:cl_headline}, last column). This is not a solver defect but the exact signature of marking in the energy norm while measuring in $L^2$, and it decomposes cleanly. Split the domain into the \emph{ring} annulus, where the $\tanh$ layer lives, and the smooth \emph{bulk}. Their supports are disjoint, so the squared $L^2$ error adds in quadrature, $\vvert{u_h - u}^2 = \int_{\mathrm{ring}} \vvert{e}^2 + \int_{\mathrm{bulk}} \vvert{e}^2 = R^2 + B^2$. Both schemes drive the ring to level $L$, so they share the same ring error $R$; uniform also resolves the bulk ($B \approx 0$), so $\vvert{u_h - u}_{\mathrm{unif}} \approx R$, while adaptive leaves the bulk coarse, so $\vvert{u_h - u}_{\mathrm{adap}} = \sqrt{R^2 + B^2}$. Backing the bulk term $B = \sqrt{\text{adaptive}^2 - \text{uniform}^2}$ out of the measured errors (Table~\ref{tab:cl_decomp}) shows that $B$ is essentially \emph{constant} at $\approx 3.5\cdot10^{-4}$ across $L$ --- energy-based marking never sees enough error \emph{density} in the smooth bulk to refine it, so the bulk floor is $L$-independent --- while the uniform (ring) error $R$ halves per level. The gap is therefore $\sqrt{R^2 + B^2}/R = \sqrt{1 + (B/R)^2}$ with $B$ fixed and $R$ shrinking, so it must \emph{diverge} with $L$; the value $2.03$ at $L = 5$ is merely $R$ passing through $\approx 0.57\,B$. A goal-oriented estimator targeting the $L^2$ functional would refine the bulk and close the gap; the energy-optimal estimator used here does not, so this is a \emph{DoF/memory} claim at matched \emph{estimator} accuracy, with the $L^2$ gap explained rather than hidden.}

\paragraph{Estimator saturation and a stopping criterion.} \robert{Figure~\ref{fig:cl_convergence} (right) plots, for each $L$, the total estimator $\eta$ against its refineable part --- the contribution from faces not yet at the finest level $L$. As the sweep exhausts the depth-$L$ ceiling, the refineable part collapses below the total: the remaining error sits at level $L$ and cannot be refined further. We stop the loop on the dimensionless \emph{refineable energy fraction}
\begin{equation}
    \rho = \frac{\text{refineableEtaSq}}{\text{totalEtaSq}}, \qquad \text{stop when } \rho < \tau,\ \tau = 0.05,
\end{equation}
where the numerator is the estimated error energy on faces below level $L$ --- precisely the energy Dörfler can still act on. This is the right metric for three reasons: it is exactly the quantity the plot tracks; it is computable (no exact solution needed, unlike the $L^2$ error); and it is dimensionless, so the same $\tau$ works across $L$ --- unlike the absolute $\eta$, whose $L$-dependent floor makes no single tolerance portable. The $L = 3$ run illustrates it: $\rho$ holds at $1.0$ for steps $0$--$2$ (nothing at level $L$ yet), then drops $0.300 \to 0.026$ and stops at step $4$.}

\robert{But $\tau = 0.05$ is deliberately conservative. Once the refineable part has decoupled from the total ($\rho$ first falls below $1$), the true $L^2$ error is already essentially converged; the remaining steps buy almost nothing. Reading the stopping step off the same trajectories for a range of tolerances (Table~\ref{tab:cl_tol}, all computable from one run --- no re-solve), the $L^2$ error at $\tau = 0.3$ is within $1.5\%$ of the fully-saturated value for every $L$. The strict tail hurts most exactly where it is most expensive: for $L = 5$, tightening $\tau$ from $0.3$ to $0.05$ adds $+10\,308$ DoFs ($+56\%$) and $\approx 6.0$ hours of solve time for a $1.2\%$ $L^2$ gain, because the marginal faces it refines carry vanishing error. A tolerance $\tau \approx 0.2\text{--}0.3$ --- stopping once the refineable part has clearly decoupled rather than nearly vanished --- is the better operating point, sharpening the DoF saving to $3.7\times / 5.4\times / 9.3\times$. The absolute vertical offset between the $L$ curves in Figure~\ref{fig:cl_convergence} (right) is largely normalisation ($\eta \sim h_\L$), not accuracy, so each panel is read \emph{within} its $L$; cross-$L$ accuracy is the job of Figure~\ref{fig:cl_convergence} (left).}

\paragraph{Solve cost does not follow the DoF saving.} \robert{The one honest cost is the linear solver. Figure~\ref{fig:cl_minres} reports MINRES iterations (bars) and pure solve time (twin axis) over the sweep. Wall-clock \emph{grows} over the sweep and is dramatically worse than uniform despite far fewer DoFs: adaptive $L = 5$ takes $\approx 3.4$ hours and $654$ iterations at $28\,588$ DoFs, while uniform $L = 5$ takes $46$ seconds and $21$ iterations at $169\,472$ DoFs --- a $5.9\times$ DoF saving turned into a $270\times$ slowdown. The measured time is the MINRES \texttt{solve} call only (not dofset construction, assembly, or the preconditioner factorisation), so it is genuine solve cost, from two compounding causes: the block-diagonal preconditioner degrades on irregular adaptive meshes, and each iteration gets more expensive because the Galerkin-projected operators \emph{densify} --- the subdivision basis functions are globally supported, so the projection fills far more nonzeros per row than a nodal matrix of the same DoF count, whereas the uniform operators stay sparse and well-conditioned ($\approx 20$ iterations). The adaptivity win here is thus in DoFs and memory, not wall-clock, with the current solver; the preconditioner is the bottleneck and a subdivision-compatible multigrid variant is left for future work (which reinforces the case for the looser $\tau$ above).}

\robert{In summary, on a manufactured problem with a sharp circular layer and a known exact solution, adaptive subdivision refinement reaches the accuracy of a uniform level-$L$ solve at a small fraction of its DoFs, and the saving grows with depth ($3.3\times, 4.6\times, 5.9\times$ to saturation; $3.7\times, 5.4\times, 9.3\times$ at a looser stop). The refineable/total estimator split identifies when a depth $L$ is exhausted and yields a portable, computable stopping criterion; the admissibility padding costs only $O(\text{interface})$ extra faces per step; and the conformity interface DoFs are a modest, bounded overhead. The one honest cost is the solver: because the projected operators densify under the globally supported basis and the block preconditioner weakens on irregular meshes, the DoF saving is a memory saving rather than, yet, a wall-clock one.}

\section{Conclusion}

\robert{We have presented a complete matrix-level framework for hierarchical subdivision $k$-form spaces and their use in an adaptive loop for structure-preserving PDE discretisations. The central observation is that any active subdivision basis function is a column of an accumulated subdivision matrix $\Smat{k}{\l}{\L}$; selecting columns according to a subdivision partition produces a hierarchical space $\hierSDFspace{k}{\C}{\L}$ whose linear independence and de Rham exactness are guaranteed by the partition condition proved in the companion paper~\cite{companion_paper}. Once the hierarchical subdivision matrix $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ is assembled, all bilinear forms are obtained by Galerkin restriction of the embedding-level operators without recomputing any integrals, and the active-space linear system reduces to standard sparse linear algebra on the finest mesh $\T_\L$.

The adaptive loop adds a subdivision-specific \textsc{Pad} step to the standard \textsc{Solve}--\textsc{Estimate}--\textsc{Mark}--\textsc{Refine} cycle. Padding alternates pinch resolution with inadmissible-faces padding and grading to a fixed point: pinch resolution repairs a pinched coarse region that would otherwise carry a spurious harmonic (a closed but non-exact discrete $1$-form, breaking de Rham exactness), while inadmissible-faces padding and grading enforce the interior-vertex (Good-vertex) requirement and bound the level jump at coarse/fine interfaces. Both rules only ever refine, are local, and are inexpensive relative to the linear solve.

The numerical experiments confirm the two key structural properties of the framework. For the Maxwell eigenvalue problem, exact subdivision partitions reproduce the reference FEEC spectrum without spurious modes; inexact ones introduce them. This serves as a direct diagnostic for the correctness of the active-DoF construction and the de Rham complex structure. For the mixed vector Laplace equation, the adaptive loop concentrates refinement at the location of steep gradients and achieves convergence behaviour consistent with the hierarchical approximation theory.

Several directions remain open. The precise correspondence between the subdivision PAD step and the $L$-chain completion used in hierarchical B-spline AFEM~\cite{Cabanas.2026} has not been established; clarifying this connection would relate the two frameworks and potentially enable convergence proofs to be transferred. On the solver side, the block-diagonal LDLT preconditioner used here is effective for the problem sizes considered, but a multigrid preconditioner that respects the globally supported subdivision basis would reduce the per-step cost for larger problems; this requires a prolongation operator compatible with $\Amat{k}{\activeDoFs}{\L}$ and is left for future work. Finally, a promising direction is to adopt the overlapping patch framework of Bressan~\cite{Bressan.2024} in place of the non-overlapping subdivision partition used here. Overlapping patches can provide a richer set of admissible active-DoF configurations and may admit a more flexible PAD step, at the cost of requiring a partition-of-unity treatment to maintain linear independence; adapting the matrix-level assembly and the de Rham complex to that setting is an open problem.}

\section{Active-DoF assembly pseudo-code}
\label{app:active_dof_pseudocode}

\robert{This appendix gives the full active-DoF assembly routine
$\textsc{BuildActiveDoFs}$ whose role in Section~\ref{sec:construction_of_hierarchical_subdiv_spaces}
is described around Lemma~\ref{lemma:active_k_form_dofs_per_level}.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:buildactivedofs} produces the interface DoFs by a frontier sweep
(steps~(a)--(d)) that maintains the
set of interior edges separating resolved (coarse-side) faces from
unresolved (finer) regions. Each level the frontier is split into child
edges and interface vertices, the vertices emit the DoFs they introduce on
the following level. The ordering of operations within each level is fixed:
old frontier edges are consumed first, new ones are spawned, DoFs are
emitted from the previous level's vertices, and finally the frontier is
split for the next level.}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\color{orange}
\caption{$\textsc{BuildActiveDoFs}(\C)$: active degrees of freedom from a subdivision partition}
\label{alg:buildactivedofs}
\BlankLine
\tcp{1.\ Native DoFs}
$\D^2 \leftarrow \C$\;
\For{$\l = 0, \, \ldots, \, \L$}{
    $\D^0[\l] \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    $\D^1[\l] \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    \For{each $f \in \C[\l]$}{
        $\D^0[\l] \leftarrow \D^0[\l] \cup \mathrm{verts}(f)$\;
        $\D^1[\l] \leftarrow \D^1[\l] \cup \mathrm{edges}(f)$\;
    }
}
\BlankLine
\tcp{2.\ Interface DoFs (frontier sweep)}
$\mathrm{frontier} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
$\mathrm{pendingVerts} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
\For{$\l = 0, \, \ldots, \, \L$}{
    \tcp{(a) consume frontier edges whose fine side is now resolved at level $\l$}
    $\mathrm{frontier} \leftarrow \{ e \in \mathrm{frontier} : \text{fine-side faces of } e \notin P[\l] \}$\;
    \tcp{(b) spawn new frontier edges: interior edges of $\mathcal{F}_\l$ with exactly one adjacent face in $P[\l]$}
    $\mathrm{frontier} \leftarrow \mathrm{frontier} \cup \textsc{FindInterfaceEdges}(\l, \D)$\;
    \tcp{(c) emit DoFs from interface vertices spawned at level $\l - 1$}
    \For{each $v \in \mathrm{pendingVerts}$}{
        $\D^0[\l] \leftarrow \D^0[\l] \cup \{v\}$\;
        $\D^1[\l] \leftarrow \D^1[\l] \cup \mathrm{activeEdges}(v)$\;
        $\D^2[\l] \leftarrow \D^2[\l] \cup \mathrm{activeFaces}(v)$\;
    }
    $\mathrm{pendingVerts} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
    \tcp{(d) split frontier edges into child edges and interface vertices for level $\l + 1$}
    \If{$\l < \L$}{
        $\mathrm{newFrontier} \leftarrow \emptyset$;\quad $\mathrm{newVerts} \leftarrow \emptyset$\;
        \For{each $e \in \mathrm{frontier}$}{
            $(e_1, e_2, v_{\mathrm{mid}}) \leftarrow \textsc{SplitEdge}(e)$ at level $\l + 1$\;
            $\mathrm{newFrontier} \leftarrow \mathrm{newFrontier} \cup \{e_1, e_2\}$;\quad
            $\mathrm{newVerts} \leftarrow \mathrm{newVerts} \cup \{v_{\mathrm{mid}}\}$\;
        }
        \For{each endpoint $v$ of edges in $\mathrm{frontier}$, taken once per $v$}{
            \lIf{$v$ borders a refined region}{$\mathrm{newVerts} \leftarrow \mathrm{newVerts} \cup \{v\}$}
        }
        $\mathrm{frontier} \leftarrow \mathrm{newFrontier}$;\quad
        $\mathrm{pendingVerts} \leftarrow \mathrm{newVerts}$\;
    }
}
\Return $\D$\;
\end{algorithm}
